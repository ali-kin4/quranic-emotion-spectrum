# Classical Arabic Sources — Annotated Edition Notes

This file documents the classical Arabic lexica and tafsir works cited in the
manuscripts, with edition information and notes on relevance to specific roots
in the negative-emotion spectrum.

## Lexica (Etymology)

### Ibn Fāris al-Rāzī, Aḥmad (d. 395 AH / 1004 CE)
**Muʿjam Maqāyīs al-Lughah**
- Edition cited: ʿAbd al-Salām Muḥammad Hārūn (ed.), Beirut: Dār al-Fikr, 1399
  AH / 1979 CE; 6 vols.
- Foundational Arabic root-based etymological lexicon. Ibn Fāris organizes the
  language by its triliteral roots and assigns each a single core meaning
  (*aṣl wāḥid*) from which all derivatives flow. This methodology is a direct
  ally for the scalar-semantic argument of the present paper: Ibn Fāris already
  treats each root as anchored to a unifying meaning, with derived forms
  representing graded extensions.
- Relevant entries: ض-ي-ق (constriction), ح-ز-ن (rough/unyielding ground →
  grief), أ-س-ف (intense grief shading into anger), س-خ-ط (displeasure),
  غ-ض-ب (active anger), غ-ي-ظ (compressed/boiling anger), م-ي-ز
  (separation/severance), ب-غ-ي (transgressive seeking), ط-غ-ي (overflow,
  rebellion), ع-ت-و (obstinate harshness).

### Ibn Manẓūr al-Ifrīqī (d. 711 AH / 1311 CE)
**Lisān al-ʿArab**
- Edition cited: 3rd printing, Beirut: Dār Ṣādir, 1414 AH / 1993 CE; 15 vols.
- The largest classical Arabic lexicon. Documents pre-Islamic and early Islamic
  semantic ranges of every root, with abundant poetic and prosaic shawāhid.
- Particularly valuable for the *ḥuzn* analysis: Ibn Manẓūr preserves the
  pre-Islamic geological sense of the root (rugged, uneven terrain) that
  underwrites the metaphor "grief is rough ground."

### al-Rāghib al-Iṣfahānī (d. ca. 502 AH / 1108 CE)
**al-Mufradāt fī Gharīb al-Qurʾān**
- Edition cited: Ṣafwān ʿAdnān Dāwūdī (ed.), Damascus and Beirut: Dār al-Qalam
  & al-Dār al-Shāmiyyah, 1412 AH / 1992 CE.
- The single most important pre-modern lexicon of *Qurʾānic* vocabulary
  specifically. Al-Rāghib's treatment is uniquely precise on the contextual
  semantics of each lexeme as it occurs in revelation, distinguishing nuances
  that broader lexica blur.
- Crucial for: the distinction between *ghaḍab* and *ghayẓ* (al-Rāghib treats
  *ghayẓ* as the "containedness" of anger, prefiguring the modern container
  metaphor); the distinction between *baghy* and *ʿudwān*.

### al-Muṣṭafawī, Ḥasan (1925–2005)
**al-Taḥqīq fī Kalimāt al-Qurʾān al-Karīm**
- Edition cited: Tehran: Wizārat al-Thaqāfah wa-l-Irshād al-Islāmī, 1416 AH
  and reprints; 14 vols.
- Twentieth-century root-based Qurʾānic lexicon. Mostafavi's working
  methodology is the explicit identification of the *aṣl wāḥid* of each
  root and the tracing of every Qurʾānic derivative to it — an approach
  uniquely well-suited to scalar-semantic analysis.
- Used as the primary modern philological reference in the methodology
  section of the paper.

### al-Fīrūzābādī (d. 817 AH / 1415 CE)
**al-Qāmūs al-Muḥīṭ**
- Edition cited: Beirut: Muʾassasat al-Risālah.
- Concise classical lexicon, often used to verify quick semantic claims; less
  rich than *Lisān al-ʿArab* but more compact.

### al-Zabīdī, Muḥammad Murtaḍā (d. 1205 AH / 1791 CE)
**Tāj al-ʿArūs min Jawāhir al-Qāmūs**
- Edition cited: Kuwait: al-Turāth al-ʿArabī; 40 vols.
- Largest classical Arabic lexicon ever compiled. Used for adjudicating fine
  semantic distinctions among near-synonyms.

## Tafsīr Works

### al-Ṭabāṭabāʾī, Muḥammad Ḥusayn (d. 1402 AH / 1981 CE)
**al-Mīzān fī Tafsīr al-Qurʾān**
- Edition cited: Beirut: Muʾassasat al-Aʿlamī li-l-Maṭbūʿāt; 20 vols.
- Major 20th-century Shīʿī exegesis. Particularly rich on the lexical and
  semantic analysis of divine *ghaḍab*/*sakhaṭ* (vol. 18, on Sūrat al-Fatḥ).
- Persian translation: Muḥammad Bāqir Mūsavī Hamadānī, Qom: Daftar-i
  Intishārāt-i Islāmī.

### al-Zamakhsharī (d. 538 AH / 1144 CE)
**al-Kashshāf ʿan Ḥaqāʾiq Ghawāmiḍ al-Tanzīl**
- Edition cited: Beirut: Dār al-Kitāb al-ʿArabī, 1407 AH; 4 vols.
- Classical Muʿtazilī exegesis. Indispensable for rhetorical-semantic readings
  of emotion verses, especially the metaphorical force of *takādu tamayyazu*
  (Q. 67:8).

### al-Ṭabarsī, Faḍl b. al-Ḥasan (d. ca. 548 AH / 1153 CE)
**Majmaʿ al-Bayān fī Tafsīr al-Qurʾān**
- Edition cited: Beirut: Dār al-Maʿrifah / Dār Iḥyāʾ al-Turāth al-ʿArabī;
  10 vols.
- Standard medieval Imāmī exegesis with strong lexical-grammatical apparatus.

### al-Fakhr al-Rāzī (d. 606 AH / 1209 CE)
**Mafātīḥ al-Ghayb (al-Tafsīr al-Kabīr)**
- Edition cited: Beirut: Dār Iḥyāʾ al-Turāth al-ʿArabī, 1420 AH; 32 vols.
- Classical exegesis with the strongest semantic-philosophical apparatus of
  any pre-modern tafsīr. Particularly useful on Q. 96:6–7 (cognitive analysis
  of *ṭughyān*).

### Ibn ʿĀshūr, Muḥammad al-Ṭāhir (d. 1393 AH / 1973 CE)
**al-Taḥrīr wa-l-Tanwīr**
- Edition cited: Tunis: al-Dār al-Tūnisiyyah li-l-Nashr, 1984; 30 vols.
- Twentieth-century semantic-rhetorical commentary. Particularly nuanced on
  the lexical analysis of *baghy*, *ṭughyān*, and *ʿutuww* in the rebellion
  cluster.

## Notes on Citation

- For Hijri-only dates, both AH and CE are given. For Persian sources, the
  Solar Hijri (SH) year is given with a parenthetical CE conversion.
- All edition information has been verified against WorldCat or the publisher
  catalogue. Where multiple printings exist, the most widely-cited
  scholarly edition has been chosen.
- Online open-access versions of most classical works are available at
  shamela.ws (Sunni) and shiaonlinelibrary.com (Shīʿī); these are not
  cited as official editions but are useful for verification.
