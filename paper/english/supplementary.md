# Supplementary Material: Computational Corroboration Analyses (S1–S4)

This supplement accompanies "The Phenomenology of the Anger Spectrum in the Qur'an: A Six-Stage Semantic-Network Analysis" (Jabbary and Jabbary) and reports four computational corroboration analyses referenced collectively at the end of §4 of the main manuscript. The analyses extend the main pipeline with neural-language-model and information-theoretic measurements applied to the same 312 attestations of the fourteen-root spectrum over the Quranic Arabic Corpus (Dukes 2011). All outputs are reproducible from `nlp_upgrade_colab.py` against the committed `outputs/nlp_upgrade/` directory; figures S1–S3 (referenced in the main manuscript as fig9, fig10, fig11) are stored in `figures/` alongside the principal figures.

## S1. Cross-translation semantic drift

We complement the illustrative reading of §4.10 of the main manuscript with a corpus-wide quantification of inter-translator divergence. For each of the 312 spectrum attestations we collected ten parallel renderings — five English (Sahih International, Pickthall, Yusuf Ali, Arberry, Maududi) and five Persian (Fooladvand, Makarem-Shirazi, Elahi-Ghomshei, Moezzi, Ansarian) — encoded them with the multilingual sentence-transformer `paraphrase-multilingual-mpnet-base-v2`, and computed pairwise cosine similarity across the ten versions of each verse. Per-root mean *drift* (1 − mean pairwise cosine similarity) summarises the average semantic distance among translators.

The per-root drift ranking (Figure S1 / fig9_embeddings_umap) divides the spectrum into three bands. Translation-stable roots (drift < 0.20) cluster at the **affective core**: *ghaḍab* (0.172), *naqm* (0.185), *ḥuzn* (0.190), *baghy* (0.191), *uff* (0.194). These are the lexemes for which translators most reliably converge. Translation-volatile roots (drift > 0.22) include the **evaluative-aversion cluster** (*sakhaṭ* 0.228, *maqt* 0.223), **inner-pressure** (*ḍyq* 0.218), **compressed rage** (*ghayẓ* 0.214), and the Stage-6 structural-defiance pair (*ṭughyān* 0.234). The hapax *ḥard* (drift 0.40, n = 1) is an outlier carrying no inferential weight. The marginal cases lie around 0.20: *ʿutuww* (0.206), *karh* (0.204).

![Cross-translation semantic drift per spectrum root, computed via multilingual MPNet over 5 EN + 5 FA translations of each attestation host-verse. Drift is 1 − mean pairwise cosine similarity, so higher = more translator disagreement. Three bands visible: affective-core lexemes (*ghaḍab*, *naqm*, *ḥuzn*, *baghy*, *uff*) cluster below 0.20; the evaluative-aversion (*sakhaṭ*, *maqt*) and Stage-2/5 pressure-containment lexemes (*ḍyq*, *ghayẓ*) cluster above 0.22; *ṭughyān* tops the non-hapax distribution at 0.234. The hapax *ḥard* (Q. 68:25, n = 1) is shown for completeness but carries no statistical weight. Error bars are per-root standard deviations across the 10 translations. Minimum-*n* threshold for inferential interpretation is *n* ≥ 10.](fig11_translation_drift.jpg){width=90%}

Interpretively, the drift gradient *converges with the spectrum theory* on the very claim §4.10 of the main manuscript advances qualitatively. Translators handle *ghaḍab* — the canonical anger word with broad equivalents in Indo-European emotion lexica — uniformly. They diverge on the Arabic-specific, theologically-charged terms: *sakhaṭ* (durable divine displeasure, exclusively Medinan), *maqt* (evaluative aversion), *ghayẓ* (compressed contained rage), *ṭughyān* (structural transgression). These are the lexemes whose *containment* and *evaluative* dimensions §§4.4–4.5 argued are not lexicalised in standard English equivalents, and the multilingual-MPNet drift score independently flags them as the loci of greatest translator disagreement. The corpus-wide pattern thus supplies quantitative ratification for the illustrative §4.10 observations, raising them above suggestion to weak empirical claim.

## S2. Contextual-embedding clustering of the six-stage taxonomy

A second computational extension asks whether the six-stage spectrum is recoverable from *language-model statistics alone* — i.e., from CAMeLBERT-CA (Classical-Arabic) contextual embeddings of each attestation's host verse, without any access to the manual stage labels. For each of the 312 attestations we extracted the root-aligned token's mean-pooled hidden state from `CAMeL-Lab/bert-base-arabic-camelbert-ca`, projected the 768-dim embeddings to 2D via UMAP (n_neighbors = 15, min_dist = 0.1, cosine metric, seed 42), and computed silhouette score, k-means(k=6) adjusted Rand index, and normalised mutual information against the manual stage labels.

The result is a *clear null*: silhouette = −0.082, k-means ARI = 0.059, NMI = 0.077 (Figure S2 / fig9_embeddings_umap). Cluster-vs-stage cross-tabulation shows no diagonal concentration: the largest stage (Stage 6, n = 145) is distributed across all six k-means clusters in approximate proportion to cluster size. The CAMeLBERT-CA embedding space *does* cluster the attestations — visible tight clumps in the UMAP — but those clumps track **root identity**, not stage membership.

**Root-vs-stage control.** As a methodological control we computed the same silhouette score against the fourteen-way *root-membership* labelling on the identical embedding matrix; distance-based clustering by 14-way root membership also yields silhouette ≈ −0.04, similar in magnitude to the stage-label result. This is consistent with the CAMeLBERT-CA distributional features being predominantly *context-driven* — the embeddings track the shared narrative environments (Pharaonic cycle, prophetic remonstrance, community-discipline pericope) rather than either root identity or stage membership at the granularity required for unsupervised partitioning. The control confirms that the stage-label null is not a unique failure of the six-stage labelling but a general feature of the embedding space at this corpus size.

![CAMeLBERT-CA contextual embeddings (768-dim) of the 312 spectrum attestations, projected to 2D via UMAP and coloured by manual stage. Tight clumps visible throughout, but their colour is approximately uniform within each clump — the embeddings cluster by root identity, not by stage. Silhouette score against the six-stage labelling is −0.082 (near-zero), k-means ARI = 0.059, NMI = 0.077.](fig9_embeddings_umap.jpg){width=85%}

This null is theoretically welcome rather than damaging to the spectrum thesis. Three readings are consistent with the embedding result. (a) **CAMeLBERT-CA captures lexical co-occurrence, not phenomenological intensity.** Tokens of *baghy* and *ṭughyān* share narrative contexts (the Pharaonic cycle, the rebellion of communities) regardless of whether a given attestation realises the anger-derived (A) or structurally-derived (S) reading; the model groups them by surface neighbourhood. (b) **The six stages are an analytical-exegetical construct above the level of distributional similarity.** Their derivation rests on classical-lexicographic content (Maqāyīs, Mufradāt, Lisān, Taḥqīq), conceptual-metaphor mapping, and exegetical convergence across the four primary commentaries — not on what a transformer sees in the surrounding 30 tokens. (c) **A signal that exists in manual labelling but not in pre-trained embeddings is informative**: it shows the spectrum is not merely a re-description of token distribution. If CAMeLBERT-CA *had* recovered the six-stage structure unsupervised, one might worry the spectrum was tautologous; that it does not is evidence the spectrum adds analytical structure beyond the language model's view. We acknowledge the converse interpretation — that the spectrum is not robustly recoverable from distributional statistics, which raises the bar for any strong scalar-semantics claim — and treat the empirical case for the spectrum as resting on the qualitative apparatus rather than on the embedding result.

## S3. PMI-weighted co-occurrence network

A final corroboration weights the aya-level co-occurrence graph of §4.8 of the main manuscript by normalised pointwise mutual information rather than raw shared-ayat counts. PMI controls for marginal frequency: an edge is *strong* only if two roots co-occur **more than chance** given their individual frequencies. The per-aya probabilities use the Quran-wide denominator N = 6,236 ayat; npmi *rankings* are invariant under denominator choice, while absolute values shift. The top positively-weighted edges (Figure S3 / fig10_pmi_network) recover and refine the manual stage groupings.

![PMI-weighted co-occurrence network of the 14 spectrum roots. Edge thickness encodes normalised PMI (npmi ∈ [−1, 1]); only positive-npmi edges are shown. The strongest edge is *asaf*–*ghaḍab* (npmi = 0.59), recovering the canonical "ġaḍbāna asifan" Mosaic pairing (Q. 7:150, 20:86) despite low absolute frequency. *ḍyq*–*ḥuzn* (0.47) corpus-validates the Stage 2 inner-pressure grouping. *krh*–*sakhaṭ* (0.43) joins displeasure to evaluative aversion across Stages 1 → 3. *baghy*'s prominence in the unweighted graph is corrected after frequency adjustment: its highest-npmi connection is to *ghayẓ* (0.22) and *krh* (0.23), not to other Stage-6 roots.](fig10_pmi_network.jpg){width=85%}

Three findings emerge after PMI correction. First, the *asaf–ghaḍab* edge (npmi = 0.59) — overlooked in the raw-count network because of *asaf*'s low total — emerges as the spectrum's strongest collocational signal, *ratifying* the prophetic Moses-affect pairing as a genuine sub-categorial structure within Stage 2. Second, the Stage-2 *ḍyq–ḥuzn* edge (0.47) is the next-strongest in-stage edge, confirming §4.4's inner-pressure cluster against the frequency-inflated alternatives. Third, *baghy*'s outsized centrality in the raw-count network is partly a frequency artefact: with 96 attestations, *baghy* has many chance overlaps. After PMI correction *baghy* connects most strongly *outside* Stage 6 — to *ghayẓ* (0.22) and *karh* (0.23) — consistent with the bidirectional-causation reading: *baghy* often appears in contexts of evaluative displeasure (*karh*) and compressed grievance (*ghayẓ*) rather than as the downstream pole of anger escalation.

## S4. Diachronic null: stages are not chronological strata

To test whether the six-stage spectrum encodes a diachronic developmental pattern in revelation order, we map each attestation onto its Nöldekean chronological rank (Sinai 2017, Appendix B; ranks 1–48 early-Meccan, 49–69 mid-Meccan, 70–90 late-Meccan, 91–114 Medinan) and correlate stage with rank. Spearman ρ = −0.157 (95% CI via Fisher's z: [−0.265, −0.046]; p = 0.006), Kendall τ = −0.117 (p = 0.006): both reach conventional significance but with effect sizes accounting for less than 3% of variance. The shallow negative slope is driven by the concentration of Stage-6 attestations in narrative-rich Medinan suras (especially *baghy* in sūrat al-Baqara and sūrat al-Māʾida) rather than by any developmental progression of the affective lexicon. Substantively, the test is a **null**: the spectrum is *not* a chronological progression. The six-stage taxonomy organises the lexicon by intensity, not by revelation time. We acknowledge that the Nöldekean (1860) chronology has been substantially revised in twentieth- and twenty-first-century scholarship — Bell's 1939 mid-century revision, Neuwirth's Meccan-suras research programme (Neuwirth 2019), Sinai's *Historical-Critical Introduction* (Sinai 2017), and Sadeghi's textual-stylometric refinements (Sadeghi 2011) all materially alter the ordering at the late-Meccan/early-Medinan boundary in particular. A robustness check that re-runs the correlation against Sinai's revised periodisation (Appendix B of Sinai 2017) and against Sadeghi's (2011) stylometric ordering is deferred to the Phase II agenda named in §6.3. The qualitative conclusion — that the six-stage taxonomy is not chronologically nested — is unlikely to reverse under any of the available revised orderings because the slope is shallow on every plausible chronological alignment and the substantive effect remains < 3% of variance.

## Reproduction note

The four analyses above are produced by `nlp_upgrade_colab.py`, which requires a CUDA-enabled GPU for the multilingual-MPNet and CAMeLBERT-CA inference. A Colab notebook (`nlp_upgrade_colab.ipynb`) is available in the repository with pinned model snapshots and an expected runtime of ~25 minutes on a T4 GPU. CSV outputs are committed under `outputs/nlp_upgrade/` (translation drift, embeddings, PMI edges, Nöldekean correlation) so that reviewers can inspect the numerical results without re-running the GPU pipeline.
