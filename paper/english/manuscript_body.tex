\subsection{Abstract}\label{abstract}

The Qur'an deploys an exceptionally articulated lexicon for the
\emph{anger spectrum}, yet existing scholarship treats it word-by-word
or through normative-ethical frames. This study argues that fourteen
core Arabic roots (\emph{ʾff, krh, ḍyq, ḥzn, ʾsf, nqm, sxṭ, mqt, ġḍb,
ḥrd, ġyẓ, bġy, ṭġy, ʿtw}) form a graded semantic field organised by
\emph{intensity of action} across six phenomenological stages: pre-anger
displeasure, inner pressure, evaluative aversion, active anger,
compressed rage, and behavioural outcomes. We integrate Izutsu's
semantic-field approach, Kennedy--Sassoon scalar semantics, and
Lakoff--Kövecses conceptual metaphor theory, validating the continuum
against the Quranic Arabic Corpus\footnote{Kais Dukes, \emph{Quranic
  Arabic Corpus (Morphology, Version 0.4)} (Leeds: University of Leeds,
  2011), available at https://corpus.quran.com (GPL-licensed).} through
a reproducible pipeline over 312 attestations. Three findings are
reported under uncertainty-aware metrics. First, the asymptotic χ²(5) =
227.15 is large but a permutation null yields \emph{p} = 0.24; the
omnibus is \emph{consistent with} rather than confirmatory of
non-uniformity, the substantive case resting on qualitative evidence.
Second, \emph{sakhaṭ} attests exclusively in Medinan suras
(Holm-adjusted \emph{p} = 0.05), the sole confirmed instance of
\emph{discursive-contextual lexical specialisation}. Third, network
analysis identifies \emph{baghy} as a bridging node to the broader
moral-evaluation field. Q. 67:8 (\emph{takādu tamayyazu min al-ġayẓ}) is
an unusually transparent instance of ``container collapse''; a κ =
0.79-validated tafsir audit supports a \emph{bidirectional-causation}
reading of Stage 6. Implications follow for Qur'anic translation and
Islamic psychology.

\textbf{Keywords:} Qur'an, anger spectrum, semantic field, cognitive
linguistics, scalar semantics, conceptual metaphor, emotion regulation,
Arabic corpus linguistics, Izutsu, Islamic psychology.

\subsection{1. Introduction}\label{introduction}

\subsubsection{1.1. Statement of the
problem}\label{statement-of-the-problem}

The Qur'anic lexicon for the \emph{anger spectrum} exhibits a precision
that survives only with difficulty into translation. Where English
versions level \emph{uff}, \emph{karh}, \emph{ḍīq}, \emph{ḥuzn},
\emph{asaf}, \emph{naqm}, \emph{sakhaṭ}, \emph{maqt}, \emph{ghaḍab},
\emph{ḥard}, \emph{ghayẓ}, \emph{baghy}, \emph{ṭughyān}, and
\emph{ʿutuww} into a small set of broad equivalents --- ``displeasure,''
``distress,'' ``grief,'' ``anger,'' ``rage,'' ``transgression,''
``rebellion'' --- the Arabic original distinguishes them with the
precision of a graded taxonomy. We advance the structural hypothesis
that these fourteen Qur'anic anger-spectrum roots are not synonyms but
coordinated nodes in a single \emph{graded} semantic field, organised
along \emph{intensity of action} (\emph{shiddat al-kunish} in the
Persian original), traversing pre-anger displeasure, inner pressure and
contraction, evaluative aversion, active anger, compressed/explosive
rage, and --- at the behavioural pole --- outcomes (\emph{baghy},
\emph{ṭughyān}, \emph{ʿutuww}) whose causation is bidirectional rather
than purely emotional.

This claim has consequences beyond philology: it implies that the Qur'an
encodes --- through its lexical architecture, not by explicit
theoretical statement --- an implicit theory of the genealogy of moral
failure, in which the social-structural pathologies the text most
frequently denounces (transgression, tyranny, defiance) are
\emph{related to} and often developmentally downstream from inner
experiences of irritation, dislike, constriction, and grief. As we argue
in §4--§5, the relationship at the behavioural pole is
\emph{bidirectional}: anger may produce
\emph{baghy}/\emph{ṭughyān}/\emph{ʿutuww}, but these behaviours can
equally produce anger (in their victims, the prophets, the divine
response). The distance from \emph{uff} (Q. 17:23) to \emph{ʿutuww} (Q.
25:21) is graded rather than categorical --- the distance walked, absent
intervention, by an unmanaged life of irritation.

\subsubsection{1.2. Aims and originality}\label{aims-and-originality}

This paper has three aims. The first is \textbf{methodological}: to
demonstrate that combining Izutsu's qualitative semantic-field approach,
scalar-semantic ordering in the Kennedy--Sassoon tradition, and
conceptual-metaphor theory in the Lakoff--Kövecses tradition produces
stronger philological claims than any of the three frameworks in
isolation. The claim is deliberately measured. Izutsu's field method, in
the three foundational works (1959, 1964, 2002),\footnote{Izutsu,
  \emph{Structure of the Ethical Terms}; idem, \emph{God and Man}; idem,
  \emph{Ethico-Religious Concepts}.} yields a network of mutually
defining lexemes around focus words, but it does \emph{not} generate an
ordering relation \emph{within} the network: Izutsu recovers the field
but is silent on whether, within the field, \emph{ġayẓ} is ``more
intense than'' \emph{ghaḍab}. Lakoff and Kövecses (1987) supply a
cognitive-metaphor schema anchored lexicographically in \emph{English}
(Holland--Quinn's American idioms), and the two-volume \emph{Metaphors
of ANGER across Languages} (Kövecses, Benczes and Szelid
2025)\footnote{Kövecses, Benczes and Szelid (eds), \emph{Metaphors of
  ANGER}.} --- twenty-two languages from Mandarin to Yoruba ---
\emph{omits Classical Arabic entirely}. Kennedy and Sassoon's
scalar-semantic apparatus orders predicates along a dimension and a unit
but has not been applied to a Qur'anic emotion field. The present
paper's distinct contribution is to \emph{do all three together} on the
same fourteen-root inventory: the Izutsu layer fixes field membership,
the Kennedy--Sassoon layer fixes the action-intensity ordering, and the
CMT layer grounds the climactic \emph{ghayẓ--kaẓm--tamayyuz} sequence.
We acknowledge that Qāʾimīniyā\footnote{Qāʾimīniyā,
  \emph{Maʿnāshināsī-yi Shinākhtī}; and idem, \emph{Istiʿārah-hā-yi
  Mafhūmī}.} and Pakatchi--Afrashi\footnote{Pakatchi and Afrashi,
  \emph{Rūykardhā-yi Maʿnā-shinākhtī}.} already integrate Izutsu's field
method with CMT in Persian; the \emph{novel} element in the present
paper is the Kennedy--Sassoon scalar layer operationalised as a
six-stage action-intensity continuum tested against a 312-attestation
reproducible pipeline. The second aim is \textbf{descriptive}: to map
every Qur'anic occurrence of the fourteen core roots, render that map
publicly verifiable, and extract empirical regularities previous
scholarship was not in a position to detect. The third aim is
\textbf{interpretive}: to argue that the continuum constitutes a
coherent logic of anger escalation with deep structural affinities to
modern emotion psychology, and to identify the points at which the
Qur'anic continuum \emph{exceeds} the contemporary models --- most
pointedly at Stage 6, where \emph{baghy}/\emph{ṭughyān}/\emph{ʿutuww}
extend the regulatory horizon of Gross's process model into the
moral-anthropological register.

\textbf{Originality} disaggregated into three contributions with ``to
our knowledge'' qualifications. \textbf{(i) Methodological synthesis}
(moderate): the trifold integration of Izutsu's field method,
Kennedy--Sassoon scalar semantics, and Lakoff--Kövecses CMT under a
corpus pipeline. We acknowledge Qāʾimīniyā\footnote{Qāʾimīniyā,
  \emph{Maʿnāshināsī-yi Shinākhtī}; and idem, \emph{Istiʿārah-hā-yi
  Mafhūmī}.} and Pakatchi--Afrashi\footnote{Pakatchi and Afrashi,
  \emph{Rūykardhā-yi Maʿnā-shinākhtī}.} already integrate Izutsu and CMT
in Persian Qur'anic scholarship; the novel element is the
Kennedy--Sassoon scalar layer over a reproducible computational
pipeline. \textbf{(ii) Empirical findings} (variable strength): of four
distributional patterns, only the exclusively-Medinan \emph{sakhaṭ} (raw
\emph{p} = 0.005; \textbf{Holm-Bonferroni adj. \emph{p} = 0.05})
survives FDR --- borderline rather than emphatic. \emph{Asaf} (5/0),
\emph{naqm} (12/5), \emph{ʿutuww} (9/1) tilts do not survive correction
and are reported as contextual observations. \textbf{(iii) Interpretive
contribution} (most substantive): \emph{bidirectional-causation reading
of Stage 6} via κ = 0.79-validated tafsir-coding (A = 26\%, S = 49\%,
A+S = 25\%); A-only re-analysis χ²(1) = 81.4, \emph{p} \textless{} 0.001
with phenomenological core dominating anger-derived outcomes
\textasciitilde4.4×. This reconciles a puzzling distributional finding
with the structurally-driven character of
\emph{baghy}/\emph{ṭughyān}/\emph{ʿutuww} in classical exegesis. To
these add \textbf{(iv)} a fully reproducible MIT-licensed pipeline
(SHA-256 pinning, permutation/bootstrap, 50/50 manual validation) and
\textbf{(v)} the comparative observation that Q. 67:8 is an \emph{early
and unusually transparent} instance of ``container collapse'',
calibrated in §5.5 by a focused six-tradition philological survey that
acknowledges Job 32:19 (\emph{bāqaʿ}) as the one clear Northwest-Semitic
precedent for vessel-rupture imagery.

\subsubsection{1.3. Prior work and the
gap}\label{prior-work-and-the-gap}

Prior scholarship clusters into five lines, each insufficient on its own
to address the structural question motivating this paper.
\textbf{Single-word lexical studies}: the most directly relevant
Persian-language precedent --- Narimani, Iqbali and Chari\footnote{Zahra
  Narimani, Masoud Iqbali and Majid Chari, `Semantic Re-analysis of the
  Words \emph{ghayẓ}, \emph{ghaḍab}, and \emph{sakhaṭ} in the Holy
  Qurʾān with an Exegetical Approach', \emph{Pizhūhish-hā-yi Qurʾān va
  Ḥadīth} 54:1 (1400 SH/2021), pp.~219-239, in Persian, DOI:
  10.22059/jqst.2021.322181.669740.} on \emph{ghayẓ}, \emph{ghaḍab}, and
\emph{sakhaṭ} --- establishes on tafsir grounds that \emph{ghaḍab} is
central, \emph{sakhaṭ} reserved for divine displeasure with hypocrites,
and \emph{ghayẓ} exclusive to unbelievers/hypocrites in
human-attribution contexts; we confirm and extend their findings with
corpus-distributional evidence and the \emph{kaẓm} analysis. Qarizadeh
et al.\footnote{M. Qarizadeh, A. Salmani Marvast, A. Meymandi and A.
  Farsi, `Polysemy of the Word \emph{ṭughyān} in the Qurʾān: A
  Linguistic Approach', \emph{Pizhūhish-hā-yi Zabānshinākhtī-yi Qurʾān}
  (University of Isfahan) (1402 SH/2024), in Persian.} on \emph{ṭughyān}
show the root attests in 27 suras with the central meaning of
transgressing the limit. Both works illustrate the present gap:
high-quality lexical work for individual lexemes, but no integrated
continuum and no operational ordering relation among the lexemes of the
field. \textbf{Semantic-field and cognitive-semantic studies} in the
Izutsu tradition --- Qāʾimīniyā,\footnote{Qāʾimīniyā,
  \emph{Maʿnāshināsī-yi Shinākhtī}; and idem, \emph{Istiʿārah-hā-yi
  Mafhūmī}.} Pakatchi--Afrashi,\footnote{Pakatchi and Afrashi,
  \emph{Rūykardhā-yi Maʿnā-shinākhtī}.} Albayrak\footnote{Ismail
  Albayrak, `Semantics of the Qurʾānic Weltanschauung: A Critical
  Analysis of Toshihiko Izutsu's Works', \emph{American Journal of Islam
  and Society} 37:1-2 (2020).} --- have applied cognitive linguistics to
Qur'anic vocabulary, but the \emph{graded continuum} layer (Izutsu's own
deferral of the ordering question, §2.1) remains untreated. Izutsu's
three-layer apparatus (focus words / fields / key words) operates on
lexical-semantic \emph{relations} (paradigmatic, syntagmatic,
contrastive), \emph{not} on a measurable dimension along which field
members can be ordered; the Kennedy--Sassoon scalar-semantic

  idem, `Vagueness and Grammar', pp.~8-15; Sassoon, `Degree Functions of

open. \textbf{Ethics-of-anger studies} in the \emph{kaẓm al-ghayẓ}
tradition are normatively rich and linguistically thin. \textbf{The
cross-linguistic CMT literature} has been decisively updated by
Kövecses, Benczes and Szelid's \emph{Metaphors of ANGER across

\subsubsection{1.4. Research questions and
hypotheses}\label{research-questions-and-hypotheses}

We address five research questions: (RQ1) the internal structure of the
Qur'anic anger-spectrum field and its principal nodes; (RQ2) the
continuum ordering by action-intensity and the theoretical framework
legitimating it; (RQ3) corpus-distributional corroboration of the
continuum (frequency, Meccan/Medinan, morphology, network centrality);
(RQ4) structural homology with Gross, Plutchik, Spielberger, and
Lazarus-Scherer appraisal theory; (RQ5) practical implications for
Qur'anic translation and clinical Islamic psychology.

The principal \textbf{Qur'anic Action-Intensity Continuum Hypothesis}
holds that the fourteen core roots (ʾff, krh, ḍyq, ḥzn, ʾsf, nqm, sxṭ,
mqt, ġḍb, ḥrd, ġyẓ, bġy, ṭġy, ʿtw) operate as fourteen distinct grades
on a single semantic continuum structured by \emph{intensity of action},
organised in six phenomenological stages, supported by qualitative
(etymological, exegetical, metaphorical) and quantitative (frequency,
distribution, network) evidence. Five subsidiary hypotheses: (H1)
non-uniform six-stage frequency distribution; (H2) near-equal split
between phenomenological core (S1--5) and behavioural pole (S6),
supporting the bidirectional Stage-6 reading; (H3)
discursively-specialised exclusive-Medinan distribution of
\emph{sakhaṭ}; (H4) \emph{baghy} as bridge node in the co-occurrence
network; (H5) Q. 67:8 as transparent instance of ``container collapse''
CMT failure mode.

\subsection{2. Theoretical Framework}\label{theoretical-framework}

\subsubsection{2.1. Izutsu's semantic-field
method}\label{izutsus-semantic-field-method}

Toshihiko Izutsu, in three foundational works---\emph{The Structure of
the Ethical Terms in the Koran}, \emph{God and Man in the Koran}, and
\emph{Ethico-Religious Concepts in the Qurʾān}\footnote{Toshihiko
  Izutsu, \emph{The Structure of the Ethical Terms in the Koran: A Study
  in Semantics}, Studies in the Humanities and Social Relations 2
  (Tokyo: Keio Institute of Philological Studies, 1959); idem, \emph{God
  and Man in the Koran: Semantics of the Koranic Weltanschauung} (Tokyo:
  Keio Institute of Cultural and Linguistic Studies, 1964); idem,
  \emph{Ethico-Religious Concepts in the Qurʾān}, rev. edn (Montreal:
  McGill-Queen's University Press, 2002; orig. 1966).}---introduced a
methodological revolution to Qur'anic studies. Drawing on the
structuralist linguistics of the Trier--Weisgerber tradition and on East
Asian philosophy of language, Izutsu argued that the central terms of
the Qur'an---\emph{Allāh}, \emph{īmān}, \emph{kufr}, \emph{ẓulm},
\emph{taqwā}---are not isolated propositions but nodes in a network of
syntagmatic, paradigmatic, and oppositional relations. The aggregate of
these relations constitutes what he called the ``Qur'anic
\emph{Weltanschauung}.''

Izutsu's analytic vocabulary distinguishes three layers: \emph{focus
words} are the central organising concepts of a given semantic field;
\emph{semantic fields} are the networks of mutually defining lexemes
formed around focus words; and \emph{key words} are the terms that recur
across multiple fields and serve to integrate them into a single
worldview. This three-layer structure has proved extraordinarily
productive in subsequent Qur'anic semantic work, both in English (Saleh,
Rippin) and Persian.\footnote{See Qāʾimīniyā, \emph{Maʿnāshināsī-yi
  Shinākhtī}; idem, \emph{Istiʿārah-hā-yi Mafhūmī}; and Pakatchi and
  Afrashi, \emph{Rūykardhā-yi Maʿnā-shinākhtī}.}

For the present paper, Izutsu's framework justifies the move from
atomistic single-lexeme analysis to integrated field analysis. The
fourteen target roots are not chosen at random but as the field around
the focus words \emph{ghaḍab} (the central anger term) and \emph{baghy}
(the central behavioural-outcome term), with the lower-intensity cluster
(\emph{uff}, \emph{karh}, \emph{ḍīq}, \emph{ḥuzn}, \emph{asaf})
identifying the pre-anger and inner-pressure boundary of the field and
\emph{ʿutuww} its upper-intensity boundary. Yet Izutsu's framework, in
its original form, is silent on a question central to our enterprise:
granted that these lexemes co-belong to a field, \emph{what is the
structural relation between them within the field?} It is at this point
that we require scalar semantics.

\subsubsection{2.2. Scalar semantics: from network to
continuum}\label{scalar-semantics-from-network-to-continuum}

The contemporary linguistic theory of \emph{gradable
predicates}\footnote{Christopher Kennedy, \emph{Projecting the
  Adjective: The Syntax and Semantics of Gradability and Comparison},
  Outstanding Dissertations in Linguistics (New York: Garland, 1999);
  idem, `Vagueness and Grammar: The Semantics of Relative and Absolute
  Gradable Adjectives', \emph{Linguistics and Philosophy} 30:1 (2007),
  pp.~1-45; Galit Weidman Sassoon, `The Degree Functions of Negative
  Adjectives', \emph{Natural Language Semantics} 18:2 (2010),
  pp.~141-181.} supplies the missing apparatus. A graded predicate maps
to a scale characterised by three components: a \emph{dimension} of
measurement, an \emph{ordering} on that dimension, and a unit of
comparison. Scalar predicates take degree modifiers and admit
comparative constructions, signalling their scalar structure. The
Qur'anic anger field is precisely such a scale: the dimension is
\emph{intensity of action} (decomposable into locus, experiential
intensity, scope of consequence); the ordering follows the six-stage
progression; the implicit unit is the \emph{degree of externalisation}
of emotion from inner phenomenology to social rupture. Without this
framework, the claim that \emph{ghayẓ} is ``more intense than''
\emph{ghaḍab} relies on tacit native-speaker intuition.

\subsubsection{2.3. Conceptual metaphor theory and the container
schema}\label{conceptual-metaphor-theory-and-the-container-schema}

Conceptual metaphor theory\footnote{George Lakoff and Mark Johnson,
  \emph{Metaphors We Live By} (Chicago: University of Chicago Press,
  1980); George Lakoff and Zoltán Kövecses, `The Cognitive Model of
  Anger Inherent in American English', in Dorothy Holland and Naomi
  Quinn (eds), \emph{Cultural Models in Language and Thought}
  (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1987), pp.~195-221; Zoltán
  Kövecses, \emph{Metaphor and Emotion: Language, Culture, and Body in
  Human Feeling} (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2000); idem,
  \emph{Metaphor: A Practical Introduction}, 2nd edn (Oxford: Oxford
  University Press, 2010); Kövecses, Benczes and Szelid (eds),
  \emph{Metaphors of ANGER}.} treats metaphor as a cognitive structure
that allows abstract domains (emotion) to be understood through concrete
ones (container, heat). Lakoff and Kövecses\footnote{Lakoff and
  Kövecses, `Cognitive Model of Anger', pp.~195-221.} established that
English anger is organised around ANGER IS THE HEAT OF A FLUID IN A
PRESSURIZED CONTAINER, accommodating a four-step trajectory: fluid in
container → intensifying pressure → containment attempts → container
collapse. Our central observation is that the Qur'anic triplet
\emph{ghayẓ--kaẓm--tamayyuz} operationalises this exact structure
thirteen centuries early. \emph{Ghayẓ} is glossed by all four
authoritative lexica as ``compressed contained anger'' --- fluid in
sealed vessel. \emph{Kaẓm}, etymologically tying off a waterskin, is the
agent-internal regulation that maintains containment (Q. 3:134
\emph{al-kāẓimīn al-ghayẓ}). \emph{Tamayyuz} (Q. 67:8) depicts the
failure mode: the container itself bursts apart. The Qur'anic verb
derives from \emph{m-y-z} (to part, sever) and figures the
disintegration of the \emph{vessel}, not (as in English \emph{explode},
\emph{burst}, \emph{blow up}) the \emph{exit} of contents --- an
unusually transparent instantiation of the failure-mode schema. We treat
\emph{tamayyuz} as a \emph{manifestation} of Stage 5, not an independent
core root.

\subsubsection{2.4. The integrated
framework}\label{the-integrated-framework}

The three layers operate complementarily: Izutsu identifies field
boundaries; Kennedy-Sassoon scalar semantics order members along the
action-intensity dimension; CMT explains the cognitive basis of the
pressurisation-collapse metaphor organising Stage 5. Each supplies what
the others lack --- without Izutsu, no motivation for
field-coordination; without scalar semantics, no apparatus for ordering;
without CMT, no cognitive grounding for the climactic
\emph{kaẓm--tamayyuz} sequence.

\subsection{3. Methods}\label{methods}

\subsubsection{3.1. Corpus}\label{corpus}

The empirical analysis uses the \textbf{Quranic Arabic Corpus} (QAC),
version 0.4, prepared by Kais Dukes at the University of Leeds and
released under the GPL.\footnote{Dukes, \emph{Quranic Arabic Corpus}.}
The QAC provides a morphologically tagged record for each of the 128,219
segmentable units in the Qur'anic text. Every record specifies: (a)
location in the form \emph{(sūra:āya:word:segment)}; (b) surface form in
Buckwalter ASCII transliteration; (c) part-of-speech tag; (d)
morphological features including root, lemma, gender, number, case, and
(where applicable) semantic flags. Verse text in Uthmanic \emph{rasm}
and Meccan/Medinan classification of suras are drawn from the Tanzil
project,\footnote{Tanzil Project, \emph{The Tanzil Quran Text} (Uthmani
  edition) (2008), https://tanzil.net (CC-BY-ND 3.0).} distributed under
CC-BY-ND 3.0.

\subsubsection{3.2. Selection of target
roots}\label{selection-of-target-roots}

The fourteen core roots were selected by three concurrent criteria,
applied transparently and reported here so that the inventory is not
vulnerable to the charge of post-hoc selection: (i) \emph{exegetical
centrality} --- appearance as a focal reference in at least three of the
four authoritative lexica (Ibn Fāris's \emph{Maqāyīs}, al-Rāghib's
\emph{Mufradāt}, Ibn Manẓūr's \emph{Lisān}, and Mostafavi's
twentieth-century \emph{Taḥqīq}); (ii) \emph{minimum corpus frequency}
of one attestation, admitting Qur'anic hapax legomena (\emph{ḥard}, Q.
68:25) when supported by an explicit gloss in at least three of the four
lexica plus consistent treatment in the four canonical tafsirs of §3.5;
(iii) \emph{thematic membership} in the anger-spectrum field without
significant overlap with adjacent fields (epistemology, faith, normative
ethics).

\textbf{Excluded candidate roots} (transparency): \emph{q-h-r}
(dominance/power, \emph{al-Qahhār} divine name); \emph{ḥ-n-q}
(post-Qur'anic, ḥadīth-only); \emph{ʿ-n-f} (adverbial harshness, not
emotion); \emph{ḍ-r-b} (polysemous, conflict-scene);
\emph{q-ṣ-w}/\emph{q-s-y} (hardness-of-heart, epistemic-moral);
\emph{gh-l-ẓ} (adverbial harshness); \emph{ʾ-n-f} (nominal \emph{anf}
``nose'', post-Qur'anic anger sense); \emph{š-n-ʾ} (binary
loving/hating, reclassified as umbrella). \emph{Karh} (Stage 1) is
borderline --- \emph{karāha} is affective, \emph{ikrāh} (coercion)
structural; we include the affective sense and discuss the structural
extension separately (§4.3.1). Five umbrella moral terms (\textbf{šnʾ,
jrm, fsq, ẓlm, ʿdw}) are used for robustness (§4.9) and cross-field
co-occurrence (Table 4), excluded from the principal continuum either
because of inflated cardinality (\emph{ẓlm} n=315 functions as a
meta-category) or because they belong to adjacent normative-ethics
fields.

\subsubsection{3.3. Computational
pipeline}\label{computational-pipeline}

The Python (MIT-licensed) pipeline operates in five steps: parse the QAC
morphology file; filter on target roots; collapse multi-segment
morphemes to surface words preserving the \emph{(sūra:āya)} coordinate;
join with sura-level metadata for Meccan/Medinan classification; emit
master concordance, per-root concordances, distributional summaries, and
network/centrality CSVs. The complete analysis is reproducible from a
fresh clone of the repository in four commands (\texttt{pip\ install},
\texttt{extract\_concordance.py}, \texttt{advanced\_metrics.py},
\texttt{visualize\_*.py}).

\textbf{Reproducibility audit (summary).} QAC v0.4\footnote{Dukes,
  \emph{Quranic Arabic Corpus}; release date May 2011.} is pinned by
SHA-256 in the repository; minimum-version dependencies are listed in
\texttt{requirements.txt}; outputs are released under MIT (code) and
CC-BY 4.0 (manuscripts/figures); the 14 headline exemplar verses and a
stratified 50-segment audit on the four most frequent core roots
(\emph{ḥzn}, \emph{bġy}, \emph{ġḍb}, \emph{ṭġy}) returned 100\% correct
segment-to-root assignment against Tanzil. Full audit at
\texttt{data/concordance/validation\_audit.md}.

\subsubsection{3.4. Quantitative analyses}\label{quantitative-analyses}

Quantitative tests beyond frequency tabulation: (a) χ²(5) and χ²(1) for
uniform-stage and S1--5/S6 nulls, with Cramér's \emph{V} and Cohen's
\emph{w}, plus a marginal-preserving permutation null (10,000 perms,
deterministic seed) for the omnibus; (b) three monotonic-trend tests
(Spearman ρ, Mann-Kendall, Cochran-Armitage-style OLS slope); (c)
two-sided exact binomials with the corpus-derived 0.74 Meccan-ayat
baseline, Holm-Bonferroni step-down adjusted over the family of fourteen
per-root tests, and a granular sensitivity table at baselines
0.70--0.78; (d) morphological breakdown by POS; (e) aya-level
co-occurrence network\footnote{Cf. M. S. Mottaghi et al., `A Graph-based
  Algorithm for Clustering Qurʾānic Surahs', \emph{Signal and Data
  Processing Journal (Iran)} (2024).} with weighted degree, betweenness,
closeness, and eigenvector centrality, accompanied by non-parametric
bootstrap 95\% percentile CIs (1,000 ayat-resamples with replacement).
The combined uncertainty-aware reporting is, to our knowledge, the most
rigorous yet applied to Qur'anic computational-corpus studies; the wider
2025 cross-scripture NLP literature\footnote{E.g. M. Nandan, I. Godbole,
  P. Kapparad and S. Bhattacharjee, `Comparative Analysis of Religious
  Texts: NLP Approaches to the Bible, Quran, and Bhagavad Gītā', in
  \emph{Proceedings of the Workshop on New Horizons in Computational
  Linguistics for Religious Texts (CLRel)} (Stroudsburg, PA: Association
  for Computational Linguistics, 2025).} operates at the discourse-topic
level on coarse sentiment and does not yet apply this battery at the
lexeme level.

\subsubsection{3.5. Qualitative analyses}\label{qualitative-analyses}

Qualitative complements: (a) comparative etymology across the four
authoritative lexica (Maqāyīs, Mufradāt, Lisān, Taḥqīq); (b) comparative
\emph{tafsīr} analysis on a broadened set of nine commentaries --- the
four primary (al-Ṭabāṭabāʾī's \emph{al-Mīzān}, al-Zamakhsharī's
\emph{al-Kashshāf}, al-Ṭabarsī's \emph{Majmaʿ al-Bayān}, Ibn ʿĀshūr's
\emph{al-Taḥrīr}) supplemented with al-Ṭabarī, al-Qurṭubī, Ibn Kathīr,
Sayyid Quṭb, and ʿAbduh-Riḍā's \emph{al-Manār}; for Sufi-tradition
cross-checking on \emph{kaẓm al-ghayẓ} (§4.7), al-Qushayrī's
\emph{Laṭāʾif al-Ishārāt}. Where the broader set diverges from the
primary four on key claims (whether \emph{ghayẓ} is exclusively
predicated of unbelievers; whether \emph{sakhaṭ} is exclusively directed
at the \emph{Munāfiqūn}; whether \emph{naqm} admits a non-retributive
sense), divergences are recorded in §4 and corresponding claims
qualified. (c) phenomenological analysis of contextual markers
(\emph{ḍīq al-ṣadr}, \emph{ḥuzn al-qalb}, \emph{asaf al-nafs},
\emph{ḍāqa dharʿan}); (d) illustrative translation-criticism on five
English translations (Yusuf Ali, Pickthall, Arberry, Saheeh
International, Hilali-Khan) at canonical exemplar verses (§4.10) ---
framed throughout as illustrative rather than systematic.

algorithms operate without systematic error.

\textbf{On the choice of permutation over asymptotic χ² for the sparse
six-cell design.} The omnibus test of §4.2 (χ²(5) over 312 attestations
in a six-cell design) has one cell at \emph{n} = 11 (Stage 5) and
another at \emph{n} = 145 (Stage 6), with \emph{baghy} alone supplying
96 attestations --- almost a third of the total and twice the per-stage
uniform expectation. On such marginally-imbalanced designs the
asymptotic χ² approximation is known to be unreliable:
Cochran\footnote{William G. Cochran, `Some Methods for Strengthening the
  Common χ² Tests', \emph{Biometrics} 10:4 (1954), pp.~417-451.} warns
that observed cell frequencies dominating a single category can inflate
the statistic without corresponding evidence against the null. Fisher's
exact test\footnote{R. A. Fisher, `On the Interpretation of χ² from
  Contingency Tables, and the Calculation of \emph{p}', \emph{Journal of
  the Royal Statistical Society} 85:1 (1922), pp.~87-94.} solves the
problem in principle for 2 × 2 tables, with Patefield's network
algorithm\footnote{W. M. Patefield, `An Efficient Method of Generating
  Random R × C Tables with Given Row and Column Totals', \emph{Applied
  Statistics} 30:1 (1981), pp.~91-97.} and the Mehta--Patel
extension\footnote{Cyrus R. Mehta and Nitin R. Patel, `A Network
  Algorithm for Performing Fisher's Exact Test in \emph{r} × \emph{c}
  Contingency Tables', \emph{Journal of the American Statistical
  Association} 78:382 (1983), pp.~427-434.} enabling feasible
enumeration for general \emph{r} × \emph{c} tables. The contemporary
recommendation (Agresti)\footnote{Alan Agresti, \emph{Categorical Data
  Analysis}, 3rd edn (Hoboken, NJ: Wiley, 2013), §3.5.} is to favour
permutation- or exact-conditional inference for sparse contingency
tables, retaining the asymptotic χ² as a descriptive statistic. Our
marginal-preserving permutation null (10,000 perms, fixed RNG seed
20260509) holds the per-root attestation counts fixed and randomly
permutes the fourteen stage labels across roots; the empirical
\emph{p}-value is the fraction of permuted χ² values meeting or
exceeding the observed 227.15. This is the natural extension of
Fisher-exact reasoning to a many-cell design where exact enumeration of
the conditional reference set is intractable.

Aggregating across the six stages: Stage 1 = 44; Stage 2 = 60; Stage 3 =
27; Stage 4 = 25; Stage 5 = 11; Stage 6 = 145. The asymptotic
chi-squared test against the null of uniform six-stage distribution
rejects (χ²(5) = 227.15, \emph{p} \textless{} 0.001), with effect sizes
Cramér's \emph{V} = 0.38 and Cohen's \emph{w} = 0.85 (large effect by
Cohen's conventions). However, an asymptotic χ² on a six-cell
distribution with one cell at \emph{n} = 11 (Stage 5) and another at
\emph{n} = 145 (Stage 6) is not above suspicion: the chi-squared
approximation requires expected counts ≥ 5 in every cell (which is met)
but is \emph{known} to be conservative under marginal
imbalance.\footnote{William G. Cochran, `Some Methods for Strengthening
  the Common χ² Tests', \emph{Biometrics} 10:4 (1954), pp.~417-451.
  (Bibliography entry to be added on author follow-up; cited from
  secondary literature.)} We therefore complement the asymptotic test
with a \textbf{marginal-preserving permutation null}: the per-root
attestation counts are held fixed and the fourteen stage labels are
randomly permuted across roots (10,000 permutations, fixed RNG seed
20260509), yielding the empirical \emph{p}-value as the fraction of
permuted χ² values that meet or exceed the observed 227.15. The result
is \emph{p} = 0.24 --- i.e.~under random stage relabelling of the
fourteen roots, a χ² statistic this extreme is not unusual, because much
of the omnibus's mass derives from the fact that \emph{baghy} alone
contributes 96 attestations (versus a uniform expectation of
\textasciitilde52 per stage) and would inflate any single-stage cell
into which it fell. We read this honestly: the asymptotic significance
over-states the strength of evidence for non-uniformity on this sparse,
marginally-imbalanced design, and the substantive case for the six-stage
architecture rests on the qualitative lexicographic, exegetical, and
metaphorical evidence developed in §4.1 and §4.3--§4.8 rather than on
the omnibus \emph{p}-value alone. We retain the asymptotic χ² as a
descriptive statistic with appropriate effect-size reporting, but treat
it as \emph{consistent with} --- not as \emph{positive evidence for} ---
H1. A discriminating test of monotonicity, distinct from the omnibus, is
reported in §4.2.1 below. A complementary chi-squared test against the
null of equal split between the phenomenological core (Stages 1--5 = 167
attestations) and the behavioural-outcomes pole (Stage 6 = 145
attestations) \emph{fails to reject} (χ²(1) = 1.55, Cohen's \emph{w} =
0.07 --- trivial effect). Because failure to reject a null is
\emph{absence of evidence of a difference} and not evidence of equality,
we report this result not as positive corroboration of H2 but as a
finding \emph{consistent with} the reading developed in §4.8.5 and §5.3:
the Stage-6 lexemes (\emph{baghy}, \emph{ṭughyān}, \emph{ʿutuww}) are
not exclusively anger-derived but stand in a \emph{bidirectional} causal
relation to the anger spectrum---anger may produce these behaviours, but
the behaviours can equally produce anger (in their victims, the
prophets, and the divine response). The test is, with these sample
sizes, underpowered to discriminate between competing models of the
Stage 1--5 ↔ Stage 6 relation; the substantive interpretation rests on
the qualitative tafsir-grounded analysis of §4.8.5, not on the
statistical near-balance.

\paragraph{4.2.1. Monotonic-trend tests for the six-stage
ordering}\label{monotonic-trend-tests-for-the-six-stage-ordering}

Because the χ²(5) test confirms non-uniformity but says nothing about
\emph{ordering}, we report three complementary monotonic-trend tests of
stage rank against attestation frequency. (i) \textbf{Spearman
rank-correlation} between stage index (1--6) and per-stage attestation
count is ρ = --0.09 with two-sided asymptotic \emph{p} = 0.85 --- the
count rises from S1 (44) to S2 (60), drops through S3--S5 (27, 25, 11),
then jumps to S6 (145), producing a non-monotonic profile that the
rank-correlation correctly identifies. (ii) \textbf{Mann-Kendall}
(small-sample-robust) yields \emph{S} = --3, \emph{z} = --0.38, \emph{p}
= 0.71. (iii) \textbf{Cochran--Armitage-style} OLS slope of count on
stage rank yields slope = 10.17, \emph{z} = 0.85, \emph{p} = 0.40. All
three tests fail to reject the no-trend null.

We read this as the substantively interpretable result: the
action-intensity continuum is an ordering of \emph{semantic intensity},
not of \emph{attestation frequency}. The Qur'anic corpus is not a random
sample from a population of anger-spectrum lexicalisations but a closed
hortatory text whose lexical weights are \emph{pedagogically
determined}. One expects heaviest density precisely at the two poles of
moral consequence --- Stages 1--2 (the lexicalised entry points for
\emph{kaẓm}, \emph{taqwā}, \emph{ḥilm}) and Stage 6 (the
social-structural pathologies the text most frequently denounces) ---
with intervening stages (S3--5) middle-trajectory states of lower
independent pedagogical salience.

The failure of the three trend tests therefore \emph{supports}, rather
than challenges, the bidirectional-causation reading developed in
§4.8.5: a randomly-sampled language sample would predict monotonic
frequency-as-intensity, but a hortatory text predicts emphasis at the
points where ethical pedagogy can intervene (S1--2, where regulation is
feasible) and where moral consequences manifest (S6, where the
structural pathologies are catalogued). A \emph{flat} trend test, given
this structure, is the prediction the theory generates, not an
embarrassment to it. The load-bearing evidence for the continuum's
structure remains the qualitative apparatus of §4.1 and §4.3--§4.8
(lexicographic, exegetical, metaphorical) and the morphological gradient
reported in Figure 5.

\subsubsection{4.3. Stage 1 --- Pre-anger
displeasure}\label{stage-1-pre-anger-displeasure}

The lower bound of the continuum is occupied by two roots---\emph{ʾ-f-f}
and \emph{k-r-h}---that mark the \emph{pre-anger} register: vocal
irritation and inner aversion respectively.

\textbf{4.3.0. \emph{Uff} (3; all Meccan).} \emph{Uff} (gloss:
\emph{taḍajjur}, ``irritation'') is the Qur'anic register's lowest
verbal expression of displeasure. Its three attestations occupy
paradigmatic moral positions: Q. 17:23 prohibits saying \emph{uff} to
one's parents (the \emph{lowest} sign of displeasure morally weighted at
the \emph{highest} relational stake); Q. 21:67 places it in Abraham's
mouth confronting idol-worshippers (prophetic remonstrance); Q. 46:17
ascribes it to a rebellious child reproaching believing parents (the
inverse). \emph{Uff} anchors the lower boundary of the spectrum at a
register \emph{below} anger proper but already morally accountable.

\textbf{4.3.1. \emph{Karh} (41; 14M/27Md; raw \emph{p} = 0.005,
Holm-adj. 0.05).} \emph{Karh} exhibits a \emph{twofold structure}:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{(\alph{enumi})}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Inner \emph{karāha} (dislike)} --- pre-anger evaluative
  dislike, framed as \emph{reformable} by Q. 2:216 (\emph{ʿasā an
  takrahū shayʾan wa-huwa khayrun lakum}); the Stage-1 anger-spectrum
  sense. (b) \textbf{Structural \emph{ikrāh} (coercion)} --- not an
  emotion but a structural extension of distaste into compulsion (Q.
  2:256 \emph{lā ikrāha fī al-dīn}; Q. 16:106; Q. 24:33). We include
  \emph{karh} on the strength of its \emph{karāha} sense; \emph{ikrāh}
  is a structural cognate worth noting because it shares the root and
  instantiates a Stage-6-like behavioural extension of distaste.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{4.4. Stage 2 --- Inner pressure and
contraction}\label{stage-2-inner-pressure-and-contraction}

Stage 2 brings together three roots---\emph{ḍ-y-q}, \emph{ḥ-z-n},
\emph{ʾ-s-f}---with 60 combined attestations. Each marks a distinct
phenomenological mode within the inner-pressure family.

\textbf{4.4.1. \emph{Ḍīq} (13; 9M/4Md).} Ibn Fāris glosses \emph{ḍyq} as
``constriction, narrowness''; al-Rāghib in opposition to \emph{saʿah}
(expansion). The root appears predominantly in the construction
\emph{ḍīq al-ṣadr} (constriction of the chest), localising the
experience to the embodied site. Q. 15:97 frames it as the prophetic
response to social rejection --- an inner, bodily-localised pressure
that does not translate into outward aggression. Al-Ṭabāṭabāʾī
(\emph{al-Mīzān} 12:195) observes the verse expresses divine
\emph{knowledge} of prophetic \emph{ḍīq} rather than prohibition: a
phenomenologically natural, theologically licit state, not a moral
failing. Morphological profile: 8 verbs / 3 nouns / 1 participle ---
registering a transient, situationally-induced state.

\textbf{4.4.1.1. \emph{Ḍāqa dharʿan} --- inhibited aggression at Lot, Q.
11:77/80.} The compound idiom \emph{ḍāqa bihim dharʿan} (``his arm-span
tightened by them'') in the Lot narrative figures \emph{post-anger
blocked aggression} --- anger at action-threshold but structurally
inhibited. The four-step trajectory (trigger \emph{sīʾa bihim} →
constriction \emph{ḍyq+dharʿ} → aspirational voicing \emph{law anna lī
bikum quwwatan} → transfer of agency to the divine through the angels'
arrival) shows that \emph{ḍyq} sits at \emph{both} poles of the
spectrum: pre-anger contraction \emph{and} upper-pressure threshold of
inhibition. The Qur'anic narrative theologises inhibited aggression:
when the wronged agent lacks power, divine agency completes the anger
trajectory.

\textbf{4.4.2. \emph{Ḥuzn} (42; 26M/16Md).} The most frequent and most
verbal Stage-2 lexeme (37v / 5n = 88\% verbal). Ibn Manẓūr preserves the
etymologically primary ``rugged, uneven ground'' (\emph{al-arḍ
al-ġalīẓah}); the metaphorical extension figures grief as inner
roughness, anticipating Kövecses's EMOTION IS A LANDSCAPE
schema.\footnote{Kövecses, \emph{Metaphor and Emotion}.} Q. 2:38 places
\emph{ḥuzn} in syntagmatic pair with \emph{khawf} and opposes both to
divinely-given guidance --- \emph{ḥuzn} as the affective signature of
\emph{separation from guidance}; al-Ṭabāṭabāʾī (\emph{al-Mīzān} 1:146)
calls this foundational for \emph{ʿilm al-nafs al-Qur'anī}.
\textbf{Caveat (transversality)}: \emph{Ḥuzn} is \emph{not} strictly
anger-derived. It has four valences --- standalone grief over loss (Q.
12:84, Jacob over Joseph), externally-induced sorrow from being wronged
(prophetic grief at rejection), positive moral compunction (the
affective register of \emph{tawba}), and anger turned inward when
externally inhibited (closest to the Stage-2 placement here). Inclusion
is justified by corpus density and proximity of (b) and (d) to the anger
field, but the valence is more transversal than a single stage label
admits. The 88\% verbal profile reinforces the Stage-2 generalisation:
inner-pressure lexemes denote transient, regulable states.

\textbf{4.4.3. \emph{Asaf} (5; 5M/0Md).} The least frequent Stage-2
lexeme. Ibn Fāris and al-Rāghib gloss as the conjunction of grief and
anger (\emph{al-jamʿ bayna al-ḥuzn wa-l-ġaḍab}) --- at the threshold
between inner pressure and active anger. Q. 43:55 (\emph{fa-lammā
āsafūnā intaqamnā minhum}) frames \emph{asaf} as the immediate causal
precursor of divine retribution; al-Ṭabarsī (\emph{Majmaʿ} 9:82) glosses
\emph{al-asaf} here as ``the intense anger arising from prior grief.''
The exclusive-Meccan distribution (5/0; raw \emph{p} = 0.34, adj.
\emph{p} = 1.00) is suggestive but evidentially weak --- at \emph{n} = 5
the test has very low power. Across all five attestations, \emph{asaf}
occurs in narrative passages depicting prior prophets' encounter with
rejection and in the \emph{ghaḍbān asifan} compound applied to Moses
(§4.4.3.1). We register the 5/0 distribution as a \emph{contextual
observation} warranting follow-up at higher \emph{n} (e.g.~against the
\emph{ḥadīth} corpus), not as a confirmed empirical finding.

\textbf{4.4.3.1. \emph{Ghaḍbān asifan} --- sacred anger at Moses, Q.
7:150 / 20:86.} The compound \emph{ghaḍbān asifan}, applied to Moses on
his return to find the calf-worshippers, generates a three-layer
dialectic: \emph{ghaḍab} is outward/kinetic (throwing the tablets,
seizing Aaron); \emph{asaf} is inward/potential (compassionate grief
over the relapse). \emph{Asaf} grounds \emph{ghaḍab}: absent its
compassionate ground, Moses' anger would be violence; present, it is
anger in service of guidance. The lexicon's pairing in a single nominal
predication encodes moral-productivity-of-prophetic-anger as a
structural property, not as ad-hoc apologetics.

\textbf{4.4.4. Network evidence for Stage-2 coherence.} The aya-level
co-occurrence network (Figure 5) reveals that the strongest single edge
connects \emph{ḥzn} and \emph{ḍyq} (weight 3); these two lexemes share
three verses in the Qur'an, more than any other pair within the
spectrum. This empirical density provides independent verification of
the Stage-2 grouping. Centrality analysis (Figure 6) places \emph{ḥzn}
among the highest-degree nodes (weighted degree 5, tied with
\emph{ġḍb}).

\subsubsection{4.5. Stage 3 --- Evaluative
aversion}\label{stage-3-evaluative-aversion}

Stage 3 brings together three roots---\emph{n-q-m}, \emph{s-kh-ṭ},
\emph{m-q-t}---with 27 combined attestations. Each marks a distinct mode
of \emph{evaluative-aversive} response to a moral object.

\textbf{4.5.1. \emph{Naqm} (17 attestations; 12 Meccan / 5 Medinan).}
\emph{Naqm} is the lexeme of \emph{vengeful disapproval}: anger that is
grounded in moral evaluation and oriented toward retribution. Q. 7:126
(\emph{wa-mā naqamū minnā illā an āmannā bi-āyāti rabbinā}) is the
linguistic exemplar: anger directed \emph{at others' belief} ---
Pharaoh's sorcerers, on accepting Moses' message, are described as
suffering Pharaonic \emph{naqm} precisely \emph{because} they believed.
The grammatical construction \emph{naqamū min} + (cause) is a canonical
Qur'anic formula for evaluatively-grounded anger.

The lexeme also generates \emph{al-muntaqim} (``the Avenger'') as a
divine attribute (Q. 32:22 and parallels), establishing a direct
theological-lexical bridge from the human-affective register to the
divine retributive register. Position on the spectrum: \emph{naqm} sits
between \emph{sakhaṭ} and \emph{ghaḍab} on the action axis. It is more
action-oriented than \emph{sakhaṭ} (which is a stable moral
disapprobation) but less kinetic than \emph{ghaḍab} (which mobilises the
motor agent). The Meccan tilt (12/5) is consistent with the lexeme's
frequent appearance in narrative-prophetic passages (the Pharaonic
sorcerers, the people of Lot, the people of Madyan).

\textbf{4.5.2. \emph{Sakhaṭ} (4 attestations; 0 Meccan / 4 Medinan).}
Despite its low frequency, \emph{sakhaṭ} furnishes one of the paper's
principal distributional findings. The exclusive-Medinan
distribution---zero Meccan attestations among four total---has a
two-sided exact binomial p-value of \textbf{0.005} against the
corpus-derived baseline of 0.74 Meccan probability, statistically
significant at α = 0.05.

\textbf{\emph{Baseline derivation, made explicit.}} The 0.74 Meccan-rate
baseline is the proportion of \emph{ayat} (verses, not suras) classified
Meccan in the QAC sura metadata, computed as
\texttt{meccan\_ayat\ ÷\ total\_ayat} weighted by \texttt{total\_verses}
per sura. We use the ayat-weighted rate rather than the sura-count rate
(\textasciitilde0.69) because an attestation drawn at uniform random
from the corpus hits an aya, not a sura; the longer Medinan suras
inflate the Medinan share at the aya level. The implementation is in
\texttt{analysis/advanced\_metrics.py}
(\texttt{\_compute\_meccan\_baseline}).

is mildly sensitive to the baseline. We report the two-sided exact

\emph{sakhaṭ} adjusted \emph{p} = 0.05 (raw 0.005) --- borderline rather

tied) tilts do not survive correction. With \emph{n} = 4 the test is
underpowered; the substantive claim rests on the convergence of
distributional evidence with the contextual-tafsir reading. All four
\emph{sakhaṭ} attestations are tied to the \emph{Munāfiqūn} --- Q. 47:28
(\emph{ittabaʿū mā askhaṭa Allāh wa-karihū riḍwānahu}) is canonical.
Following Narimani, Iqbali and Chari,\footnote{Narimani, Iqbali and
  Chari, `Semantic Re-analysis'.} we read \emph{sakhaṭ} not as an
instantaneous emotional surge but as sustained \emph{ethical
disapprobation}; we propose:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Hypothesis (Discursive Specialisation of \emph{sakhaṭ}):}
\emph{sakhaṭ} operates as a discursively specialised lexeme, reserved
for divine displeasure with the \emph{Munāfiqūn} --- agents whose
\emph{zāhir} departs from their \emph{bāṭin}. Since the \emph{Munāfiqūn}
category is constitutively a Medinan-period phenomenon, \emph{sakhaṭ}
attests exclusively in Medinan suras. This is one instance of a general
phenomenon of \emph{discursive-contextual lexical specialisation} in the
Qur'anic register.
\end{quote}

\textbf{4.5.3. \emph{Maqt} (6 attestations; 4 Meccan / 2 Medinan).}
\emph{Maqt} is \emph{intense moral aversion}: the four lexica converge
on \emph{ashaddu al-ibghāḍ} (Zajjāj/al-Layth in \emph{Tahdhīb
al-Lugha}); al-Rāghib grounds it specifically in observation of a
reprehensible act (\emph{ʿan amrin qabīḥin rakibahu}), making
\emph{maqt} purely evaluative. Two contrasts with \emph{ghaḍab}:
causation (purely evaluative vs.~injury/frustration-triggered); action
profile (rejection-distance vs.~approach-aggression). Canonical
exemplars --- Q. 40:10 (\emph{maqt al-nafs} doubled), Q. 4:22
(institutional moral aversion in forbidden marriages), Q. 35:39 (rising
divine \emph{maqt} against disbelievers), Q. 61:3 (saying-without-doing)
--- stabilise the lexeme as the Qur'anic register's term for moral
aversion as evaluative response, distinct from the kinetic-motor
\emph{ghaḍab} of Stage 4.

\subsubsection{4.6. Stage 4 --- Active
anger}\label{stage-4-active-anger}

Stage 4 (25 attestations) covers active, motor-mobilising anger.

\textbf{4.6.1. \emph{Ghaḍab} (24; 13M/11M).} The central anger lexeme of
the Qur'an, with near-balanced Meccan/Medinan distribution marking it as
the unmarked default. The four lexica gloss it as inner agitation of the
heart oriented toward retribution (al-Rāghib) or inner motion disposing
the agent to action (Ibn Fāris). Predicated balance-fully of God (Q.
48:6), prophets, and believers. Morphologically nominal (16 n / 6 v / 2
ptcp = 67\% nominal vs \emph{ḥuzn}'s 88\% verbal): \emph{ghaḍab} is a
\emph{structural concept-state} with organisational ontological standing
rather than a transient affect. Al-Fakhr al-Rāzī's \emph{Mafātīḥ
al-Ghayb} glosses divine \emph{ghaḍab} as \emph{irādat inzāl al-ʿiqāb}
--- a normative-juridical orientation, not an emotional response.
Network analysis places \emph{ghḍb} among the top three for betweenness
centrality (point estimate 0.17), with notable edges to \emph{ġyẓ}
(upward transition to S5), \emph{ḥzn} (downward S2 link), and \emph{bġy}
(S6 link).

\textbf{4.6.3. \emph{Ḥard} (Q. 68:25, hapax).} A Qur'anic hapax in the
Garden-Owners parable (\emph{wa-ghadaw ʿalā ḥardin qādirīn}):
garden-owners who vow in the night to harvest before dawn so as to deny
the poor their share. Classical exegesis converges on a dual gloss:
al-Ṭabarsī's \emph{Majmaʿ al-Bayān} reads \emph{ḥard} as both
\emph{ghaḍab} (anger) and \emph{qaṣd} (purposive intent);
al-Ṭabāṭabāʾī's \emph{al-Mīzān} refines as anger-plus-deliberate-denial
--- purposive anger weaponised through resource-denial. \emph{Ghaḍab}
mobilises the motor agent outward; \emph{ḥard} mobilises toward
deliberate, miserly withholding --- anger that strips the world of what
it is owed. The narrative consequence is the destruction of the garden
by night-storm: divine retribution against the \emph{ḥard}-driven plan
to deny. A single attestation thus serves as linguistic anchor for an
entire moral-psychological category.

\subsubsection{4.7. Stage 5 --- Compressed/explosive
rage}\label{stage-5-compressedexplosive-rage}

\textbf{4.7.1. \emph{Ghayẓ} and the \emph{kaẓm} metaphor (11
attestations; 3M/8Md).} The four lexica converge: \emph{ghayẓ} is
``compressed contained anger'' (al-Rāghib), ``the highest concentration
of restrained anger'' (Mostafavi), ``filling of the self with restrained
anger'' (Ibn Manẓūr) --- pressurised contents of a sealed vessel. Q.
3:134 commends \emph{al-kāẓimīn al-ghayẓ}; the verb \emph{kaẓm} derives
from the act of tying off a waterskin, the agent-internal regulation
that sustains containment. Read through Lakoff and Kövecses,\footnote{Lakoff
  and Kövecses, `Cognitive Model of Anger'.} the \emph{ghayẓ--kaẓm} pair
instantiates ANGER IS A PRESSURIZED CONTAINER with precision:
\emph{ghayẓ} the contained fluid, \emph{kaẓm} the seal (Figure 7). The
Medinan tilt (8/3) tracks the discursive context: inner anger management
becomes a salient pedagogical concern in the formative Medinan community
facing internal \emph{Munāfiq} and external Quraysh hostility.

\textbf{4.7.2. \emph{Tamayyuz}: container collapse in Q. 67:8
(manifestation, not separate root).} Q. 67:8 --- \emph{takādu tamayyazu
min al-ġayẓ} (``{[}the fire{]} is on the verge of bursting apart from
rage'') --- is the single most articulated image of rage in the Qur'an.
\emph{Tamayyaza} (root \emph{m-y-z}: to part, sever) figures not the
\emph{exit} of pressurised content (as in English \emph{she blew her
top}, \emph{he exploded}, \emph{I lost it}) but the \emph{structural
integrity of the container itself failing}. We treat \emph{tamayyuz} as
a manifestation of Stage 5 rather than a distinct core root, retaining
the fourteen-root inventory. H5 is corroborated in the form: Q. 67:8
instantiates the container-collapse failure mode with greater lexical
transparency than the English exemplars in Lakoff and Kövecses's
corpus;\footnote{Lakoff and Kövecses, `Cognitive Model of Anger'.}
whether this is the strongest historical instance is left open pending
the cross-cultural philological survey we commend in §5.5.

\subsubsection{4.8. Stage 6 --- Behavioural outcomes (with
caveats)}\label{stage-6-behavioural-outcomes-with-caveats}

\textbf{4.8.1. \emph{Baghy} (96; 55M/41Md).} The most frequent lexeme of
the spectrum and the principal bridge node by point-estimate centrality.
Glossed as ``seeking beyond rightful bounds'' (al-Rāghib) and
``aggressive seeking with intent to oppress'' (Mostafavi). Q. 42:42
collocates \emph{baghy} with \emph{ẓulm}, embedding it in the
moral-evaluation register. Ibn ʿĀshūr clarifies (\emph{al-Taḥrīr}
25:126): \emph{baghy} and \emph{ẓulm} are near-synonymous, except
\emph{baghy} is specific to non-rightful aggression against another
while \emph{ẓulm} covers all injustice.

\textbf{4.8.2. \emph{Baghy} as bridge node (with bootstrap
uncertainty).} \emph{Baghy} attains the highest point-estimate closeness
centrality (0.55) and is tied with \emph{ghaḍab} (0.53) and \emph{asaf}
(0.49) for the highest betweenness centrality (0.15). Bootstrap 95\%
percentile CIs (1,000 ayat-resamples) are wide --- for \emph{baghy}:
betweenness ∈ {[}0.00, 0.31{]}; closeness ∈ {[}0.23, 0.84{]};
eigenvector ∈ {[}0.00, 0.68{]} --- reflecting the small graph (14 nodes)
and modest edge density. On strict non-overlap, no node's centrality is
significantly greater than another's. The bridging-role claim must
therefore be qualified: \emph{baghy} leads on three of four
point-estimate measures, but the bootstrap does not rule out
alternatives. What is \emph{robust} at the aggregate level is the
cross-field connectivity to the umbrella moral terms (Table 4):
\emph{baghy} shows 16 aya-level co-occurrences with
\emph{ẓlm/jrm/fsq/ʿdw/šnʾ} combined (8 with \emph{ʿdw}, 4 with
\emph{ẓlm}), an integer-count pattern independent of centrality ranking.
H4 is \emph{consistent with} point estimates, qualified by bootstrap.

congruent with Lazarus\footnote{Richard S. Lazarus, \emph{Emotion and
  Adaptation} (New York: Oxford University Press, 1991).} and
Scherer,\footnote{Klaus R. Scherer, `Appraisal Considered as a Process
  of Multilevel Sequential Checking', in Klaus R. Scherer, Angela Schorr
  and Tom Johnstone (eds), \emph{Appraisal Processes in Emotion: Theory,
  Methods, Research} (New York: Oxford University Press, 2001),
  pp.~92-120; idem, `What Are Emotions? And How Can They Be Measured?',
  \emph{Social Science Information} 44:4 (2005), pp.~695-729.} and
aligning with Roseman's\footnote{Ira J. Roseman, `Appraisal Determinants
  of Emotions: Constructing a More Accurate and Comprehensive Theory',
  \emph{Cognition and Emotion} 10:3 (1996), pp.~241-277.} ``agency-self
/ motive-inconsistent / high-control'' pattern. Qarizadeh et al.'s
polysemy analysis\footnote{Qarizadeh et al., `Polysemy of
  \emph{ṭughyān}'.} notes the cognitive structure; we identify
\emph{ṭughyān} as the cognitive-appraisal node of Stage 6.

\textbf{4.8.4. \emph{ʿUtuww} (10 attestations; 9 Meccan / 1 Medinan).}
The most extreme lexeme --- ``obstinate excess'' (Ibn Manẓūr), ``extreme
self-aggrandisement in disobedience'' (al-Rāghib). Q. 25:21 collocates
\emph{ʿatū ʿutuwwan kabīrā} with \emph{istakbarū fī anfusihim}:
rebellion crystallised into existential posture. Strong Meccan
distribution (9/1; raw \emph{p} = 0.06; Holm-adjusted \emph{p} = 1.00)
is consistent with its rhetorical function as terminal designation for
Nūḥ-, ʿĀd-, Thamūd-, and Pharaonic-people narratives, but does not
survive FDR correction.

\textbf{4.8.5. Bidirectional causation and a tafsir-grounded audit.}
\emph{Baghy} arises frequently from greed and \emph{ʿulw fī al-arḍ}
(cf.~Q. 6:21); \emph{ṭughyān} --- paradigmatically Pharaonic (Q. 79:17)
--- is rooted in structural arrogance, with Q. 26:55 (\emph{innahum lanā
la-ghāʾiẓūn}) reversing polarity to show oppressor-anger arising
\emph{from} the rebellion of the oppressed; \emph{ʿutuww} attaches to
\emph{istikbār} as settled character. The causation arrow is therefore
\emph{bidirectional}. To operationalise this, each of the 145 Stage-6
attestations was coded against the joint reading of \emph{al-Mīzān},
\emph{al-Kashshāf}, \emph{Majmaʿ al-Bayān}, and \emph{al-Taḥrīr
wa-l-Tanwīr}: \textbf{A} (anger-derived), \textbf{S} (structurally
derived: greed/\emph{istikbār}/\emph{ʿulw} without explicit anger
antecedent), or \textbf{A+S} (joint). Coding by the corresponding author
with second-coder verification on a 20\% subsample (κ = 0.79; full
coding at \texttt{data/concordance/stage6\_causation\_coding.csv}).
Results: A = 38 (26\%), S = 71 (49\%), A+S = 36 (25\%); by root,
\emph{baghy} 22A/50S/24A+S, \emph{ṭughyān} 11A/19S/9A+S, \emph{ʿutuww}
5A/2S/3A+S. Re-running χ²(1) on A-only (Stages 1--5 = 167
vs.~anger-derived Stage-6 = 38) yields χ²(1) = 81.4, \emph{p}
\textless{} 0.001 --- the phenomenological core dominates anger-derived
outcomes \textasciitilde4.4×. The full-Stage-6 near-balance (χ²(1) =
1.55) is therefore explained: S-attestations belong to an adjacent
moral-anthropological field of \emph{ẓulm-istikbār-ʿulw} that the corpus
pervasively documents, and \emph{baghy}/\emph{ṭughyān}/\emph{ʿutuww} are
not coextensive with ``anger outcomes'' in the strict sense.

\subsection{5. Discussion}\label{discussion}

\subsubsection{5.1. The integrated logic of
escalation}\label{the-integrated-logic-of-escalation}

The findings cohere into a single structural claim: the Qur'anic lexicon
of the anger spectrum encodes a \textbf{logic of anger escalation} that
operates simultaneously along three dimensions---locus (inner → outer),
intensity (low → high), and scope (individual → structural). The six
stages instantiate this trifold transformation. The lexemes are not
synonyms; they are \emph{grades}, ordered by an intelligible cognitive
logic.

In compact form: \emph{Pre-anger displeasure → Inner pressure →
Evaluative aversion → Active anger → Compressed rage → Behavioural
outcomes (with caveats)}. The arrows index not mere temporal succession
but conditional intensification at each step where regulation may
succeed or fail. The Stage 6 step is, however, distinctive: the move
into behavioural outcomes is \emph{not} a deterministic escalation from
anger but is influenced by independent vectors (greed, \emph{istikbār},
structural arrogance), which is why we read the near-balance between
Stages 1--5 (167) and Stage 6 (145) as theoretically informative rather
than paradoxical.

\subsubsection{5.2. Convergence with contemporary emotion
psychology}\label{convergence-with-contemporary-emotion-psychology}

The Qur'anic six-stage continuum maps onto four families of contemporary
emotion theory at points of structural rather than psychometric
convergence --- sufficient to ground the Islamic-psychology implications
of §5.7 and to identify where the Qur'anic lexicon \emph{extends} the
contemporary models. The mapping operates at three levels: Gross's
process model, Spielberger's STAXI and Plutchik's intensity wheel, and
the appraisal-theoretic apparatus of Lazarus, Scherer, and Roseman.

\textbf{Gross's process model.} Gross (1998, 2014, 2015)\footnote{James
  J. Gross, `The Emerging Field of Emotion Regulation: An Integrative
  Review', \emph{Review of General Psychology} 2:3 (1998), pp.~271-299;
  idem (ed.), \emph{Handbook of Emotion Regulation}, 2nd edn (New York:
  Guilford Press, 2014); idem, `Emotion Regulation: Current Status and
  Future Prospects', \emph{Psychological Inquiry} 26:1 (2015), pp.~1-26.}
partitions emotion regulation into five sequential strategies along the
developmental trajectory of an emotion: \emph{situation selection},
\emph{situation modification}, \emph{attentional deployment},
\emph{cognitive change}, and \emph{response modulation}. The Qur'anic
continuum partitions the trajectory more finely on the affective side:
\textbf{S1} (\emph{uff}, \emph{karāha}) ↔ \emph{situation selection} ---
the threshold of withdrawal, where Q. 17:23 forbids even \emph{uff} to
one's parents; \textbf{S2} (\emph{ḍīq}, \emph{ḥuzn}, \emph{asaf}) ↔
\emph{situation modification}, with \emph{ḍīq al-ṣadr} localising the
regulatory turn inward; \textbf{S3} (\emph{naqm}, \emph{sakhaṭ},
\emph{maqt}) ↔ \emph{attentional deployment} and \emph{cognitive change}
--- the evaluative-aversive lexemes encoding cognitive reappraisal under
social-religious norms; \textbf{S4} (\emph{ghaḍab}, \emph{ḥard}) ↔
\emph{response selection}, the kinetic-motor enactment in retributive or
denial-of-resource directions; \textbf{S5} (\emph{ghayẓ}/\emph{kaẓm};
\emph{tamayyuz}) ↔ \emph{response modulation}, with \emph{kaẓm}
operationalising agent-internal suppression and \emph{tamayyuz} (Q.
67:8) its container-collapse failure mode. \textbf{S6} (\emph{baghy},
\emph{ṭughyān}, \emph{ʿutuww}) lies beyond Gross's frame: process-model
regulation concerns the \emph{emotional episode}, but Stage 6
lexicalises \emph{structural-moral pathologies of personhood} outliving
any single episode --- with the §4.8.5 caveat that Stage 6 has
bidirectional causation with the spectrum.

\textbf{Plutchik's intensity wheel; Spielberger's STAXI architecture.}
Plutchik\footnote{Robert Plutchik, \emph{Emotion: A Psychoevolutionary
  Synthesis} (New York: Harper \& Row, 1980); idem, `The Nature of
  Emotions', \emph{American Scientist} 89:4 (2001), pp.~344-350.}
organises anger on a three-level escalation (\emph{annoyance → anger →
rage}); the Qur'anic continuum \emph{refines} this by partitioning
\emph{annoyance} into vocal irritation (\emph{uff}, S1), inner aversion
(\emph{karāha}, S1), and inner pressure (\emph{ḍīq}, \emph{ḥuzn},
\emph{asaf}, S2) --- three Qur'anic stages where Plutchik has one label
--- and by inserting the \emph{compressed-rage} register (\emph{ghayẓ},
S5) between \emph{anger} (≈ S4 \emph{ghaḍab}) and the unmarked terminal
\emph{rage} (closest analogue: \emph{tamayyuz} at Q. 67:8).
Spielberger's STAXI-2\footnote{Charles D. Spielberger,
  \emph{Professional Manual for the State-Trait Anger Expression
  Inventory--2 (STAXI-2)} (Odessa, FL: Psychological Assessment
  Resources, 1999).} further partitions anger into \emph{trait}
(dispositional propensity), \emph{state} (situational episode), and
\emph{control} (subdivided into \emph{anger-in}, \emph{anger-out},
\emph{anger-control}). The Qur'anic lexicon realises this trichotomy at
the lexical surface: trait-like sustained states (\emph{ḥuzn} at 88\%
verbal registering recurrent affective episodes, \emph{ḍīq},
\emph{karāha}); state-like situational responses (\emph{sakhaṭ} as
discursively-specialised divine displeasure toward the \emph{Munāfiqūn},
\emph{maqt} as evaluative-aversive response to an observed \emph{qabīḥ},
\emph{ghaḍab} as the unmarked anger-episode); and \emph{kaẓm} (Q. 3:134)
as the Qur'anic \emph{anger-control} lexeme, operationally close to
Spielberger's \emph{anger-in} / suppression construct with the addition
of explicit moral commendation.

\textbf{Appraisal theory: Scherer's checks and Roseman's
\emph{ṭughyān}-pattern.} Appraisal theory (Frijda → Lazarus → Scherer →
Roseman)\footnote{Nico H. Frijda, \emph{The Emotions}, Studies in
  Emotion and Social Interaction (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press,
  1986); Lazarus, \emph{Emotion and Adaptation}; Roseman, `Appraisal
  Determinants'; Scherer, `Appraisal Considered as a Process'; idem,
  `What Are Emotions?'.} holds that emotional response is determined not
by stimulus alone but by cognitive \emph{appraisal} along a defined set
of dimensions. Scherer's component-process model\footnote{Scherer,
  `Appraisal Considered as a Process', pp.~92-120; idem, `What Are
  Emotions?', pp.~695-729.} specifies four sequential checks
(\emph{relevance}, \emph{implications}, \emph{coping potential},
\emph{normative significance}); the Qur'anic Stage-3 lexemes operate as
a lexicon for the normative-significance check at progressively higher
intensities (\emph{naqm} → \emph{sakhaṭ} → \emph{maqt}). Roseman's
appraisal grid\footnote{Roseman, `Appraisal Determinants', pp.~241-277.}
orders emotions by five dimensions (\emph{situational state},
\emph{motivational state}, \emph{probability}, \emph{agency},
\emph{control potential}). Q. 96:6--7 (\emph{kallā inna al-insāna
la-yaṭghā · an raʾāhu staġnā}) conditions \emph{ṭughyān} on the
appraisal of \emph{istighnāʾ}: the agent perceives high coping
potential, low external constraint, and self-agency, producing the
\emph{agency-self / motive-consistent / high-control} pattern Roseman
would predict for defiance / contempt. The Qur'anic \emph{ṭughyān} is,
on this reading, a textbook instance of Roseman's appraisal pattern
lexicalised under a single root in the seventh century that
twentieth-century cognitive appraisal theory has independently
re-derived. The Qur'anic lexicon thus encodes --- as architectural
design rather than theoretical statement --- insights contemporary
emotion science has reached independently.

\subsubsection{5.3. The phenomenology/outcomes
balance}\label{the-phenomenologyoutcomes-balance}

A naive ten-root reading once suggested 2.4× more attestations at the
behavioural pole than at Stages 1--3 combined, indicating discursive
over-emphasis on outcomes. The revised fourteen-root inventory (167 /
145; χ²(1) = 1.55, fail to reject) tells a different story: the expanded
phenomenological vocabulary (\emph{uff}, \emph{karh}, \emph{naqm},
\emph{maqt}, \emph{ḥard}) brings the lower stages into
corpus-distributional balance with Stage 6, exactly as one would expect
if (i) the phenomenological side is richer than the prior analysis
allowed, and (ii) Stage-6 lexemes are not exclusively anger-derived but
stand in bidirectional causal relation to anger. The methodological
lesson: corpus-distributional analyses of religious texts are maps of
\emph{discursive emphasis}, fundamentally entangled with
category-membership and category-causation decisions; the 10/4 → 14/6
shift changed both the empirical pattern and its interpretation.

\subsubsection{5.4. Discursive-contextual
specialisation}\label{discursive-contextual-specialisation}

The FDR-surviving Medinan exclusivity of \emph{sakhaṭ}, alongside the
contextual-only Meccan tilts of \emph{asaf}, \emph{naqm}, and
\emph{ʿutuww}, the hapax-Meccan \emph{ḥard}, the Medinan tilt of
\emph{karh}, and the strong umbrella patterns \emph{jrm} (Meccan) and
\emph{fsq} (Medinan), instantiate \textbf{discursive-contextual
specialisation}: members of multi-lexeme semantic fields preferentially
attaching to specific discursive registers. The pattern is not random
--- \emph{sakhaṭ} attaches to the \emph{Munāfiqūn} (a Medinan
phenomenon), \emph{asaf} to prior-prophet narratives
(Meccan-rhetorical), \emph{jrm} to anti-\emph{shirk} polemic (Meccan),
\emph{fsq} to intra-community discipline (Medinan). El-Awa on textual
relations\footnote{Salwa M. S. El-Awa, \emph{Textual Relations in the
  Qurʾan: Relevance, Coherence and Structure}, Routledge Studies in the
  Qurʾan (London: Routledge, 2006).} and Sinai on Meccan/Medinan
diachrony\footnote{Nicolai Sinai, \emph{The Qurʾan: A
  Historical-Critical Introduction}, The New Edinburgh Islamic Surveys
  (Edinburgh: Edinburgh University Press, 2017); idem, \emph{Key Terms
  of the Qurʾan: A Critical Dictionary} (Princeton: Princeton University
  Press, 2023).} supply the theoretical framework: specialisation is
exactly the kind of pattern one would expect from the Qur'an's gradual
response to evolving sociolinguistic contexts. The phenomenon is, to our
knowledge, undocumented as a systematic corpus-distributional pattern in
Qur'anic-linguistic scholarship; future work should test the
generalisation across other multi-lexeme fields, in dialogue with
QurAna/QurSim\footnote{Abdul-Baquee M. Sharaf and Eric Atwell, `QurAna:
  Corpus of the Quran Annotated with Pronominal Anaphora', in
  \emph{Proceedings of the Eighth International Conference on Language
  Resources and Evaluation (LREC'12)} (Istanbul: ELRA, 2012),
  pp.~130-137; and idem, `QurSim: A Corpus for Evaluation of Relatedness
  in Short Texts', in the same volume, pp.~2295-2302.} and the
QuranMorph\footnote{Diyam Akra, Tymaa Hammouda and Mustafa Jarrar,
  \emph{QuranMorph: Morphologically Annotated Quranic Corpus}, arXiv
  preprint 2506.18148 (2025), https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.18148.} and
MASAQ\footnote{Majdi Sawalha et al., `Morphologically-Analyzed and
  Syntactically-Annotated Quran Dataset (MASAQ)', \emph{Data in Brief}
  58 (2024), 111211, DOI: 10.1016/j.dib.2024.111211.} extensions of the
Dukes corpus.

\subsubsection{5.5. Q. 67:8 and the container metaphor: a focused
comparative
survey}\label{q.-678-and-the-container-metaphor-a-focused-comparative-survey}

Lakoff and Kövecses's 1987 chapter in Holland and Quinn's \emph{Cultural
Models}\footnote{Lakoff and Kövecses, `Cognitive Model of Anger',
  pp.~195-221.} reconstructed the conceptual model underlying
American-English anger metaphors as a \emph{four-step trajectory} on the
PRESSURIZED CONTAINER schema: (i) anger is a hot fluid in a container
(pp.~197-198; \emph{filled with anger}, \emph{contain her rage}); (ii)
intensity raises the pressure (pp.~198-200; \emph{bursting with anger},
\emph{about to explode}); (iii) containment attempts (pp.~200-202;
\emph{keep it in}, \emph{bottle it up}); (iv) container failure
(pp.~202-205; \emph{exploded}, \emph{blew his top}, \emph{flipped his
lid}) --- in which the English exemplars lexicalise the failure-mode as
the \emph{exit of contents} with the container preserved or only
metonymically displaced.

The Qur'anic \emph{ghayẓ--kaẓm--tamayyuz} triad walks through these four
steps with a precision the English exemplars do not match at step (iv).
At step (i), the four authoritative lexica gloss \emph{ghayẓ} as
compressed contained anger (al-Rāghib: \emph{ashadd al-ġaḍab};
al-Mostafawī: \emph{takāthuf al-iḍṭirāb al-shadīd al-mukbūt}). At step
(ii), the syntagm \emph{imtilāʾ al-nafs bi-l-ġayẓ} encodes the
rising-pressure step morphologically. At step (iii), \emph{kaẓm} (Q.
3:134 \emph{al-kāẓimīn al-ġayẓ}) --- etymologically the tying-off of a
waterskin --- names the agent-internal suppression operation in a single
lexical operation, where Lakoff and Kövecses (p.~201) require \emph{the
lid}, \emph{the cork}, \emph{the cap}, \emph{the bottle stopper} as four
separate metaphors. At step (iv), the divergence is decisive: Q. 67:8
(\emph{takādu tamayyazu min al-ġayẓ}) lexicalises \emph{the parting /
severing of the vessel itself} --- \emph{tamayyaza} deriving from
\emph{m-y-z} ``to part, to sever'' (Lisān s.v.) --- figuring the
fire-vessel of Jahannam \emph{bursting apart structurally} from the rage
of its contents, not the exit of contents from an intact vessel. The
English-language step (iv) holds the container constant and reports the
trajectory of contents; the Qur'anic step (iv) holds the rage constant
and reports the trajectory of \emph{the container}.

To situate the strength of this claim, we conducted a focused
philological survey across six pre-modern traditions whose anger-lexica
have been studied through the conceptual-metaphor lens. (1)
\textbf{Akkadian} anchors anger in the \emph{libbu} (heart/abdomen) and
\emph{kabattu} (liver) as filled containers, paradigmatically in the
idiom \emph{libbāti malû} ``to be filled with rage'',\footnote{\emph{Chicago
  Assyrian Dictionary}, vol.~L (Chicago: Oriental Institute, 1973),
  p.~164; D. Bodi, `An Akkadian-Aramaic Idiomatic Expression in Ezekiel
  16:30 \emph{amūlâ libbātēk} ``I am filled with anger against you,''
  and Remarks on the Languages in Persian Times', \emph{Transeuphratène}
  50 (2018), pp.~13-38.} with cognate stative verbs \emph{ezēzu} ``to be
furious'' and the abstract \emph{uzzatu} ``wrath''; recent
bodily-mapping work on Neo-Assyrian texts\footnote{J. Lauri, S. Svärd,
  T. Alstola, H. Jauhiainen, A. Sahala, K. Lindén, M. Sams and L.
  Nummenmaa, `Embodied Emotions in Ancient Neo-Assyrian Texts Revealed
  by Bodily Mapping of Emotional Semantics', \emph{iScience} 27:12
  (2024), 111365, DOI: 10.1016/j.isci.2024.111365.} confirms a
dominantly FILLING / HEAT-IN-CONTAINER schema and finds no idiom of
vessel rupture. (2) \textbf{Biblical Hebrew} centres on \emph{ḥēmâ}
(heat/fury, frequently the object of \emph{šāphakh} ``to pour out'' ---
a CONTENTS-EXIT image), \emph{ʾaph} (literally ``nose''), and the
formulaic \emph{ḥarôn-ʾaph} ``burning of the nose''; Kruger\footnote{P.
  A. Kruger, `A Cognitive Interpretation of the Emotion of Anger in the
  Hebrew Bible', \emph{Journal of Northwest Semitic Languages} 26:1
  (2000), pp.~181-193; idem, `Emotions in the Hebrew Bible: A Few
  Observations on Prospects and Challenges', \emph{Old Testament Essays}
  28:2 (2015), pp.~395-420.} demonstrates that ANGER IS FLUID IN A
CONTAINER and ANGER IS FIRE IN THE NOSE are the dominant conceptual
metaphors of the Hebrew Bible, both schematically EXIT-typed rather than
vessel-collapsing. The instructive exception is \textbf{Job 32:19},
where Elihu, possessed by anger, declares his belly to be
``\emph{kĕ-yayin lōʾ-yippātēaḥ \ldots{} yibbāqēaʿ}'' --- ``like wine
that is not opened \ldots{} about to burst (lit. \emph{be split}) like
new wineskins'': the verb \emph{bāqaʿ} names exactly the structural
failure of the vessel that Q. 67:8's \emph{tamayyaza} names, and we
therefore qualify our claim accordingly --- Q. 67:8 is \emph{an early
and unusually transparent} lexicalisation of container collapse, not a
unique one. (3) \textbf{Homeric Greek} uses \emph{kholos} (bile/gall, a
fluid that \emph{rises} --- \emph{kholos \ldots{} anēi}, \emph{Iliad}
9.646) and \emph{mēnis} (ritualised, suprapersonal wrath);\footnote{Leonard
  C. Muellner, \emph{The Anger of Achilles: Mēnis in Greek Epic}
  (Ithaca, NY: Cornell University Press, 1996).} Cairns\footnote{Douglas
  L. Cairns, `Ethics, Ethology, Terminology: Iliadic Anger and the
  Cross-cultural Study of Emotion', in S. Braund and G. W. Most (eds),
  \emph{Ancient Anger: Perspectives from Homer to Galen}, Yale Classical
  Studies 32 (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2003), pp.~11-49.}
reads both as fluid-and-heat schemas, with no Iliadic image of the body
or its containing organ being torn apart by anger --- the closest
analogue, \emph{thumos} welling up to the \emph{phrenes}, remains within
the EXIT register. (4) \textbf{Vedic and Classical Sanskrit} lexicalise
anger principally as \emph{krodha} (with the recurrent compound
\emph{krodha-agni} ``fire of anger'') and \emph{manyu} (martial wrath,
often weaponised as Indra's bolt: ṚV 10.83--84, the
\emph{Manyu-Sūkta});\footnote{Stephanie W. Jamison and Joel P. Brereton,
  \emph{The Rigveda: The Earliest Religious Poetry of India}, 3 vols.
  (New York: Oxford University Press, 2014), vol.~III for translation of
  and commentary on the \emph{Manyu-Sūkta}, ṚV 10.83-84.} the dominant
images are FIRE and WEAPON-RELEASE, not vessel-disintegration. (5)
\textbf{Classical and Modern Chinese} lexicalises anger through
\emph{nù} (怒), \emph{fèn} (愤), and \emph{qì} (氣), with Yu\footnote{Ning
  Yu, `Metaphorical Expressions of Anger and Happiness in English and
  Chinese', \emph{Metaphor and Symbolic Activity} 10:2 (1995),
  pp.~59-92, DOI: 10.1207/s15327868ms1002\_1.} and Kövecses, Benczes and
Szelid\footnote{Kövecses, Benczes and Szelid (eds), \emph{Metaphors of
  ANGER}.} identifying ANGER IS HOT \emph{QÌ} IN A CONTAINER as the
central schema; idioms of overflow (\emph{qì-chōng} ``\emph{qì} surges
out'') elaborate exit, not vessel-rupture. (6) \textbf{Pre-Islamic
\emph{Jāhilī} Arabic} already deploys \emph{ḥamiya} ``to be heated'' and
\emph{ġalā} ``to boil'' for hostile arousal, alongside \emph{ġayẓ} in
the cognate fluid-heat register; the lexical groundwork for Q. 67:8 is
thus genuinely Arabian, not a Qur'anic neologism.

\textbf{On Kövecses, Benczes, and Szelid (2025).} The two-volume
\emph{Metaphors of ANGER across Languages: Universality and
Variation}\footnote{Kövecses, Benczes and Szelid (eds), \emph{Metaphors
  of ANGER}.} is the most ambitious cross-cultural survey of
anger-metaphor since Lakoff and Kövecses (1987) and supplies the proper
benchmark for any new claim of cross-cultural variation in container
metaphors. The encyclopedia spans twenty-two languages across volumes 1
and 2, with extended treatments of Mandarin Chinese, Japanese, Korean,
Hungarian, Polish, Russian, Spanish, German, Wolof, Hausa, Yoruba, and
Modern Standard Arabic (the last covered in a single chapter on
present-day MSA expressions), but it \emph{does not include a dedicated
treatment of Classical Arabic or of Qur'anic anger imagery}. The
Qur'anic \emph{ghayẓ--kaẓm--tamayyuz} triad, and Q. 67:8 in particular,
appear nowhere in the two volumes. The encyclopedia's editorial framing
(Kövecses's introduction to vol.~1) explicitly invites comparative work
in traditions not represented; the present analysis can be read as a
direct response to that invitation, supplying the Classical-Arabic and
Qur'anic data that the 2025 encyclopedia leaves un-canvassed and
contributing one new datum --- Q. 67:8 --- to the cross-cultural
inventory of step-(iv) container-failure lexicalisations.

\textbf{Net assessment.} Five of the six traditions surveyed here
concentrate anger-imagery around \emph{filling}, \emph{heating},
\emph{fluid-rising}, and \emph{exit / overflow}; the \textbf{Hebrew
Bible alone} offers a clear pre-Qur'anic parallel to vessel-rupture
proper (Job 32:19, \emph{bāqaʿ}). We accordingly revise our claim from
any unqualified uniqueness to \textbf{``an early and unusually
transparent''} lexicalisation of container collapse, with Job 32:19
acknowledged as a structurally cognate Northwest-Semitic precedent; the
Qur'anic \emph{m-y-z} root specifies the \emph{parting / severing} of
the vessel where Hebrew \emph{bāqaʿ} specifies its \emph{splitting},
both naming the failure-mode of the container rather than the trajectory
of its contents. The present discussion remains a \emph{comparative}
claim, not a strong universality claim: we do not assert that step-(iv)
container-failure lexicalisation is universally available, only that the
Qur'anic \emph{tamayyuz} and the Hebrew \emph{bāqaʿ} both instantiate it
transparently at the lexical level. The Qur'anic data thus
\emph{strengthen} --- without by themselves \emph{establishing} --- the
cross-cultural universalist hypothesis of CMT,\footnote{Lakoff and
  Kövecses, `Cognitive Model of Anger'; Kövecses, \emph{Metaphor and
  Emotion}.} and they extend the empirical base to seventh-century
Arabic in a register that the 2025 \emph{Metaphors of ANGER}
encyclopedia, conspicuously omitting Classical Arabic, leaves
un-canvassed.

\subsubsection{5.6. Implications for Qur'anic translation (conditional
on follow-up
evaluation)}\label{implications-for-quranic-translation-conditional-on-follow-up-evaluation}

Translation criticism of the Qur'an has long observed that translations
tend to level the spectrum---that \emph{ġaḍab} and \emph{ġayẓ} alike
become \emph{anger}; that \emph{baghy}, \emph{ṭughyān}, and
\emph{ʿutuww} alike become \emph{transgression} (Table 6). Our
four-verse, five-translation illustrative sample (§4.10) is consistent
with this pattern but is too small (\emph{n} = 4 verses × 5 translations
= 20 cells) to support strong prescriptive claims; recent computational
work --- notably Gaanoun and Alsuhaibani\footnote{Gaanoun and
  Alsuhaibani, `Sentiment Preservation'.} on sentiment preservation
across seven English translations and the ELQV emotion-labelled dataset
of 2,100 Qur'anic verses\footnote{{[}ELQV authors{]}, `Leveraging Large
  Language Models'.} --- has begun to operationalise the levelling
concern at scale on coarse Ekman categories, though without a graded
reference model of the lexical spectrum against which the levelling
could be benchmarked. We frame the present discussion as
\textbf{conditional implications}: contingent on a follow-up systematic
evaluation (a stratified random sample of \textasciitilde30 verses
across the six stages, with multi-rater coding against the spectrum
reference model), the analysis would \emph{if confirmed} support the
following candidate equivalents for critical and study-edition
translations:

\begin{quote}
\emph{uff}: vocal sigh of irritation · \emph{karh} (\emph{karāha}):
inner dislike · \emph{karh} (\emph{ikrāh}): coercion (a structural
extension, not an emotion) · \emph{ḍīq}: inner constriction · \emph{ḍāqa
dharʿan}: arm-span tightening / inhibited aggression · \emph{ḥuzn}:
persistent grief (transversal valence) · \emph{asaf}: regretful dismay
(with anger) · \emph{naqm}: vengeful disapproval · \emph{sakhaṭ}: moral
disapprobation · \emph{maqt}: intense moral aversion · \emph{ghaḍab}:
anger · \emph{ḥard}: anger combined with deliberate denial ·
\emph{ghayẓ}: contained / boiling rage · \emph{tamayyuz min al-ġayẓ}:
bursting from rage · \emph{baghy}: aggressive transgression ·
\emph{ṭughyān}: rebellious overflow · \emph{ʿutuww}: obstinate defiance.
\end{quote}

A second conditional implication, which we register as a hypothesis
rather than a recommendation, is that pedagogical translations may
benefit from marking each lexeme's position on the continuum (by inline
gloss, footnote, or chromatic coding) --- but the empirical case for
this rests on the comparative comprehension study we commend to future
work. The 30-verse stratified evaluation, paired with an inter-rater
coded comparison across the present spectrum reference model and the
ELQV labels, is a natural Phase II for the present dataset.

\subsubsection{5.7. Implications for Islamic
psychology}\label{implications-for-islamic-psychology}

The continuum offers a culturally-grounded framework mapping onto
Gross's model without importation of secular vocabulary, with three
conceptual implications: \textbf{(i) Prevention} --- early-stage
recognition through indigenous Stages 1--2 lexicon (\emph{uff},
\emph{karāha}, \emph{ḍīq}, \emph{ḥuzn}) supports CBT-style interception
of affective dysregulation; \textbf{(ii) Crisis intervention} --- the
\emph{kaẓm} construct integrates with contemporary
suppression-and-reappraisal techniques; \textbf{(iii) Rehabilitation}
--- the post-failure Stage 6 is framed as recoverable through
\emph{tawba}, providing motivational reinforcement for anger-disorder
and post-violent-trauma rehabilitation. These implications are
conceptual; their translation into validated clinical interventions
awaits empirical studies we recommend as a priority for future work.

\subsection{6. Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

\subsubsection{6.1. Substantive
contributions}\label{substantive-contributions}

This paper has advanced three contributions to the scholarship.

\textbf{Methodologically}, we have demonstrated that the integration of
three previously separate frameworks---Izutsu's semantic-field analysis,
Kennedy--Sassoon scalar semantics, and Lakoff--Kövecses
conceptual-metaphor theory---coupled with a reproducible computational
pipeline over the Quranic Arabic Corpus, produces a research apparatus
stronger than the sum of its parts. To our knowledge, this trifold
integration with corpus-empirical validation has not previously been
deployed in Qur'anic studies as a unified analytic framework, although
Qāʾimīniyā\footnote{Qāʾimīniyā, \emph{Maʿnāshināsī-yi Shinākhtī}; idem,
  \emph{Istiʿārah-hā-yi Mafhūmī}.} and Pakatchi\footnote{Pakatchi and
  Afrashi, \emph{Rūykardhā-yi Maʿnā-shinākhtī}.} have integrated Izutsu
and conceptual-metaphor theory, and Persian-language scalar-semantic
work on Qur'anic vocabulary may exist in venues we have not been able to
canvass. The research apparatus is openly available and applicable to
other Qur'anic semantic fields.

\textbf{Descriptively}, we have produced an exhaustive, reproducible
concordance of 312 attestations across the fourteen core spectrum roots,
with corresponding distributional and network-analytic outputs. The CSV
outputs --- including FDR-adjusted binomial tests, marginal-preserving
permutation null \emph{p}-values, and bootstrap 95\% CIs on every
centrality estimate --- provide a foundation on which further
philological and computational work can build.

\textbf{Theoretically}, we have formulated and empirically defended the
\textbf{Qur'anic Action-Intensity Continuum Hypothesis}: the claim that
fourteen core lexemes of the Qur'anic anger-spectrum field operate as a
single graded continuum structured by \emph{intensity of action} and
partitioned into six phenomenologically meaningful stages. The
hypothesis is supported by qualitative evidence from the four
authoritative lexica and from a now-broadened tafsir consultation
(§3.5), and by quantitative evidence from frequency, distribution,
morphology, and network structure. We have, in addition, identified
distributional patterns that, to our knowledge, have not been previously
reported as a coordinated set, while reporting their post-FDR evidential
strength transparently: the exclusively-Medinan \emph{sakhaṭ} survives
Holm-Bonferroni correction at α = 0.05 \emph{borderline} (raw \emph{p} =
0.005, adjusted \emph{p} = 0.05) and is robust across baselines
0.70--0.78; the exclusively-Meccan \emph{asaf}, the Meccan-tilted
\emph{naqm}, the hapax-Meccan \emph{ḥard}, and the Medinan-tilted
\emph{karh} are reported as \emph{contextual observations} warranting
follow-up rather than as confirmed findings. The general phenomenon of
\textbf{discursive-contextual lexical specialisation} of which we
hypothesise these are instances is proposed as a research target rather
than as a settled result. We have entered explicit caveats on the
bidirectional causation of the Stage-6 lexemes (§4.8.5), grounded in a κ
= 0.79-validated tafsir-coding audit, and argued that Q. 67:8 furnishes
an \emph{early and unusually transparent} linguistic exemplar of
``container collapse''---a claim whose form has been calibrated by the
focused six-tradition philological survey of §5.5, which identifies Job
32:19 (\emph{bāqaʿ}) as the one clear Northwest-Semitic precedent for
vessel-rupture imagery and accordingly tempers any strong uniqueness
reading.

\subsubsection{6.2. Answers to the research
questions}\label{answers-to-the-research-questions}

\textbf{RQ1 (internal structure of the field and principal nodes).} The
Qur'anic anger-spectrum field is a fourteen-node lexical network
organised around two structural roles. The bridge node is \emph{baghy},
with the highest point-estimate closeness centrality (0.55) and the
heaviest cross-field connectivity to the umbrella moral-evaluation terms
(16 aya-level co-occurrences with
\emph{ẓulm}/\emph{jrm}/\emph{fsq}/\emph{ʿadāwah}/\emph{shanaʾān} ---
\textasciitilde53\% of the total cross-field ties for the fourteen
spectrum roots). Stage-internal hubs are \emph{ḥuzn} (Stage 2, 42
attestations, 88\% verbal) and \emph{ghaḍab} (Stage 4, 24 attestations,
67\% nominal), with the \emph{ḥzn--ḍyq} edge (weight 3) the strongest
single tie in the network. Several peripheral nodes --- \emph{sakhaṭ},
\emph{asaf}, \emph{ḥard}, \emph{ʿutuww} --- exhibit marked
distributional asymmetries that we interpret as instances of
discursive-contextual specialisation (§5.4).

\textbf{RQ2 (continuum ordering and theoretical legitimation).} The
fourteen lexemes order on a six-stage continuum --- pre-anger
displeasure, inner pressure and contraction, evaluative aversion, active
anger, compressed/explosive rage, behavioural outcomes (with
bidirectional-causation caveat) --- organised by the composite dimension
of \emph{intensity of action}: locus (inner → outer), experiential
intensity (low → high), and scope of consequence (individual →
structural). The ordering is licensed by the trifold theoretical
synthesis of §2: Izutsu's semantic-field method fixes the field
boundary, the Kennedy--Sassoon scalar-semantic apparatus supplies the
dimension and the ordering relation, and Lakoff--Kövecses CMT grounds
the climactic \emph{ghayẓ--kaẓm--tamayyuz} sequence in the PRESSURIZED
CONTAINER schema. The lexicographic evidence of Table 1 shows the
ordering is not the construction of the present analysis but is
internally encoded in the four authoritative lexica themselves, which
gloss each root with progressively more externalised and structural
meanings as one ascends from \emph{uff} to \emph{ʿutuww}.

\textbf{RQ3 (corpus-distributional corroboration).} The empirical
distribution is \emph{consistent with} --- though not by itself
confirmatory of --- the continuum. The asymptotic χ²(5) = 227.15
(Cohen's \emph{w} = 0.85) is large but the marginal-preserving
permutation null yields \emph{p} = 0.24, indicating that the asymptotic
\emph{p}-value over-states the evidence on this sparse,
marginally-imbalanced design (§3.6); the substantive case rests on the
qualitative evidence of §4.1 and §4.3--§4.8. The Stages 1--5 vs Stage 6
comparison \emph{fails to reject} equal split (χ²(1) = 1.55), which we
read as \emph{consistent with} the bidirectional-causation reading of
§4.8.5, the substantive support coming from the κ = 0.79-validated
tafsir-coding audit (A = 26\%, S = 49\%, A+S = 25\%; A-only re-analysis
χ²(1) = 81.4, \emph{p} \textless{} 0.001). Of the four per-root
distributional patterns, only the exclusively-Medinan \emph{sakhaṭ}
survives Holm-Bonferroni correction (raw \emph{p} = 0.005, adj. \emph{p}
= 0.05, borderline); the morphological gradient (verbal S1--2 → nominal
S6) and the bridging centrality profile at \emph{baghy}
(bootstrap-respecting) supply convergent corpus-internal evidence.

\textbf{RQ4 (structural homology with contemporary emotion psychology).}
The Qur'anic continuum is structurally homologous with four families of
contemporary emotion theory developed in §5.2: with Gross's
five-strategy process model of emotion regulation in Stages 1--5, with
Plutchik's three-level intensity wheel and Spielberger's STAXI
trait/state/control architecture in the lexical surface of the spectrum,
and with the Lazarus--Scherer--Roseman appraisal apparatus in Q. 96:6--7
(where \emph{ṭughyān} is conditioned on the appraisal of
\emph{istighnāʾ} in a textbook agency-self / motive-consistent /
high-control pattern). The continuum extends beyond all four frameworks
at Stage 6, where the structural-moral pathologies of \emph{baghy},
\emph{ṭughyān}, and \emph{ʿutuww} lexicalise the moral-anthropological
consequences of unmanaged emotion in a register process- and
trait/state-models do not contain. The integration is qualified by the
bidirectional-causation caveat of §4.8.5: Stage-6 lexemes stand in a
\emph{bidirectional} causal relation with the anger spectrum, not a
deterministic downstream one.

\textbf{RQ5 (practical implications for translation and Islamic
psychology).} \emph{Conditional} practical implications follow at two
levels. For Qur'anic translation, the analysis supplies candidate
distinct-equivalent renderings for the fourteen lexemes (§5.6) and
position-marking conventions that, \emph{if confirmed} by the stratified
follow-up evaluation we commend (\textasciitilde30 verses sampled across
the six stages with multi-rater coding against the spectrum reference
model and the ELQV labels), would address the systematic levelling
pattern catalogued by Gaanoun and Alsuhaibani (2025). For Islamic
psychology (§5.7), three applications follow: (i) early-stage
\emph{prevention} via Stages 1--2 vocabulary (\emph{uff}, \emph{karāha},
\emph{ḍīq}, \emph{ḥuzn}) supporting CBT-style affective-dysregulation
interception in a culturally-grounded register; (ii) \emph{crisis
intervention} via \emph{kaẓm} as the Qur'anic \emph{anger-control}
lexeme, operationally close to Spielberger's \emph{anger-in} construct
with the addition of explicit moral commendation; (iii) post-failure
\emph{rehabilitation} via the \emph{tawba} framework. All practical
implications are framed as hypotheses for empirical follow-up, not as
settled prescriptions.

\subsubsection{6.3. Limitations and future
work}\label{limitations-and-future-work}

The analysis is bounded by five limitations, each pointing to a
constructive next step we frame as a Phase II research agenda for the
present dataset. (i) \textbf{Surface-lexical scope.} Discourse-analytic
close-reading of the spectrum's narrative \emph{deployment} across suras
--- which stage clusters surface in prophetic-narrative versus
legal-prescriptive versus polemical-eschatological passages --- is left
for future work, in dialogue with El-Awa's relevance-theoretic
framework\footnote{El-Awa, \emph{Textual Relations}.} and Sinai's
Meccan/Medinan periodisation.\footnote{Sinai, \emph{The Qurʾan: A
  Historical-Critical Introduction}; idem, \emph{Key Terms}.} (ii)
\textbf{Tafsir engagement.} A \emph{tafsir-archive deep-dive} ---
systematic indexing of the fourteen-root inventory against the full
classical/medieval/Sufi/modernist tradition (al-Qushayrī, the Akbarī
school via \emph{Tafsīr al-Qāshānī} and Sulamī's \emph{Ḥaqāʾiq}, the
Twelver tradition via al-ʿAyyāshī and al-Qummī, the Mu'tazilī tradition
via Abū Muslim al-Iṣfahānī, the modernist tradition beyond the citations
already made), with κ-validated multi-coder reading on contested verses
--- would supply deeper exegetical grounding and an empirical map of how
the bidirectional-causation reading of Stage 6 is distributed across the
tradition. (iii) \textbf{Cognitive-linguistic apparatus.} Engagement
with classical Arabic \emph{bayān} theory (al-Jurjānī's \emph{Dalāʾil
al-Iʿjāz}, al-Sakkākī's \emph{Miftāḥ al-ʿUlūm}) would supply an
indigenous substrate within which the continuum could be re-stated in
the categories of classical Arabic rhetoric. (iv) \textbf{Ḥadīth
integration.} A parallel concordance over Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, Ṣaḥīḥ
Muslim, and al-Kāfī would supply convergent evidence on the \emph{kaẓm}
construct and a cross-corpus replication test for the underpowered
\emph{asaf} 5/0 distribution. (v) \textbf{Islamic-psychology
operationalisation.} The §5.7 implications await translation into pilot
studies of \emph{kaẓm}-grounded emotion-regulation training, with
stage-marking protocols validated against the STAXI and Difficulties in
Emotion Regulation Scale.

Four NLP analyses are queued as Phase II follow-ups, each addressing a
specific evidential limitation. (a) \textbf{AraBERT / CAMeLBERT
contextual-embedding validation} --- applied to the 312 attestations and
clustered unsupervised (k-means / HDBSCAN), would supply an independent
test of stage-recoverability: if the six manually-assigned stages emerge
as separable clusters, the architecture is corroborated by a method
whose stage-assignment has no human input. (b) \textbf{Cross-translation
semantic-drift quantification} --- a multilingual embedding model
applied to the 312 attestations across 5--10 EN/FA translations would
quantify the levelling effect catalogued in §4.10 and Gaanoun and
Alsuhaibani (2025), supplying per-root, per-translation drift estimates
against the Arabic source. (c) \textbf{Diachronic / chronological-order
test} --- re-coding the 312 attestations by Nöldeke or Bell chronologies
(rather than binary Meccan/Medinan) and testing for stage-shift would
discriminate competing models of the spectrum's emergence. (d)
\textbf{PMI-weighted collocation network} --- replacing the raw
aya-level co-occurrence counts of §4.8 with positive pointwise mutual
information would yield a network corrected for marginal-frequency
artefacts, generating a more conservative test of the
\emph{baghy}-as-bridge claim. Each is feasible in a Colab notebook
environment over the existing pipeline. The methodological commitment
shaping the analysis remains: distributional, statistical, and
computational evidence is a \emph{complement to} --- not a replacement
for --- the classical philological and exegetical tradition, and the
load-bearing claims rest on the convergence of the two registers.

\subsubsection{6.4. Closing remark}\label{closing-remark}

The Qur'anic lexicon encodes a sophisticated theory of the anger
spectrum --- not as explicit theoretical formulation but as the
architectural design of its semantic surface. Recovering that theory
requires methodological tools that bring together classical philological
attentiveness, contemporary linguistic-theoretical precision, and
corpus-computational verifiability. We hope the present study, alongside
Izutsu, Qāʾimīniyā, Pakatchi, Narimani et al., and Qarizadeh et al.,
advances this triple integration. The classical and the computational
are complementary instruments whose joint deployment opens horizons
neither could disclose alone.
