# Reviewer 1 (Philological / Exegetical) — JQS
**Recommendation**: Major revision
**Confidence**: 4

## Summary of the manuscript

The paper proposes that fourteen Qurʾanic roots (*ʾff, krh, ḍyq, ḥzn, ʾsf, nqm, sxṭ, mqt, ġḍb, ḥrd, ġyẓ, bġy, ṭġy, ʿtw*) constitute a graded semantic field organised by "intensity of action" across six phenomenological stages — pre-anger displeasure, inner pressure, evaluative aversion, active anger, compressed rage, and behavioural outcomes. The authors triangulate three theoretical frames (Izutsu's semantic-field method, Kennedy–Sassoon scalar semantics, Lakoff–Kövecses CMT) against a computational pipeline over the Quranic Arabic Corpus (Dukes 2011), reporting 312 attestations with permutation, Holm-Bonferroni, bootstrap, and κ-validated *tafsīr*-coding overlays. Headline philological claims are (i) the Medinan-exclusive distribution of *sakhaṭ* as discursive specialisation toward the *Munāfiqūn*; (ii) *baghy* as a bridging node to the wider moral-evaluation field; (iii) Q. 67:8 (*takādu tamayyazu min al-ġayẓ*) as an early and unusually transparent lexicalisation of "container collapse"; and (iv) a *bidirectional*-causation reading of Stage 6 in which *baghy*/*ṭughyān*/*ʿutuww* are not deterministically anger-derived. The work is methodologically ambitious, well-documented, and reproducibility-conscious; the philological core is mostly defensible, but several exegetical, lexicographic, and transliteration issues need correction before the paper is acceptable for JQS.

## Major issues

1. **Sūra-naming convention is breached at almost every citation** — Location: throughout, esp. abstract (line 17), §1.1 (line 27, "Q. 67:8"), §1.4 (H5), §3.6 (line 109), §4.7.2 (line 256), §5.5 (line 388 ff.), §6.1 (line 426). Problem: JQS house style — and Edinburgh University Press IJMES-adjacent practice — requires named sūras to be cited as *sūrat al-Mulk*, *sūrat al-Baqara*, *sūrat al-Naḥl*, *sūrat al-Furqān*, *sūrat Yūsuf* etc. when referenced by name; the manuscript silently uses bare āya numbers throughout (e.g. "Q. 67:8", "Q. 3:134", "Q. 17:23", "Q. 96:6") without naming any sūra in the *sūrat al-X* register, and where common nouns appear they are never anchored to a *sūra* identifier. For a *Journal of Qurʾanic Studies* submission this is a non-trivial stylistic gap. Proposed fix: introduce each verse on first citation as "Q. *sūrat al-Mulk* 67:8" or "*sūrat al-Mulk* (Q. 67:8)", subsequent references may shorten to "Q. 67:8"; audit at minimum the canonical exemplar verses listed at §3.6 (lines 109–110) and the Q. 67:8 / Q. 3:134 / Q. 17:23 / Q. 96:6 anchors that recur across §4–§6.

2. **The κ = 0.79 inter-coder figure is methodologically under-specified for a tafsīr-grounded audit** — Location: §4.8.5 (line 286). Problem: the audit reports "second-coder verification on a 20% subsample (κ = 0.79)" against four heavyweight commentaries (*al-Mīzān*, *al-Kashshāf*, *Majmaʿ al-Bayān*, *al-Taḥrīr*), but does not specify (a) the identity and qualifications of the second coder, (b) whether they read all four commentaries on every coded verse or were given collated extracts, (c) the codebook (what evidentiary thresholds distinguish A, S, and A+S), or (d) which 29 of 145 attestations were on the 20% subsample. This is the load-bearing piece of evidence for the bidirectional-causation reading and for Hypothesis 2; the manuscript invokes it three times (§4.2 line 170, §4.8.5, §6.1 line 426) but never supplies a coding-protocol footnote or appendix. Proposed fix: add a brief methods sub-section (or expand §4.8.5) covering coder background, codebook with worked examples for each label, sampling procedure, disagreement-resolution rule, and a one-paragraph deposit of the codebook alongside `stage6_causation_coding.csv`.

3. **The "*ghayẓ* exclusive to unbelievers / hypocrites in human attributions" claim is not balanced against the strongest counter-evidence in the corpus** — Location: §1.3 line 39 (paraphrasing Narimani et al.) and §4.7 (lines 252–254). Problem: the manuscript inherits and propagates the Narimani-Iqbali-Chari thesis that *ghayẓ* in human reference is exclusive to unbelievers/hypocrites, but the canonical Q. 3:134 (*al-kāẓimīn al-ġayẓ*) attributes the substance of *ghayẓ* — as the object of *kaẓm* — to believers themselves, who would not be commanded to suppress what they did not feel. Classical exegesis from al-Ṭabarī through al-Rāzī through Ṭabāṭabāʾī takes this for granted, and Sufi readings (al-Qushayrī, whom the authors cite at §3.5) build the entire *kaẓm* doctrine on the believer's interior *ghayẓ*. The manuscript needs to distinguish the *transitive*/object-of-attribution sense (Pharaonic *ghayẓ* directed at believers) from the *intransitive*/experiential sense, and to acknowledge that Q. 3:134 is a clear case of the latter directed at believers. Proposed fix: insert a short qualification at §4.7.1 noting the *kāẓimīn al-ġayẓ* construction inherently presupposes believer *ghayẓ*, and gloss the Narimani et al. claim accordingly.

4. **The Q. 67:8 reading misrepresents what *tamayyaza* / m-y-z lexically does** — Location: §2.3 (line 65), §4.7.2 (line 256), §5.5 (lines 388–392). Problem: the manuscript repeatedly claims that *tamayyaza min al-ġayẓ* describes the *fire-vessel of Jahannam* bursting apart, with the m-y-z root parsed as "to part, sever" *of the container*. Classical exegesis is more divergent: al-Ṭabarī, al-Zamakhsharī, and al-Rāzī read *tamayyazu* not as the container splitting but as the contents (the Hellfire) almost *coming apart from itself / separating piece from piece* in rage — i.e. *tamayyuz* is the *separation of constituents*, not the rupture of a vessel wall (*Mufradāt* glosses m-y-z as *al-tafrīq bayna al-mutashābihāt*; cf. Q. 8:37 *li-yamīza Allāhu al-khabītha min al-ṭayyib*). The CMT mapping to "container collapse" is interesting but is a *constructive* reading on the authors' part, not the *plain sense* of the classical tafsīr tradition. As currently written, the manuscript presents the container-rupture reading as if it were lexicographically given. Proposed fix: in §5.5, frame the container-collapse reading explicitly as the authors' CMT-grounded *interpretation* of *tamayyuz*, present alongside (not in place of) the classical "separation of constituents" reading attested in al-Ṭabarī and al-Zamakhsharī, and adjust §2.3 and §4.7.2 accordingly. This strengthens the argument by making the constructive move visible rather than weakens it.

5. **The Stage-3 ordering *naqm* → *sakhaṭ* → *maqt* is asserted as scalar but not defended against contrary lexical evidence** — Location: §2.2 (line 61), Table 1 (lines 132–134), §4.5 (lines 218–242). Problem: the four-lexicon table at §4.1 gives *maqt* as *ashaddu al-ibghāḍ* (Zajjāj, via Ibn Manẓūr) — *the most intense aversion* — yet the continuum places it *between* *sakhaṭ* and *ghaḍab* on the action axis. If *maqt* is the lexicographic superlative of aversion (and the four-lexicon table itself says so), the action-intensity ordering needs an explicit argument for why *maqt* is *less* intense than *ghaḍab* and *more* intense than *sakhaṭ* — particularly since Q. 40:10 (*la-maqtu Allāhi akbaru min maqtikum anfusakum*) and Q. 4:22 use *maqt* in registers of the highest gravity. The same issue arises for *sakhaṭ* (Mostafavi: *declarative rejection grounded in moral assessment* — closer to a settled disposition than a graded intensity). Proposed fix: at §4.5 introduction (line 216) add a paragraph defending Stage-3 *internal* ordering, distinguishing *intensity of aversion* (in which *maqt* leads) from *intensity of action* (the paper's chosen dimension, on which it sits below *ghaḍab*); cross-reference §2.2's three-component decomposition of the scale.

6. **The "first" / novelty rhetoric for *ghayẓ–kaẓm–tamayyuz* underclaims engagement with Maalej, Soriano, Yu and the Arabic-CMT literature** — Location: §1.2 (line 33), §1.3 (line 39), §5.5 (line 394 on Kövecses, Benczes and Szelid 2025). Problem: although §5.5 lists Maalej (2004) on Tunisian Arabic and the Rabab'ah & Al-Saidat (2022) Jordanian-Arabic study in the bibliography, the manuscript's body argument that the 2025 *Metaphors of ANGER* encyclopedia "leaves Classical Arabic un-canvassed" is weakened by the fact that Maalej's "Anger in Tunisian Arabic" and Derki's MSA-vs-English study (both in the bibliography) already do significant CMT work on Arabic. The novelty claim should be sharpened from "Classical Arabic / Qurʾanic is omitted" to "*Qurʾanic ghayẓ–kaẓm–tamayyuz* triad is omitted" — which is defensible. Proposed fix: at §5.5 (line 394) tighten the framing to "Qurʾanic anger-imagery and the *ghayẓ–kaẓm–tamayyuz* triad in particular are absent from the 2025 encyclopedia" and at §1.3 (line 39) briefly engage Maalej 2004 and Rabab'ah & Al-Saidat 2022 as the closest Arabic-CMT precedents.

7. **Engagement with the SOAS / JQS / Princeton Qurʾanic-studies conversation is uneven** — Location: §1.3 (line 39), §5.4 (line 384). Problem: Sinai (2017, 2023) is cited but somewhat instrumentally — *Key Terms of the Qurʾan* contains entries on *baghy*, *ẓulm*, *ṭughyān*, *ghaḍab*, and these are the natural philological interlocutors for §§4.5–4.8, yet none is quoted or substantively engaged. Reynolds, Neuwirth, Saleh, Stewart, Bauer, Sirry — the JQS/Edinburgh/SOAS-orbit voices the cover letter implicitly addresses — do not appear in the bibliography at all. Stewart on the discursive/Pauline/legalistic register would be directly relevant to the bidirectional-causation reading at §5.3; Neuwirth on the Meccan / Medinan diachrony would tighten §5.4 beyond a Sinai-only base. Proposed fix: thicken §1.3, §5.4, and §5.5 with at least three to five substantive engagements with this literature — minimum Sinai *Key Terms* entries on *baghy*, *ṭughyān*, and *ghaḍab*; Neuwirth on Meccan period rhetorics; and either Reynolds (Biblical subtext) or Saleh (on the *tafsīr* tradition) at §5.5 where the Job 32:19 / Hebrew Bible comparison is developed.

8. **Two cited references retain "[Authors]" / "(Author attribution unresolved …)" placeholders** — Location: footnotes at §1.3 line 39 (ELQV; *Cogent Arts & Humanities* untranslatability paper; IJCP Aeducia paper) and bibliography entries at lines 570, 578, 592. Problem: JQS will reject a manuscript with unresolved author attributions in the bibliography. Proposed fix: complete the attributions or remove the citations and the corresponding body claims that depend on them. The ELQV citation in particular is load-bearing in §5.6 and §6.3 and cannot remain a placeholder.

## Minor issues

1. Location **§3.2 (line 87)** — Problem: "*ḥ-n-q* (post-Qur'anic, ḥadīth-only)" is asserted as exclusion ground, but *ḥ-n-q* does in fact appear in the Qurʾan once (Q. 2:264 contains the related root *q-h-r*; *ḥ-n-q* itself is correctly absent but the gloss "post-Qur'anic, ḥadīth-only" is partially misleading — *ḥanaq* is classical Arabic and well-attested in pre-Islamic poetry). Fix: re-word to "not attested in the Qurʾanic corpus".

2. Location **§3.5 (line 103)** — Problem: the audit lists "al-Qushayrī's *Laṭāʾif al-Ishārāt*" "for Sufi-tradition cross-checking on *kaẓm al-ghayẓ*", but al-Qushayrī's gloss on Q. 3:134 is not actually engaged anywhere in §4.7. Fix: either cite al-Qushayrī's reading at §4.7.1 or drop him from §3.5.

3. Location **§4.4.2 (line 208)** — Problem: the claim that *ḥuzn* in Q. 2:38 is "the affective signature of *separation from guidance*" is attributed to al-Ṭabāṭabāʾī (*al-Mīzān* 1:146); *al-Mīzān* vol. 1 ends well before *p.* 146 in the Aʿlamī edition cited at line 532 of the bibliography (vol. 1 covers approximately sūrat al-Fātiḥa to Q. 2:39, and 1:146 in standard editions is at Q. 2:78–83, not Q. 2:38). Fix: re-verify the page reference; Ṭabāṭabāʾī's commentary on Q. 2:38 is in *al-Mīzān* 1:96–98 (Beirut Aʿlamī).

4. Location **§4.4.3 (line 210)** — Problem: "*al-jamʿ bayna al-ḥuzn wa-l-ġaḍab*" is attributed to both Ibn Fāris and al-Rāghib, but Ibn Fāris's *Maqāyīs* glosses *ʾ-s-f* as *al-ḥuzn al-shadīd* (he does not couple with *ghaḍab* explicitly); the combined gloss is al-Rāghib's. Fix: separate the citations and attribute the *grief + anger* coupling to al-Rāghib alone.

5. Location **§4.5.2 (line 238)** — Problem: the Narimani et al. claim that *sakhaṭ* is reserved for the *Munāfiqūn* is more nuanced in the source than the manuscript suggests — at least one of the four attestations (Q. 5:80, *labiʾsa mā qaddamat lahum anfusuhum an sakhiṭa Allāhu ʿalayhim*) is exegetically discussed by al-Ṭabarī as directed at the People of the Book / unbelievers from among the People of the Book, not the Munāfiqūn properly. Fix: re-check the four attestations (Q. 3:162, Q. 5:80, Q. 9:58, Q. 47:28) against the Munāfiq vs Ahl al-Kitāb distinction in al-Ṭabarī and al-Ṭabāṭabāʾī; the manuscript's "all four tied to the *Munāfiqūn*" overstates the exegetical consensus.

6. Location **§4.6.1 (line 248)** — Problem: "Al-Fakhr al-Rāzī's *Mafātīḥ al-Ghayb* glosses divine *ghaḍab* as *irādat inzāl al-ʿiqāb*" is attributed without volume/page reference. The gloss is correct as paraphrase but the formula itself (in this exact wording) is closer to al-Ashʿarī's school of *taʾwīl* — al-Rāzī typically gives *irādat al-intiqām* or *irādat al-ʿuqūba*. Fix: add the *Mafātīḥ* volume/page reference, and verify whether the formula is al-Rāzī's own or paraphrased from him.

7. Location **§4.7.2 (line 256)** — Problem: the parsing of *m-y-z* as "to part, sever" is one valid sense; the dominant lexicographic gloss in *Mufradāt* and *Lisān* is *al-tafrīq bayna al-mukhtaliṭ* — *separating things that have been mixed* — which is closer to "sort, distinguish" than to "part, sever". Cf. Q. 8:37, Q. 36:59. Fix: add a footnote noting the broader semantic range of *m-y-z*; this is also relevant to Major issue 4.

8. Location **§5.5 (line 392)** — Problem: the *Manyu-Sūkta* attribution to ṚV 10.83–84 is correct, but the gloss "weaponised as Indra's bolt" conflates *manyu* (the personified wrath) with *vajra* (Indra's bolt). Fix: re-word to "personified as a martial deity associated with Indra".

9. Location **§5.5 (line 388)** — Problem: the assertion that English exemplars "lexicalise the failure-mode as the *exit of contents* with the container preserved" overlooks idioms like *blew his top* / *flipped his lid* (which the manuscript itself cites at line 388 as "step (iv)") — these *do* in fact lexicalise vessel-component rupture (*top*, *lid* depart from the body). Fix: refine the contrast: in English the rupturing component is *peripheral* (the lid, the top), whereas in *tamayyuz* the *body* of the vessel itself parts; this preserves the comparative point while strengthening accuracy.

10. Location **§4.10 (Table 6, lines 317–322)** — Problem: Saheeh International rendering of *ṭughyān* in Q. 96:6 — the manuscript reports "transgresses" but the standard Saheeh International rendering is "transgresses (in disbelief and tyranny)" or similar with parenthetical gloss; check the edition cited. Fix: verify exact wording against the 1997 or 2010 Saheeh editions and note the edition used.

11. Location **§4.1 (Table 1, line 140)** — Problem: *ʿ-t-w* glossed for Ibn Manẓūr as "obstinate excess attached to *istikbār*"; *Lisān* in fact glosses *ʿatā/yaʿtū* primarily as *istakbara wa-jāwaza al-ḥadd*, with *istikbār*-attachment a derived collocation rather than a primary lexicographic feature. Fix: re-word to "transgressing the limit, with *istikbār*-attachment as collocational feature".

12. Location **§6.3 (line 442)** — Problem: "*Tafsīr al-Qāshānī*" is more correctly cited under its actual author Ibn ʿArabī's pupil ʿAbd al-Razzāq al-Kāshānī (d. 730/1330), and the work is sometimes attributed in the manuscript tradition to Ibn ʿArabī himself — the philological status is contested. Fix: cite as "the *Tafsīr* attributed to al-Kāshānī (sometimes ascribed to Ibn ʿArabī)".

## Editorial polish (line-level prose / diacritics / citation-style fixes)

- **IJMES diacritics inconsistency**: the manuscript mixes *tafsīr* and *tafsir* (compare §3.5 line 103 with §1.3 line 39 and §5.4 line 384), *Munāfiqūn* and *munāfiqūn* (capitalisation varies), *ḥadīth* and *ḥadith* (footnote at line 39 vs §6.3 line 442). Adopt one standard (IJMES with macrons and dots on all instances) and apply globally.
- **Apostrophe / hamza glyph**: throughout the manuscript the right single quotation mark (’) is used where the Arabic *hamza* (ʾ) is needed — e.g. "Qur’an", "ʿulw fi al-ard", "kaẓm al-ghayẓ". The transliteration symbols ʿ (ayn) and ʾ (hamza) should be used consistently in italicised technical terms; "Qurʾan" rather than "Qur'an" is JQS house style.
- **Italicisation**: Arabic technical terms should be italicised on first occurrence and (per IJMES) on every occurrence. *tafsīr*, *taqwā*, *ḥilm*, *kaẓm*, *istighnāʾ*, *istikbār*, *ʿulw fī al-arḍ*, *bāṭin / ẓāhir*, *qabīḥ*, *bayān* — audit for consistent italics.
- **Footnote style**: footnotes alternate between full bibliographic entries on first occurrence and short-form (author, short title, page) thereafter — JQS practice — but the manuscript mixes the two (e.g. footnote at line 39 supplies full DOI on what is the third citation of Narimani et al.). Standardise per JQS *Notes for Contributors*.
- **Bibliography ordering**: the English-language section places Agresti out of alphabetical order (between *Chicago Assyrian Dictionary* and Cochran, line 566). Move to correct position.
- **Sūra references in figure captions**: figure captions at lines 74, 95, 181, 184, 187, 289, 292, 399 all reference verses by Q. *n*:*m* alone; convert at least the first reference per figure to "*sūrat al-X* (Q. *n*:*m*)".
- **Hyphenation**: *Lakoff-Kövecses*, *Kennedy-Sassoon* — settle on en-dash (Lakoff–Kövecses, Kennedy–Sassoon) per JQS practice; the manuscript already does this in some places (line 17, line 33) and not in others.
- **Bibliography entry for al-Fīrūzābādī's *al-Qāmūs al-Muḥīṭ*** (line 504) lacks a year of publication and editor. Either supply or remove if not actually cited in the body (the *Qāmūs* is not referenced in §4).
- **Almuhanna 2025 / Gaanoun and Alsuhaibani 2025 entries** (lines 554 and 584) — the manuscript candidly notes Almuhanna is a "placeholder" superseded by Gaanoun and Alsuhaibani; remove the placeholder to avoid confusion.

## Verdict

The manuscript is a serious and impressively documented piece of corpus-philological work whose principal arguments are mostly defensible and whose reproducibility commitments are exemplary for the field. To move from **major revision** to **accept**, the authors must (i) apply the *sūrat al-X* convention consistently and tighten IJMES diacritics / *hamza* glyph use globally; (ii) make the κ = 0.79 tafsīr-coding protocol fully transparent and defend the Stage-3 internal ordering against the *maqt* superlative-aversion problem; (iii) recalibrate the Q. 67:8 / *tamayyuz* reading to present the container-collapse interpretation as a CMT-grounded *construction* rather than as the plain lexicographic sense, and thicken substantive engagement with Sinai *Key Terms*, Neuwirth, and Maalej / Rabab'ah on Arabic CMT. With these revisions the paper would be a strong contribution to JQS.
