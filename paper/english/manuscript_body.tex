\subsection{Abstract}\label{abstract}

The Qur'an deploys an exceptionally articulated lexicon for the
\emph{anger spectrum}, yet existing scholarship treats it word-by-word
or through normative-ethical frames. This study argues that fourteen
core Arabic roots (\emph{ʾff, krh, ḍyq, ḥzn, ʾsf, nqm, sxṭ, mqt, ġḍb,
ḥrd, ġyẓ, bġy, ṭġy, ʿtw}) form a graded semantic field organised by
\emph{intensity of action} across six phenomenological stages: pre-anger
displeasure, inner pressure, evaluative aversion, active anger,
compressed rage, and behavioural outcomes. Integrating Izutsu's
semantic-field approach, Kennedy--Sassoon scalar semantics, and
Lakoff--Kövecses conceptual metaphor theory, we validate the continuum
against the Quranic Arabic Corpus through a reproducible Python pipeline
over 312 attestations. Three findings emerge under uncertainty-aware
metrics. First, the asymptotic χ²(5) = 227.15 is large but a
marginal-preserving permutation null yields \emph{p} = 0.24; the omnibus
is \emph{consistent with} rather than confirmatory of non-uniformity.
Second, three roots survive Holm-Bonferroni correction over the family
of fourteen per-root binomials: \emph{karh} (Medinan-tilted, adj.
\emph{p} ≈ 10⁻⁶), \emph{baghy} (Meccan-tilted, adj. \emph{p} ≈ 5 ×
10⁻³), and \emph{ghayẓ} (Medinan-tilted, adj. \emph{p} ≈ 2 × 10⁻²);
\emph{sakhaṭ} (exclusively Medinan) sits at adj. \emph{p} = 0.05, just
below the conventional threshold. The pattern instantiates
\emph{discursive-contextual lexical specialisation}. Third, network
analysis identifies \emph{baghy} as a bridging node to the broader
moral-evaluation field; Q. 67:8 of sūrat al-Mulk (\emph{takādu tamayyazu
min al-ġayẓ}) is an unusually transparent instance of ``container
collapse''; a κ = 0.79-validated \emph{tafsīr} audit supports a
\emph{bidirectional-causation} reading of Stage 6 (A = 26\%, S = 49\%,
A+S = 25\%). Implications follow, conditionally, for Qur'anic
translation and Islamic psychology.

\textbf{Keywords:} Qur'an; anger spectrum; semantic field; cognitive
linguistics; scalar semantics; conceptual metaphor; corpus linguistics.

\subsection{1. Introduction}\label{introduction}

\subsubsection{1.1. Statement of the
problem}\label{statement-of-the-problem}

The Qur'anic lexicon for the \emph{anger spectrum} exhibits a precision
that survives only with difficulty into translation. Where English
versions level \emph{uff}, \emph{karh}, \emph{ḍīq}, \emph{ḥuzn},
\emph{asaf}, \emph{naqm}, \emph{sakhaṭ}, \emph{maqt}, \emph{ghaḍab},
\emph{ḥard}, \emph{ghayẓ}, \emph{baghy}, \emph{ṭughyān}, and
\emph{ʿutuww} into a small set of broad equivalents --- ``displeasure,''
``distress,'' ``grief,'' ``anger,'' ``rage,'' ``transgression,''
``rebellion'' --- the Arabic original distinguishes them with the
precision of a graded taxonomy. We advance the hypothesis that these
fourteen roots are not synonyms but coordinated nodes in a single
\emph{graded} semantic field, organised along \emph{intensity of
action}, traversing pre-anger displeasure, inner pressure and
contraction, evaluative aversion, active anger, compressed/explosive
rage, and --- at the behavioural pole --- outcomes (\emph{baghy},
\emph{ṭughyān}, \emph{ʿutuww}) whose causation is bidirectional rather
than purely emotional.

This claim has consequences beyond philology: the Qur'an encodes ---
through its lexical architecture, not by explicit theoretical statement
--- an implicit theory of the genealogy of moral failure, in which the
social-structural pathologies the text most frequently denounces
(transgression, tyranny, defiance) are \emph{related to} and often
developmentally downstream from inner experiences of irritation,
dislike, constriction, and grief. As we argue in §4--§5, the
relationship at the behavioural pole is \emph{bidirectional}: anger may
produce \emph{baghy}/\emph{ṭughyān}/\emph{ʿutuww}, but these behaviours
can equally produce anger in their victims, the prophets, and the divine
response. The distance from \emph{uff} (sūrat al-Isrāʾ, Q. 17:23) to
\emph{ʿutuww} (sūrat al-Furqān, Q. 25:21) is graded rather than
categorical.

\subsubsection{1.2. Aims and originality}\label{aims-and-originality}

This paper has three aims. The first is \textbf{methodological}: to
demonstrate that combining Izutsu's semantic-field approach (Izutsu
1959, 1964, 2002), scalar-semantic ordering in the Kennedy--Sassoon
tradition (Kennedy 1999, 2007; Sassoon 2010), and conceptual-metaphor
theory in the Lakoff--Kövecses tradition (Lakoff and Johnson 1980;
Lakoff and Kövecses 1987; Kövecses 2000, 2010) produces stronger
philological claims than any of the three frameworks in isolation.
Izutsu's field method yields a network of mutually defining lexemes
around focus words but does not generate an ordering relation
\emph{within} the network; Lakoff and Kövecses (1987) supply a
cognitive-metaphor schema anchored lexicographically in \emph{English},
and the two-volume \emph{Metaphors of ANGER across Languages} (Kövecses,
Benczes and Szelid 2025) --- twenty-two languages from Mandarin to
Yoruba --- \emph{omits Classical Arabic entirely}. Kennedy--Sassoon
orders predicates along a dimension and a unit but has not been applied
to a Qur'anic emotion field. Qāʾimīniyā (2011, 2014) and Pakatchi and
Afrashi (2020) already integrate Izutsu with CMT in Persian; the
\emph{novel} element here is the Kennedy--Sassoon scalar layer
operationalised as a six-stage action-intensity continuum tested against
a 312-attestation reproducible pipeline, alongside (i) a κ =
0.79-validated \emph{tafsīr}-coding audit of the bidirectional-causation
reading of Stage 6, (ii) the \emph{kaẓm}-as-tying-off-of-waterskin
analysis of agent-internal containment, and (iii) the framing of Q. 67:8
of sūrat al-Mulk as an early and unusually transparent lexicalisation of
``container collapse'', calibrated against a six-tradition philological
survey (§5.5) that acknowledges Job 32:19 (\emph{bāqaʿ}) as the one
clear Northwest-Semitic precedent. The second aim is
\textbf{descriptive}: to map every Qur'anic occurrence of the fourteen
core roots, render that map publicly verifiable, and extract empirical
regularities previous scholarship was not in a position to detect. The
third aim is \textbf{interpretive}: to argue that the continuum
constitutes a coherent logic of anger escalation with structural
affinities to modern emotion psychology, and to identify the points at
which it \emph{exceeds} the contemporary models --- most pointedly at
Stage 6, where \emph{baghy}/\emph{ṭughyān}/\emph{ʿutuww} extend the
regulatory horizon of Gross's process model into the
moral-anthropological register.

\subsubsection{1.3. Prior work and the
gap}\label{prior-work-and-the-gap}

Prior scholarship clusters into five lines, each insufficient on its
own. \textbf{Single-word lexical studies}: the most directly relevant
Persian-language precedent --- Narimani, Iqbali and Chari (2021) on
\emph{ghayẓ}, \emph{ghaḍab}, and \emph{sakhaṭ} --- establishes on
\emph{tafsīr} grounds that \emph{ghaḍab} is central, \emph{sakhaṭ}
reserved for divine displeasure with hypocrites, and \emph{ghayẓ}
predominantly (not exclusively) attributed in human-reference contexts
to unbelievers/hypocrites. Q. 3:134 of sūrat Āl ʿImrān (\emph{al-kāẓimīn
al-ġayẓ}) is the canonical exception, attributing the substance of
\emph{ghayẓ} --- as the object of \emph{kaẓm} --- to believers
themselves. Qarizadeh et al.~(2024) on \emph{ṭughyān} show the root
attests in twenty-seven suras with the central meaning of transgressing
the limit. Both works illustrate the gap: high-quality lexical work for
individual lexemes, but no integrated continuum and no operational
ordering relation among field members. \textbf{Semantic-field and
cognitive-semantic studies} in the Izutsu tradition --- Qāʾimīniyā
(2011, 2014), Pakatchi and Afrashi (2020), Albayrak (2020) --- have
applied cognitive linguistics to Qur'anic vocabulary, but the
\emph{graded continuum} layer remains untreated. Kennedy--Sassoon
(Kennedy 1999, 2007; Sassoon 2010) supplies what Izutsu's method leaves
open: a measurable dimension along which field members can be ordered.
\textbf{Ethics-of-anger studies} in the \emph{kaẓm al-ghayẓ} tradition
are normatively rich and linguistically thin. \textbf{The
cross-linguistic CMT literature} has been decisively updated by
Kövecses, Benczes and Szelid (2025), which canvasses twenty-two language
traditions --- \emph{Classical Arabic is not separately treated}, and
the Qur'anic \emph{ghayẓ--kaẓm--tamayyuz} triad is absent from the
encyclopedia. The closest Arabic-CMT precedents are Maalej (2004, 2007)
on Tunisian Arabic and Rabab'ah and Al-Saidat (2022) on Jordanian
Arabic, both contemporary-dialect studies neither addressing the
Qur'anic data. \textbf{Historical-critical and corpus-linguistic
studies} supply complementary apparatus: Sinai (2017) articulates the
revised Meccan/Medinan periodisation underwriting our \emph{sakhaṭ} and
\emph{karh} findings and his \emph{Key Terms} (Sinai 2023) models the
lexicon-level entry organisation we extend at the field level; Neuwirth
(2019) frames Qur'anic form and diction as a dialogical intervention
into the late-antique discursive sphere, supplying the rhetorical-form
vocabulary at which §5.4 operates. El-Awa (2006) supplies the
relevance-theoretic vocabulary; Sharaf and Atwell (2012a, 2012b)
established Qur'anic-NLP reproducibility standards alongside QuranMorph
(Akra, Hammouda and Jarrar 2025) and MASAQ (Sawalha et al.~2024).
\textbf{Translation-criticism studies} are now quantitative at scale:
Gaanoun and Alsuhaibani (2025) show inconsistent sentiment polarity
across seven English translations, and Khan et al.~(2025) survey the
field; none supplies a graded \emph{lexeme-level} reference model
against which translation-levelling could be benchmarked.
\textbf{Islamic psychology} tends to draw on \emph{aḥādīth} and leave
systematic linguistic analysis of the Qur'anic surface in the
background. The present paper occupies the unfilled intersection of all
five lines: it adds the Kennedy--Sassoon scalar layer to the Izutsu /
CMT stack, applies it to the anger field that the 2025 \emph{Metaphors
of ANGER} encyclopedia omits, and releases the empirical pipeline under
the Sharaf--Atwell / QuranMorph reproducibility standard.

\subsubsection{1.4. Research questions and
hypotheses}\label{research-questions-and-hypotheses}

We address five research questions: (RQ1) the internal structure of the
Qur'anic anger-spectrum field and its principal nodes; (RQ2) the
continuum ordering by action-intensity and the theoretical framework
legitimating it; (RQ3) corpus-distributional corroboration of the
continuum (frequency, Meccan/Medinan, morphology, network centrality);
(RQ4) structural homology with Gross, Plutchik, Spielberger, and
Lazarus--Scherer appraisal theory; (RQ5) practical implications for
Qur'anic translation and clinical Islamic psychology.

The principal \textbf{Qur'anic Action-Intensity Continuum Hypothesis}
holds that the fourteen core roots operate as fourteen distinct grades
on a single semantic continuum structured by \emph{intensity of action},
organised in six phenomenological stages, supported by qualitative
(etymological, exegetical, metaphorical) and quantitative (frequency,
distribution, network) evidence. Five subsidiary hypotheses: (H1)
non-uniform six-stage frequency distribution; (H2) near-equal split
between phenomenological core (S1--5) and behavioural pole (S6),
supporting the bidirectional Stage-6 reading; (H3)
discursively-specialised exclusive-Medinan distribution of
\emph{sakhaṭ}; (H4) \emph{baghy} as bridge node in the co-occurrence
network; (H5) Q. 67:8 of sūrat al-Mulk as transparent instance of
``container collapse'' CMT failure mode.

\subsection{2. Theoretical Framework}\label{theoretical-framework}

\subsubsection{2.1. Izutsu's semantic-field
method}\label{izutsus-semantic-field-method}

Toshihiko Izutsu (1959, 1964, 2002) introduced a methodological
revolution to Qur'anic studies. Drawing on the structuralist linguistics
of the Trier--Weisgerber tradition and on East Asian philosophy of
language, Izutsu argued that the central terms of the Qur'an ---
\emph{Allāh}, \emph{īmān}, \emph{kufr}, \emph{ẓulm}, \emph{taqwā} ---
are not isolated propositions but nodes in a network of syntagmatic,
paradigmatic, and oppositional relations. The aggregate constitutes what
he called the ``Qur'anic \emph{Weltanschauung}.''

Izutsu distinguishes three layers: \emph{focus words} (central
organising concepts of a semantic field), \emph{semantic fields}
(networks of mutually defining lexemes around focus words), and
\emph{key words} (terms recurring across fields and integrating them
into a worldview). The structure has proved productive in subsequent
Qur'anic semantic work, in English (Saleh, Rippin) and Persian
(Qāʾimīniyā 2011, 2014; Pakatchi and Afrashi 2020).

Izutsu's framework justifies the move from atomistic single-lexeme
analysis to integrated field analysis. The fourteen target roots
constitute the field around the focus words \emph{ghaḍab} (central anger
term) and \emph{baghy} (central behavioural-outcome term), with the
lower-intensity cluster (\emph{uff}, \emph{karh}, \emph{ḍīq},
\emph{ḥuzn}, \emph{asaf}) identifying the pre-anger and inner-pressure
boundary and \emph{ʿutuww} the upper-intensity boundary. Yet Izutsu's
framework is silent on a question central to our enterprise: granted
these lexemes co-belong to a field, \emph{what is the structural
relation between them within the field?} For this we require scalar
semantics.

\subsubsection{2.2. Scalar semantics: from network to
continuum}\label{scalar-semantics-from-network-to-continuum}

The theory of \emph{gradable predicates} (Kennedy 1999, 2007; Sassoon
2010) supplies the missing apparatus. A graded predicate maps to a scale
with three components: a \emph{dimension} of measurement, an
\emph{ordering} on that dimension, and a unit of comparison. Scalar
predicates take degree modifiers and admit comparative constructions.
The Qur'anic anger field is precisely such a scale: the dimension is
\emph{intensity of action} (decomposable into locus, experiential
intensity, scope of consequence); the ordering follows the six-stage
progression; the implicit unit is the \emph{degree of externalisation}
of emotion from inner phenomenology to social rupture. Without this
framework, the claim that \emph{ghayẓ} is ``more intense than''
\emph{ghaḍab} relies on tacit native-speaker intuition. Kennedy--Sassoon
was developed for gradable adjectives rather than nominal lexical
fields; its extension here proceeds on the assumption of a
category-neutral underlying intensity scale recoverable from
lexicographic and corpus-distributional evidence.

\subsubsection{2.3. Conceptual metaphor theory and the container
schema}\label{conceptual-metaphor-theory-and-the-container-schema}

Conceptual metaphor theory (Lakoff and Johnson 1980; Lakoff and Kövecses
1987; Kövecses 2000, 2010) treats metaphor as a cognitive structure that
allows abstract domains (emotion) to be understood through concrete ones
(container, heat). Lakoff and Kövecses (1987: 195--221) reconstruct
American-English anger as a \emph{five-stage prototype scenario} --- (1)
offending event, (2) anger, (3) attempt at control, (4) loss of control,
(5) act of retribution --- onto which the central metaphors ANGER IS THE
HEAT OF A FLUID IN A PRESSURIZED CONTAINER, ANGER IS FIRE, and ANGER IS
A WILD ANIMAL are mapped, with the container / pressure cluster
operating especially in stages (2)--(4). Our central observation is that
the Qur'anic triad \emph{ghayẓ--kaẓm--tamayyuz} maps tightly onto stages
(2)--(4) of that scenario --- anger, attempt at control, and (incipient)
loss of control --- with sūrat al-Mulk Q. 67:8's \emph{takādu tamayyazu
min al-ġayẓ} providing a particularly transparent linguistic
crystallisation of stage (4). \emph{Ghayẓ} is glossed by all four
authoritative lexica as compressed contained anger --- the
\emph{cognitive-linguistic} reading takes this as the source-domain
image of fluid in a sealed vessel. \emph{Kaẓm}, etymologically tying off
a waterskin, names the agent-internal act of suppression that sustains
containment (sūrat Āl ʿImrān, Q. 3:134, \emph{al-kāẓimīn al-ghayẓ}).
\emph{Tamayyuz} (sūrat al-Mulk, Q. 67:8) figures the failure mode:
classical commentators read the verb as \emph{yanqaṭiʿu baʿḍuhā min
baʿḍ} (al-Ṭabarī, \emph{Jāmiʿ al-bayān}, ad loc.; al-Zamakhsharī,
\emph{al-Kashshāf}, ad loc.) --- the separation of constituents --- and
the cognitive-metaphor framing we propose reads this lexicographic
``separation'' as the linguistic surface of an underlying
CONTAINER-RUPTURE schema. Both readings are compatible. We treat
\emph{tamayyuz} as a \emph{manifestation} of Stage 5, not an independent
core root.

\subsubsection{2.4. The integrated
framework}\label{the-integrated-framework}

The three layers operate complementarily: Izutsu identifies field
boundaries; scalar semantics orders members along the action-intensity
dimension; CMT grounds the pressurisation-collapse metaphor organising
Stage 5. Each supplies what the others lack --- without Izutsu, no
motivation for field-coordination; without scalar semantics, no
apparatus for ordering; without CMT, no cognitive grounding for the
\emph{kaẓm--tamayyuz} sequence.

\subsection{3. Methods}\label{methods}

\subsubsection{3.1. Corpus}\label{corpus}

The empirical analysis uses the \textbf{Quranic Arabic Corpus} (QAC),
version 0.4, prepared by Kais Dukes at the University of Leeds and
released under the GPL (Dukes 2011). The QAC provides a morphologically
tagged record for each of the 128,219 segmentable units in the Qur'anic
text, specifying location \emph{(sūra:āya:word:segment)}, surface form
in Buckwalter ASCII transliteration, part-of-speech tag, and
morphological features (root, lemma, gender, number, case, and where
applicable semantic flags). Verse text in Uthmanic \emph{rasm} and
Meccan/Medinan sura classification are drawn from the Tanzil project
(Tanzil 2008), distributed under CC-BY-ND 3.0.

\subsubsection{3.2. Selection of target
roots}\label{selection-of-target-roots}

The fourteen core roots were selected by three concurrent criteria: (i)
\emph{exegetical centrality} --- appearance as a focal reference in at
least three of the four authoritative lexica (Ibn Fāris's
\emph{Maqāyīs}, al-Rāghib's \emph{Mufradāt}, Ibn Manẓūr's \emph{Lisān},
and Mostafavi's twentieth-century \emph{Taḥqīq}); (ii) \emph{minimum
corpus frequency} of one attestation, admitting Qur'anic hapax legomena
(\emph{ḥard}, sūrat al-Qalam, Q. 68:25) when supported by an explicit
gloss in at least three of the four lexica plus consistent treatment in
the four canonical \emph{tafāsīr} of §3.5; (iii) \emph{thematic
membership} in the anger-spectrum field without significant overlap with
adjacent fields (epistemology, faith, normative ethics).

\textbf{Excluded candidate roots} (transparency): \emph{q-h-r}
(dominance/power, \emph{al-Qahhār} divine name); \emph{ḥ-n-q} (not
attested in the Qurʾanic corpus); \emph{ʿ-n-f} (adverbial harshness, not
emotion); \emph{ḍ-r-b} (polysemous, conflict-scene);
\emph{q-ṣ-w}/\emph{q-s-y} (hardness-of-heart, epistemic-moral);
\emph{gh-l-ẓ} (adverbial harshness); \emph{ʾ-n-f} (nominal \emph{anf}
``nose'', post-Qur'anic anger sense); \emph{š-n-ʾ} (binary
loving/hating, reclassified as umbrella). \emph{Karh} (Stage 1) is
borderline --- \emph{karāha} is affective, \emph{ikrāh} (coercion)
structural; we include the affective sense and discuss the structural
extension separately (§4.3.1). Five umbrella moral terms (\textbf{šnʾ,
jrm, fsq, ẓlm, ʿdw}) are used for robustness (§4.9) and cross-field
co-occurrence (Table 4), excluded from the principal continuum either
because of inflated cardinality (\emph{ẓlm} n = 315 functions as a
meta-category) or because they belong to adjacent normative-ethics
fields.

\subsubsection{3.3. Computational
pipeline}\label{computational-pipeline}

The Python (MIT-licensed) pipeline operates in five steps: parse the QAC
morphology file; filter on target roots; collapse multi-segment
morphemes to surface words preserving the \emph{(sūra:āya)} coordinate;
join with sura-level metadata for Meccan/Medinan classification; emit
master concordance, per-root concordances, distributional summaries, and
network/centrality CSVs. The complete analysis is reproducible from a
fresh clone in four commands.

\textbf{Reproducibility audit.} QAC v0.4 is pinned by SHA-256; outputs
are released under MIT (code) and CC-BY 4.0 (manuscripts/figures); the
fourteen exemplar verses and a stratified fifty-segment audit on the
four most frequent core roots (\emph{ḥzn}, \emph{bġy}, \emph{ġḍb},
\emph{ṭġy}) returned 100\% correct segment-to-root assignment against
Tanzil. Full audit at \texttt{data/concordance/validation\_audit.md}.

\subsubsection{3.4. Quantitative analyses}\label{quantitative-analyses}

Quantitative tests beyond frequency tabulation: (a) χ²(5) and χ²(1) for
uniform-stage and S1--5/S6 nulls, with Cramér's \emph{V} and Cohen's
\emph{w}, plus a marginal-preserving permutation null (10,000
permutations, deterministic seed) for the omnibus, motivated by the
well-known limitations of asymptotic χ² on sparse, marginally-imbalanced
designs (Cochran 1954; Fisher 1922; Patefield 1981; Mehta and Patel
1983; Agresti 2013, §3.5);\footnote{The marginal-preserving permutation
  null holds the per-root attestation counts fixed and randomly permutes
  the fourteen stage labels across roots (10,000 permutations, fixed RNG
  seed 20260509); the empirical \emph{p}-value is the fraction of
  permuted χ² values meeting or exceeding the observed 227.15. This is
  the natural extension of Fisher-exact reasoning to a many-cell design
  where exact enumeration of the conditional reference set is
  intractable (Patefield 1981; Mehta and Patel 1983).} (b) three
monotonic-trend tests (Spearman ρ, Mann--Kendall,
Cochran--Armitage-style OLS slope); (c) two-sided exact binomials with
the corpus-derived 0.74 Meccan-ayat baseline, Holm--Bonferroni step-down
adjusted over the family of fourteen per-root tests, and a sensitivity
table at baselines 0.70--0.78; (d) morphological breakdown by POS; (e)
aya-level co-occurrence network (cf.~Mottaghi et al.~2024) with weighted
degree, betweenness, closeness, and eigenvector centrality, with
non-parametric bootstrap 95\% percentile CIs (1,000 ayat-resamples with
replacement). The combined uncertainty-aware reporting is, to our
knowledge, the most rigorous yet applied to Qur'anic
computational-corpus studies; the wider 2025 cross-scripture NLP
literature (Nandan et al.~2025) operates at the discourse-topic level on
coarse sentiment and does not yet apply this battery at the lexeme
level.

\subsubsection{3.5. Qualitative analyses}\label{qualitative-analyses}

Qualitative complements operationalise Izutsu's three-layer apparatus at
the field level: \emph{focus-word} identification (exegetical-centrality
across the four lexica → \emph{ghaḍab}, with \emph{baghy} as secondary
focus word for the structural-outcomes pole, §4.8); \emph{paradigmatic
relations} --- synonymy and gradation by intensity --- across the
fourteen roots via the lexicographic decomposition of Table 1 and the
stage assignments of §§4.3--4.8; \emph{syntagmatic contrasts} via the
aya-level co-occurrence graph (§4.8.2) and the PMI-weighted network of
Supplementary §S3. Specifically: (a) comparative etymology across the
four authoritative lexica (\emph{Maqāyīs}, \emph{Mufradāt},
\emph{Lisān}, \emph{Taḥqīq}); (b) comparative \emph{tafsīr} analysis on

al-Ṭabarsī's \emph{Majmaʿ al-Bayān}, Ibn ʿĀshūr's \emph{al-Taḥrīr})
supplemented with al-Ṭabarī, al-Qurṭubī, Ibn Kathīr, Sayyid Quṭb, and

(\emph{ḍīq al-ṣadr}, \emph{ḥuzn al-qalb}, \emph{asaf al-nafs},
\emph{ḍāqa dharʿan}); (d) illustrative translation-criticism on five
English translations (Yusuf Ali, Pickthall, Arberry, Saheeh
International, Hilali-Khan) at canonical exemplar verses (§4.10) ---
illustrative rather than systematic.

Across the six stages: S1 = 44; S2 = 60; S3 = 27; S4 = 25; S5 = 11; S6 =

= 227.15, \emph{p} \textless{} 0.001; Cramér's \emph{V} = 0.38, Cohen's
\emph{w} = 0.85, large by Cohen's conventions). However, while expected
counts are uniform at \textasciitilde52 and well above Cochran's
5-per-cell rule, the \emph{observed} marginal imbalance (S6 = 145 vs S5
= 11) is the asymmetric structure to which asymptotic χ² is most
vulnerable: \emph{baghy} alone supplies 96 attestations, almost a third
of the total. We therefore complement with a \textbf{marginal-preserving
permutation null} (Cochran 1954; Patefield 1981; Mehta and Patel 1983;
Agresti 2013), giving the empirical \emph{p}-value as the fraction of
permuted χ² values meeting or exceeding 227.15. The result is \emph{p} =
0.24 --- under random stage relabelling, a statistic this extreme is not
unusual. We read this honestly: asymptotic significance over-states the
evidence for non-uniformity, and the substantive case for the six-stage
architecture rests on the qualitative lexicographic, exegetical, and
metaphorical evidence of §4.1 and §4.3--§4.8 rather than the omnibus
\emph{p}-value. We retain the asymptotic χ² as a descriptive statistic
but treat it as \emph{consistent with} --- not \emph{positive evidence
for} --- H1. A discriminating test of monotonicity is reported in
§4.2.1.

A complementary χ² against the null of equal split between the
phenomenological core (S1--5 = 167) and the behavioural-outcomes pole
(S6 = 145) \emph{fails to reject} (χ²(1) = 1.55, Cohen's \emph{w} =
0.07). Because failure to reject is \emph{absence of evidence of a
difference} rather than evidence of equality, we report this not as
corroboration of H2 but as \emph{consistent with} the reading of §4.8.5
and §5.3: Stage-6 lexemes are not exclusively anger-derived but stand in
\emph{bidirectional} causal relation to the anger spectrum.

\paragraph{4.2.1. Monotonic-trend tests for the six-stage
ordering}\label{monotonic-trend-tests-for-the-six-stage-ordering}

Because the χ²(5) test confirms non-uniformity but says nothing about
\emph{ordering}, we report three complementary monotonic-trend tests of
stage rank against attestation frequency. (i) \textbf{Spearman} between
stage index and per-stage count is ρ = --0.09, \emph{p} = 0.85 --- the
count rises from S1 (44) to S2 (60), drops through S3--S5 (27, 25, 11),
then jumps to S6 (145), producing a non-monotonic profile. (ii)
\textbf{Mann--Kendall} yields \emph{S} = --3, \emph{z} = --0.38,
\emph{p} = 0.71. (iii) \textbf{Cochran--Armitage-style} OLS slope yields
slope = 10.17, \emph{z} = 0.85, \emph{p} = 0.40. All three fail to
reject the no-trend null.

We read this as substantively interpretable: the action-intensity
continuum is an ordering of \emph{semantic intensity}, not
\emph{attestation frequency}. The Qur'anic corpus is not a random sample
but a closed hortatory text whose lexical weights are
\emph{pedagogically determined}. One expects heaviest density at the two
poles of moral consequence --- S1--2 (entry points for \emph{kaẓm},
\emph{taqwā}, \emph{ḥilm}) and S6 (the social-structural pathologies the
text most frequently denounces) --- with S3--5 as middle-trajectory
states of lower independent salience. A flat trend test, given this
structure, is the prediction the theory generates, not an embarrassment
to it.

\subsubsection{4.3. Stage 1 --- Pre-anger
displeasure}\label{stage-1-pre-anger-displeasure}

The lower bound of the continuum is occupied by two roots ---
\emph{ʾ-f-f} and \emph{k-r-h} --- that mark the \emph{pre-anger}
register: vocal irritation and inner aversion respectively.

\textbf{4.3.1. \emph{Uff} (3; all Meccan).} \emph{Uff} (gloss:
\emph{taḍajjur}, ``irritation'') is the Qur'anic register's lowest
verbal expression of displeasure. Its three attestations occupy
paradigmatic moral positions: sūrat al-Isrāʾ (Q. 17:23) prohibits saying
\emph{uff} to one's parents (the \emph{lowest} sign of displeasure
morally weighted at the \emph{highest} relational stake); sūrat
al-Anbiyāʾ (Q. 21:67) places it in Abraham's mouth confronting
idol-worshippers (prophetic remonstrance); sūrat al-Aḥqāf (Q. 46:17)
ascribes it to a rebellious child reproaching believing parents (the
inverse). \emph{Uff} anchors the lower boundary of the spectrum at a
register \emph{below} anger proper but already morally accountable.

\textbf{4.3.2. \emph{Karh} (41; 14M/27Md; raw \emph{p} ≈ 1.0 × 10⁻⁷,
Holm-adj. \emph{p} ≈ 1.4 × 10⁻⁶ --- survives FDR).} \emph{Karh} exhibits
a \emph{twofold structure}: (a) \textbf{inner \emph{karāha} (dislike)}
--- pre-anger evaluative dislike, framed as \emph{reformable} by sūrat
al-Baqara (Q. 2:216, \emph{ʿasā an takrahū shayʾan wa-huwa khayrun
lakum}); the Stage-1 anger-spectrum sense. (b) \textbf{Structural
\emph{ikrāh} (coercion)} --- not an emotion but a structural extension
of distaste into compulsion (sūrat al-Baqara, Q. 2:256, \emph{lā ikrāha
fī al-dīn}; sūrat al-Naḥl, Q. 16:106; sūrat al-Nūr, Q. 24:33). We
include \emph{karh} on the strength of its \emph{karāha} sense. The
Medinan-tilted distribution survives Holm-Bonferroni correction
emphatically --- \emph{karh} is one of three roots, alongside
\emph{baghy} and \emph{ghayẓ}, whose distributional asymmetry is not
attributable to multiple-comparison artefact; the structural
\emph{ikrāh} sense in Medinan community-discipline contexts (consent in
marriage, freedom in religion) accounts for the heavy Medinan weighting.

\subsubsection{4.4. Stage 2 --- Inner pressure and
contraction}\label{stage-2-inner-pressure-and-contraction}

Stage 2 brings together three roots --- \emph{ḍ-y-q}, \emph{ḥ-z-n},
\emph{ʾ-s-f} --- with sixty combined attestations.

\textbf{4.4.1. \emph{Ḍīq} (13; 9M/4Md).} Ibn Fāris glosses \emph{ḍyq} as
``constriction, narrowness''; al-Rāghib in opposition to \emph{saʿah}
(expansion). The root appears predominantly in the construction
\emph{ḍīq al-ṣadr} (constriction of the chest), localising the
experience to the embodied site. Sūrat al-Ḥijr (Q. 15:97) frames it as
the prophetic response to social rejection. Al-Ṭabāṭabāʾī
(\emph{al-Mīzān} 12:195) observes the verse expresses divine
\emph{knowledge} of prophetic \emph{ḍīq} rather than prohibition: a
phenomenologically natural, theologically licit state, not a moral
failing. Morphological profile: 8 verbs / 3 nouns / 1 participle ---
registering a transient, situationally-induced state.

\textbf{4.4.1.1. \emph{Ḍāqa dharʿan} --- inhibited aggression at Lot,
sūrat Hūd, Q. 11:77/80.} The compound idiom \emph{ḍāqa bihim dharʿan}
(``his arm-span tightened by them'') in the Lot narrative figures
\emph{post-anger blocked aggression} --- anger at action-threshold but
structurally inhibited. The four-step trajectory (trigger \emph{sīʾa
bihim} → constriction \emph{ḍyq+dharʿ} → aspirational voicing \emph{law
anna lī bikum quwwatan} → transfer of agency to the divine through the
angels' arrival) shows that \emph{ḍyq} sits at \emph{both} poles of the
spectrum: pre-anger contraction \emph{and} upper-pressure threshold of
inhibition. The Qur'anic narrative theologises inhibited aggression:
when the wronged agent lacks power, divine agency completes the anger
trajectory.

\textbf{4.4.2. \emph{Ḥuzn} (42; 26M/16Md).} The most frequent and most
verbal Stage-2 lexeme (37v / 5n = 88\% verbal). Ibn Manẓūr preserves the
etymologically primary ``rugged, uneven ground'' (\emph{al-arḍ
al-ġalīẓah}); the metaphorical extension figures grief as inner
roughness (anticipating Kövecses 2000 on EMOTION IS A LANDSCAPE
schemas). Sūrat al-Baqara (Q. 2:38) places \emph{ḥuzn} in syntagmatic
pair with \emph{khawf} and opposes both to divinely-given guidance ---
\emph{ḥuzn} as the affective signature of \emph{separation from
guidance}. \textbf{Caveat (transversality)}: \emph{ḥuzn} is \emph{not}
strictly anger-derived. It has four valences --- standalone grief over
loss (sūrat Yūsuf, Q. 12:84, Jacob over Joseph), externally-induced
sorrow from being wronged (prophetic grief at rejection), positive moral
compunction (the affective register of \emph{tawba}), and anger turned
inward when externally inhibited (closest to the Stage-2 placement
here). Inclusion is justified by corpus density and proximity of (b) and
(d) to the anger field. The 88\% verbal profile reinforces the Stage-2
generalisation: inner-pressure lexemes denote transient, regulable
states.

\textbf{4.4.3. \emph{Asaf} (5; 5M/0Md).} The least frequent Stage-2
lexeme. Al-Rāghib glosses as the conjunction of grief and anger
(\emph{al-jamʿ bayna al-ḥuzn wa-l-ġaḍab}) --- at the threshold between
inner pressure and active anger. Sūrat al-Zukhruf (Q. 43:55,
\emph{fa-lammā āsafūnā intaqamnā minhum}) frames \emph{asaf} as the
immediate causal precursor of divine retribution; al-Ṭabarsī
(\emph{Majmaʿ} 9:82) glosses \emph{al-asaf} here as ``the intense anger
arising from prior grief.'' The exclusive-Meccan distribution (5/0; raw
\emph{p} = 0.34, adj. \emph{p} = 1.00) is suggestive but evidentially
weak --- at \emph{n} = 5 the test has very low power. We register the
5/0 distribution as a \emph{contextual observation} warranting follow-up
at higher \emph{n}, not as a confirmed empirical finding.

\textbf{4.4.3.1. \emph{Ghaḍbān asifan} --- sacred anger at Moses, sūrat
al-Aʿrāf, Q. 7:150 / sūrat Ṭāhā, Q. 20:86.} The compound \emph{ghaḍbān
asifan}, applied to Moses on his return to find the calf-worshippers,
generates a three-layer dialectic: \emph{ghaḍab} is outward/kinetic
(throwing the tablets, seizing Aaron); \emph{asaf} is inward/potential
(compassionate grief over the relapse). \emph{Asaf} grounds
\emph{ghaḍab}: absent its compassionate ground, Moses' anger would be
violence; present, it is anger in service of guidance.

\textbf{4.4.4. Network evidence for Stage-2 coherence.} The aya-level
co-occurrence network (Figure 5) reveals that the strongest single edge
connects \emph{ḥzn} and \emph{ḍyq} (weight 3); these two lexemes share
three verses in the Qur'an, more than any other pair within the
spectrum. Centrality analysis (Figure 6) places \emph{ḥzn} among the
highest-degree nodes (weighted degree 5, tied with \emph{ġḍb}).

\subsubsection{4.5. Stage 3 --- Evaluative
aversion}\label{stage-3-evaluative-aversion}

Stage 3 brings together three roots --- \emph{n-q-m}, \emph{s-kh-ṭ},
\emph{m-q-t} --- with twenty-seven combined attestations. Each marks a
distinct mode of \emph{evaluative-aversive} response to a moral object.
A note on internal ordering: \emph{maqt} is the lexicographic
superlative of \emph{aversion} (Zajjāj, \emph{ashaddu al-ibghāḍ}) but is
placed \emph{between} \emph{sakhaṭ} and \emph{ghaḍab} on the
\emph{action-intensity} axis because the paper's dimension is intensity
of \emph{action}, not intensity of \emph{aversion}. \emph{Maqt} is
purely evaluative --- rejection-distance rather than approach-aggression
--- and on the action axis sits below the kinetic-motor \emph{ghaḍab} of
Stage 4 and above the settled \emph{sakhaṭ} of Stage 3.

\textbf{4.5.1. \emph{Naqm} (17 attestations; 12 Meccan / 5 Medinan).}
\emph{Naqm} is the lexeme of \emph{vengeful disapproval}: anger that is
grounded in moral evaluation and oriented toward retribution. Sūrat
al-Aʿrāf (Q. 7:126, \emph{wa-mā naqamū minnā illā an āmannā bi-āyāti
rabbinā}) is the linguistic exemplar. The lexeme generates
\emph{al-muntaqim} as a divine attribute (sūrat al-Sajda, Q. 32:22 and
parallels). Position: \emph{naqm} sits between \emph{sakhaṭ} and
\emph{ghaḍab} on the action axis. The Meccan tilt (12/5) is consistent
with the lexeme's frequent appearance in narrative-prophetic passages.

\textbf{4.5.2. \emph{Sakhaṭ} (4 attestations; 0 Meccan / 4 Medinan).}
Despite its low frequency, \emph{sakhaṭ} furnishes one of the paper's
principal distributional findings. The exclusive-Medinan distribution
has a two-sided exact binomial \emph{p}-value of \textbf{0.005} against
the corpus-derived baseline of 0.74 Meccan probability.

\textbf{\emph{Baseline derivation.}} The 0.74 Meccan-rate baseline is
the proportion of \emph{ayat} (verses, not suras) classified Meccan in
the QAC sura metadata. We use the ayat-weighted rate rather than the
sura-count rate (\textasciitilde0.69) because an attestation drawn at
uniform random from the corpus hits an aya, not a sura.

their Medinan-period exegesis --- sūrat Muḥammad (Q. 47:28,
\emph{ittabaʿū mā askhaṭa Allāh wa-karihū riḍwānahu}) is canonical,
though al-Ṭabarī notes that sūrat al-Māʾida (Q. 5:80) is exegetically
discussed as directed at the People of the Book rather than the
\emph{Munāfiqūn} properly. Following Narimani, Iqbali and Chari (2021),
we read \emph{sakhaṭ} not as an instantaneous emotional surge but as
sustained \emph{ethical disapprobation}; we propose:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Hypothesis (Discursive Specialisation of \emph{sakhaṭ}):}
\emph{sakhaṭ} operates as a discursively specialised lexeme, reserved
for divine displeasure with the \emph{Munāfiqūn} --- agents whose
\emph{ẓāhir} departs from their \emph{bāṭin}. Since the \emph{Munāfiqūn}
category is constitutively a Medinan-period phenomenon, \emph{sakhaṭ}
attests exclusively in Medinan suras. This is one instance of a general
phenomenon of \emph{discursive-contextual lexical specialisation} in the
Qur'anic register, of which \emph{karh} (Medinan-community discipline)
and the umbrella patterns \emph{jrm} (Meccan) / \emph{fsq} (Medinan) are
convergent instances.
\end{quote}

\textbf{4.5.3. \emph{Maqt} (6 attestations; 4 Meccan / 2 Medinan).}
\emph{Maqt} is \emph{intense moral aversion}: the four lexica converge
on \emph{ashaddu al-ibghāḍ} (Zajjāj / al-Layth in \emph{Tahdhīb
al-Lugha}); al-Rāghib grounds it specifically in observation of a
reprehensible act (\emph{ʿan amrin qabīḥin rakibahu}), making
\emph{maqt} purely evaluative. Canonical exemplars --- sūrat Ghāfir (Q.
40:10, \emph{maqt al-nafs} doubled), sūrat al-Nisāʾ (Q. 4:22,
institutional moral aversion in forbidden marriages), sūrat Fāṭir (Q.
35:39, rising divine \emph{maqt} against disbelievers), sūrat al-Ṣaff
(Q. 61:3, saying-without-doing) --- stabilise the lexeme as the Qur'anic
register's term for moral aversion as evaluative response, distinct from
the kinetic-motor \emph{ghaḍab} of Stage 4.

\subsubsection{4.6. Stage 4 --- Active
anger}\label{stage-4-active-anger}

Stage 4 (25 attestations) covers active, motor-mobilising anger.

\textbf{4.6.1. \emph{Ghaḍab} (24; 13M/11Md).} The central anger lexeme
of the Qur'an, with near-balanced Meccan/Medinan distribution marking it
as the unmarked default. The four lexica gloss it as inner agitation of
the heart oriented toward retribution (al-Rāghib) or inner motion
disposing the agent to action (Ibn Fāris). Predicated balance-fully of
God (sūrat al-Fatḥ, Q. 48:6), prophets, and believers. Morphologically
nominal (16n / 6v / 2 ptcp = 67\% nominal vs \emph{ḥuzn}'s 88\% verbal):
\emph{ghaḍab} is a \emph{structural concept-state} with organisational
standing rather than a transient affect. Al-Rāzī's \emph{Mafātīḥ
al-Ghayb} glosses divine \emph{ghaḍab} in the register of \emph{irādat
al-ʿuqūbah} (paraphrase) --- a normative-juridical orientation, not an
emotional response. Network analysis places \emph{ghḍb} among the top
three for betweenness centrality (point estimate 0.17).

\textbf{4.6.2. \emph{Ḥard} (sūrat al-Qalam, Q. 68:25, hapax).} A
Qur'anic hapax in the Garden-Owners parable (\emph{wa-ghadaw ʿalā ḥardin
qādirīn}): garden-owners who vow in the night to harvest before dawn so
as to deny the poor their share. Classical exegesis converges on a dual
gloss: al-Ṭabarsī's \emph{Majmaʿ al-Bayān} reads \emph{ḥard} as both
\emph{ghaḍab} (anger) and \emph{qaṣd} (purposive intent);
al-Ṭabāṭabāʾī's \emph{al-Mīzān} refines as anger-plus-deliberate-denial
--- purposive anger weaponised through resource-denial. The narrative
consequence is the destruction of the garden by night-storm: divine
retribution against the \emph{ḥard}-driven plan to deny.

\subsubsection{4.7. Stage 5 --- Compressed/explosive
rage}\label{stage-5-compressedexplosive-rage}

\textbf{4.7.1. \emph{Ghayẓ} and the \emph{kaẓm} metaphor (11
attestations; 3M/8Md; raw \emph{p} = 1.6 × 10⁻³, Holm-adj. ≈ 1.9 × 10⁻²
--- survives FDR).} The four lexica converge: \emph{ghayẓ} is
``compressed contained anger'' (al-Rāghib), ``the highest concentration
of restrained anger'' (Mostafavi), ``filling of the self with restrained
anger'' (Ibn Manẓūr) --- pressurised contents of a sealed vessel. Sūrat
Āl ʿImrān (Q. 3:134) commends \emph{al-kāẓimīn al-ghayẓ}; the verb
\emph{kaẓm} derives from the act of tying off a waterskin, the
agent-internal regulation that sustains containment. The construction
itself presupposes that the believers being commended \emph{experience}
the \emph{ghayẓ} they suppress --- the Narimani et al.~claim that
\emph{ghayẓ} in human reference is exclusive to unbelievers/hypocrites
must therefore be qualified: \emph{ghayẓ} in human attribution is
\emph{predominantly} --- not exclusively --- negative, with Q. 3:134
(\emph{wa-l-kāẓimīn al-ghayẓa}) the canonical exception (transitive
\emph{ghayẓ directed at} believers is exclusively
non-believer-attributed; intransitive/experiential \emph{ghayẓ} --- the
\emph{kāẓimīn} themselves --- is attributed to believers). Read through
Lakoff and Kövecses (1987), the \emph{ghayẓ--kaẓm} pair instantiates
ANGER IS A PRESSURIZED CONTAINER with precision: \emph{ghayẓ} the
contained fluid, \emph{kaẓm} the agent's act of sealing (Figure 7). The
Medinan tilt (8/3) survives FDR and tracks the discursive context: inner
anger management becomes a salient pedagogical concern in the formative
Medinan community.

\textbf{4.7.2. \emph{Tamayyuz}: container collapse in sūrat al-Mulk, Q.
67:8 (manifestation, not separate root).} Sūrat al-Mulk (Q. 67:8) ---
\emph{takādu tamayyazu min al-ġayẓ} (``{[}the fire{]} is on the verge of
parting asunder from rage'') --- is the single most articulated image of
rage in the Qur'an. \emph{Tamayyaza} (root \emph{m-y-z}: in
\emph{Mufradāt} and \emph{Lisān} the dominant gloss is \emph{al-tafrīq
bayna al-mukhtaliṭ}, ``separating things that have been mixed''; in the
present context, the parting / severing of the contents from each other
or of the vessel from itself) figures not the \emph{exit} of pressurised
content (as in English \emph{she blew her top}, \emph{he exploded},
\emph{I lost it}) but the \emph{structural integrity of the container
itself failing}. We read this as the authors' CMT-grounded
interpretation, complementing the classical ``separation of
constituents'' gloss attested in al-Ṭabarī and al-Zamakhsharī, rather
than as the plain lexicographic sense. We treat \emph{tamayyuz} as a
manifestation of Stage 5 rather than a distinct core root, retaining the
fourteen-root inventory. H5 is corroborated in the form: Q. 67:8
instantiates the container-collapse failure mode with greater lexical
transparency than the English exemplars in Lakoff and Kövecses's corpus.

\subsubsection{4.8. Stage 6 --- Behavioural outcomes (with
caveats)}\label{stage-6-behavioural-outcomes-with-caveats}

\textbf{4.8.1. \emph{Baghy} (96; 55M/41Md; raw \emph{p} = 4.1 × 10⁻⁴,
Holm-adj. ≈ 5.3 × 10⁻³ --- survives FDR).} The most frequent lexeme of
the spectrum and the principal bridge node by point-estimate centrality.
Glossed as ``seeking beyond rightful bounds'' (al-Rāghib) and
``aggressive seeking with intent to oppress'' (Mostafavi). Sūrat
al-Shūrā (Q. 42:42) collocates \emph{baghy} with \emph{ẓulm}, embedding
it in the moral-evaluation register. Ibn ʿĀshūr clarifies
(\emph{al-Taḥrīr} 25:126): \emph{baghy} and \emph{ẓulm} are
near-synonymous, except \emph{baghy} is specific to non-rightful
aggression against another while \emph{ẓulm} covers all injustice.

\textbf{4.8.2. \emph{Baghy} as bridge node (with bootstrap
uncertainty).} \emph{Baghy} attains the highest point-estimate closeness
centrality (0.55) and is tied with \emph{ghaḍab} (0.53) and \emph{asaf}
(0.49) for the highest betweenness centrality (0.15). Bootstrap 95\%
percentile CIs (1,000 ayat-resamples) are wide --- for \emph{baghy}:
betweenness ∈ {[}0.00, 0.31{]}; closeness ∈ {[}0.23, 0.84{]};
eigenvector ∈ {[}0.00, 0.68{]} --- reflecting the small graph (14 nodes)
and modest edge density. (Nodes with zero in-graph degree ---
\emph{ʾff}, \emph{mqt}, \emph{ḥrd}, \emph{ʿtw} --- are trivially placed
at the centrality floor with CI {[}0.00, 0.00{]}.) On strict
non-overlap, no node's centrality is significantly greater than
another's. The bridging-role claim must therefore be qualified:
\emph{baghy} leads on three of four point-estimate measures, but the
bootstrap does not rule out alternatives. What is \emph{robust} at the
aggregate level is the cross-field connectivity to the umbrella moral
terms (Table 4): \emph{baghy} shows sixteen aya-level co-occurrences
with \emph{ẓlm/jrm/fsq/ʿdw/šnʾ} combined, an integer-count pattern
independent of centrality ranking. H4 is \emph{consistent with} point
estimates, qualified by bootstrap.

distinguishable from chance under the binomial.

\textbf{4.8.5. Bidirectional causation and a \emph{tafsīr}-grounded
audit.} \emph{Baghy} arises frequently from greed and \emph{ʿulw fī
al-arḍ} (cf.~sūrat al-Anʿām, Q. 6:21); \emph{ṭughyān} ---
paradigmatically Pharaonic (sūrat al-Nāziʿāt, Q. 79:17) --- is rooted in
structural arrogance, with sūrat al-Shuʿarāʾ (Q. 26:55, \emph{innahum
lanā la-ghāʾiẓūn}) reversing polarity to show oppressor-anger arising
\emph{from} the rebellion of the oppressed; \emph{ʿutuww} attaches to
\emph{istikbār} as settled character. The causation arrow is therefore
\emph{bidirectional}. To operationalise this, each of the 145 Stage-6
attestations was coded against the joint reading of \emph{al-Mīzān},
\emph{al-Kashshāf}, \emph{Majmaʿ al-Bayān}, and \emph{al-Taḥrīr
wa-l-Tanwīr} under a three-label codebook --- \textbf{A} (anger-origin:
the immediate exegetical antecedent is an anger-type affective state),
\textbf{S} (structural-origin: the antecedent is
\emph{istikbār}/\emph{ʿulw}/greed without explicit anger antecedent), or
\textbf{A+S} (both antecedents explicitly present). Two independent
graduate-level coders (the corresponding author and an Arabic-literature
graduate trained on the codebook with relevant commentary excerpts
supplied) coded all 145 attestations independently; disagreements (n =
14) were reconciled in a joint session against the four commentaries,
with original-pass codings used for κ computation. Cohen's κ = 0.79 ---
\emph{substantial} agreement (Landis and Koch 1977). Full coding at
\texttt{data/concordance/stage6\_causation\_coding.csv}. Results: A = 38
(26\%), S = 71 (49\%), A+S = 36 (25\%); by root, \emph{baghy}
22A/50S/24A+S, \emph{ṭughyān} 11A/19S/9A+S, \emph{ʿutuww} 5A/2S/3A+S
(sub-counts sum to root totals). Re-running χ²(1) on A-only (Stages 1--5
= 167 vs.~anger-derived Stage-6 = 38) yields χ²(1) = 81.4, \emph{p}
\textless{} 0.001 --- the phenomenological core dominates anger-derived
outcomes \textasciitilde4.4×. The full-Stage-6 near-balance (χ²(1) =
1.55) is therefore explained: S-attestations belong to an adjacent
moral-anthropological field of \emph{ẓulm-istikbār-ʿulw} that the corpus
pervasively documents, and \emph{baghy}/\emph{ṭughyān}/\emph{ʿutuww} are
not coextensive with ``anger outcomes'' in the strict sense.

\subsubsection{4.11. Summary of computational corroboration analyses

Material)}\label{summary-of-computational-corroboration-analyses-full-detail-in-supplementary-material}

Four computational analyses --- extending the main pipeline with
neural-language-model and information-theoretic measurements over the
same 312 attestations --- are reported in full in the Supplementary

the spectrum: affective-core lexemes (\emph{ghaḍab}, \emph{ḥuzn},
\emph{baghy}) cluster low (drift \textless{} 0.20), evaluative-aversion
and containment-pressure lexemes (\emph{sakhaṭ}, \emph{maqt},
\emph{ḍyq}, \emph{ghayẓ}) cluster high (\textgreater{} 0.22), and
\emph{ṭughyān} tops the non-hapax distribution --- convergent with the
§4.10 levelling observation. \textbf{S2 (CAMeLBERT-CA
contextual-embedding clustering)} finds silhouette = −0.082, ARI =
0.059, NMI = 0.077 against the six-stage labelling: a clear null in
which the model clusters by root identity rather than by stage, taken as
evidence that the spectrum adds analytical structure beyond the language
model's distributional view. \textbf{S3 (PMI-weighted co-occurrence
network)} corrects the §4.8 unweighted graph for marginal-frequency
artefacts: the \emph{asaf}--\emph{ghaḍab} pairing (npmi = 0.59) emerges

(Nöldekean diachronic null)} finds Spearman ρ = −0.157 (95\% CI via
Fisher's z: {[}−0.265, −0.046{]}; \emph{p} = 0.006) against revelation
order --- a small effect (\textless{} 3\% of variance) and substantively
a null, consistent with the claim that the six-stage taxonomy organises
by \emph{intensity}, not by \emph{chronology}.

\subsection{5. Discussion}\label{discussion}

\subsubsection{5.1. The integrated logic of
escalation}\label{the-integrated-logic-of-escalation}

The findings cohere into a single structural claim: the Qur'anic lexicon
of the anger spectrum encodes a \textbf{logic of anger escalation}
operating along three dimensions --- locus (inner → outer), intensity
(low → high), and scope (individual → structural). The lexemes are not
synonyms but \emph{grades} ordered by an intelligible cognitive logic.

In compact form: \emph{Pre-anger displeasure → Inner pressure →
Evaluative aversion → Active anger → Compressed rage → Behavioural
outcomes (with caveats)}. The arrows index not mere temporal succession
but conditional intensification at each step where regulation may
succeed or fail. Stage 6 is distinctive: the move into behavioural
outcomes is \emph{not} a deterministic escalation from anger but is
influenced by independent vectors (greed, \emph{istikbār}, structural
arrogance) --- which is why we read the near-balance between S1--5 (167)
and S6 (145) as theoretically informative rather than paradoxical.

\subsubsection{5.2. Convergence with contemporary emotion
psychology}\label{convergence-with-contemporary-emotion-psychology}

The six-stage continuum maps onto four families of contemporary emotion
theory at points of structural rather than psychometric convergence,
sufficient to ground the Islamic-psychology implications of §5.7 and to
identify where the Qur'anic lexicon \emph{extends} the contemporary
models.

\textbf{Gross's process model.} Gross (1998, 2014, 2015) partitions
emotion regulation into five sequential strategies: \emph{situation
selection}, \emph{situation modification}, \emph{attentional
deployment}, \emph{cognitive change}, and \emph{response modulation}.
The Qur'anic continuum partitions the trajectory more finely on the
affective side: \textbf{S1} (\emph{uff}, \emph{karāha}) ↔
\emph{situation selection}; \textbf{S2} (\emph{ḍīq}, \emph{ḥuzn},
\emph{asaf}) ↔ \emph{situation modification}; \textbf{S3} (\emph{naqm},
\emph{sakhaṭ}, \emph{maqt}) ↔ \emph{attentional deployment} and
\emph{cognitive change}; \textbf{S4} (\emph{ghaḍab}, \emph{ḥard}) ↔
\emph{response selection}, the kinetic-motor enactment; \textbf{S5}
(\emph{ghayẓ}/\emph{kaẓm}; \emph{tamayyuz}) ↔ \emph{response
modulation}, with \emph{kaẓm} operationalising agent-internal
\emph{suppression / inhibition} of expression --- one strategy among
several available in Gross's typology (Gross 2015) --- and
\emph{tamayyuz} (Q. 67:8) its container-collapse failure mode.
\textbf{S6} (\emph{baghy}, \emph{ṭughyān}, \emph{ʿutuww}) lies beyond
Gross's frame: process-model regulation concerns the \emph{emotional
episode}, but Stage 6 lexicalises \emph{structural-moral pathologies of
personhood} outliving any single episode --- with the §4.8.5 caveat that
Stage 6 has bidirectional causation with the spectrum.

\textbf{Plutchik's intensity wheel; Spielberger's STAXI architecture.}
Plutchik (1980, 2001) organises anger on a three-level escalation
(\emph{annoyance → anger → rage}); the Qur'anic continuum \emph{refines}
this by partitioning \emph{annoyance} into vocal irritation (\emph{uff},
S1), inner aversion (\emph{karāha}, S1), and inner pressure (\emph{ḍīq},
\emph{ḥuzn}, \emph{asaf}, S2) --- three Qur'anic stages where Plutchik
has one label --- and by inserting the \emph{compressed-rage} register
(\emph{ghayẓ}, S5) between \emph{anger} (≈ S4 \emph{ghaḍab}) and the
unmarked terminal \emph{rage}. Spielberger's STAXI-2 (1999) further
partitions anger into \emph{trait}, \emph{state}, and \emph{control}.
The Qur'anic lexicon realises this trichotomy at the lexical surface:
trait-like sustained states (\emph{ḥuzn} at 88\% verbal registering
recurrent affective episodes, \emph{ḍīq}, \emph{karāha}); state-like
situational responses (\emph{sakhaṭ}, \emph{maqt}, \emph{ghaḍab}); and
\emph{kaẓm} (Q. 3:134) as the Qur'anic \emph{anger-control} lexeme,
operationally close to Spielberger's \emph{anger-in} / suppression
construct with the addition of explicit moral commendation.

\textbf{Appraisal theory: Scherer's checks and Roseman's
\emph{ṭughyān}-pattern.} Appraisal theory (Frijda 1986; Lazarus 1991;
Scherer 2001, 2005; Roseman 1996) holds that emotional response is
determined not by stimulus alone but by cognitive \emph{appraisal} along
a defined set of dimensions. Scherer's component-process model specifies
four sequential checks; the Qur'anic Stage-3 lexemes operate as a
lexicon for the normative-significance check at progressively higher
intensities (\emph{naqm} → \emph{sakhaṭ} → \emph{maqt}). Sūrat al-ʿAlaq
(Q. 96:6--7) conditions \emph{ṭughyān} on the appraisal of
\emph{istighnāʾ}: the agent perceives high coping potential, low
external constraint, and self-agency --- the \emph{agency-self /
motive-consistent / high-control} pattern Roseman would predict for
defiance. The Qur'anic lexicon thus encodes --- as architectural design
rather than theoretical statement --- insights contemporary emotion
science has reached independently.

\subsubsection{5.3. The phenomenology/outcomes
balance}\label{the-phenomenologyoutcomes-balance}

A naive ten-root reading once suggested 2.4× more attestations at the
behavioural pole than at Stages 1--3 combined, indicating discursive
over-emphasis on outcomes. The revised fourteen-root inventory (167 /
145; χ²(1) = 1.55, fail to reject) tells a different story: the expanded
phenomenological vocabulary (\emph{uff}, \emph{karh}, \emph{naqm},
\emph{maqt}, \emph{ḥard}) brings the lower stages into balance with
Stage 6, as one would expect if (i) the phenomenological side is richer
than prior analysis allowed, and (ii) Stage-6 lexemes are not
exclusively anger-derived but stand in bidirectional causal relation to
anger. In Fauconnier and Turner's (2002: 17--57) terms, the Stage-6
lexemes operate as a conceptual blend whose input spaces are
wrong-doing-as-supremacism and anger-as-pressure, with the blend
inheriting causal vectors from both inputs and running them
bidirectionally. The methodological lesson: corpus-distributional
analyses of religious texts are maps of \emph{discursive emphasis},
entangled with category-membership and category-causation decisions.

\subsubsection{5.4. Discursive-contextual
specialisation}\label{discursive-contextual-specialisation}

The FDR-surviving Medinan tilt of \emph{karh} (raw \emph{p} ≈ 10⁻⁷, adj.
≈ 10⁻⁶), the borderline-FDR Medinan exclusivity of \emph{sakhaṭ} (adj.
\emph{p} = 0.050), the FDR-surviving Medinan tilt of \emph{ghayẓ} (adj.
≈ 1.9 × 10⁻²), the FDR-surviving Meccan tilt of \emph{baghy} (adj. ≈ 5.3
× 10⁻³), alongside the contextual-only Meccan tilts of \emph{asaf},
\emph{naqm}, and \emph{ʿutuww}, the hapax-Meccan \emph{ḥard}, and the
strong umbrella patterns \emph{jrm} (Meccan) and \emph{fsq} (Medinan),
instantiate \textbf{discursive-contextual specialisation}: members of
multi-lexeme semantic fields preferentially attaching to specific
discursive registers. The pattern is not random --- \emph{sakhaṭ}
attaches to the \emph{Munāfiqūn} (a Medinan phenomenon), \emph{karh} to
the structural \emph{ikrāh} idiom of Medinan community discipline,
\emph{ghayẓ} to the \emph{kaẓm} pedagogy of Medinan ethics, \emph{asaf}
to prior-prophet narratives (Meccan-rhetorical), \emph{jrm} to
anti-\emph{shirk} polemic (Meccan), \emph{fsq} to intra-community
discipline (Medinan). El-Awa (2006) on textual relations and Sinai
(2017, 2023) on Meccan/Medinan diachrony supply the theoretical
framework: specialisation is exactly the kind of pattern one would
expect from the Qur'an's gradual response to evolving sociolinguistic
contexts. The phenomenon is, to our knowledge, undocumented as a
systematic corpus-distributional pattern in Qur'anic-linguistic
scholarship; future work should test the generalisation across other
multi-lexeme fields, in dialogue with QurAna/QurSim (Sharaf and Atwell
2012a, 2012b), QuranMorph (Akra, Hammouda and Jarrar 2025), and MASAQ
(Sawalha et al.~2024).

\subsubsection{5.5. Q. 67:8 and the container metaphor: a focused
comparative
survey}\label{q.-678-and-the-container-metaphor-a-focused-comparative-survey}

Lakoff and Kövecses (1987: 195--221) reconstructed American-English
anger as a \emph{five-stage prototype scenario} --- (1) offending event,
(2) anger, (3) attempt at control, (4) loss of control, (5) act of
retribution --- with the PRESSURIZED CONTAINER schema mapped onto stages
(2)--(4). English exemplars lexicalise the stage-(4) failure-mode as the
\emph{exit of contents} with the container preserved (\emph{blew his
top}, \emph{flipped his lid} --- the rupturing component is peripheral,
not the body of the vessel). The Qur'anic \emph{ghayẓ--kaẓm--tamayyuz}
triad maps onto the same stages (2)--(4) with a precision the English
exemplars do not match at stage (4): \emph{ghayẓ} is glossed as
compressed contained anger (stage 2); \emph{kaẓm} (Q. 3:134) names the
agent-internal suppression operation (stage 3); sūrat al-Mulk (Q. 67:8,
\emph{takādu tamayyazu min al-ġayẓ}) lexicalises \emph{the parting /
severing of the vessel itself} (stage 4), figuring the fire-vessel of
Jahannam parting structurally from the rage of its contents rather than
the exit of contents from an intact vessel.

A focused six-tradition survey contextualises the strength of this
claim. \textbf{Akkadian} anchors anger in the \emph{libbu} and
\emph{kabattu} as filled containers (\emph{libbāti malû}, CAD L
163--164; Bodi 2018); recent bodily-mapping work on Neo-Assyrian texts
(Lauri et al.~2024) confirms a dominantly FILLING / HEAT-IN-CONTAINER
schema. \textbf{Biblical Hebrew} centres on \emph{ḥēmâ} (heat/fury,
frequently the object of \emph{šāphakh} ``to pour out'') and the
formulaic \emph{ḥarôn-ʾaph}; Kruger (2000, 2015) demonstrates that ANGER
IS FLUID IN A CONTAINER and ANGER IS FIRE IN THE NOSE are the dominant
metaphors of the Hebrew Bible, both schematically EXIT-typed. The
instructive exception is Job 32:19, where Elihu declares his belly
\emph{kĕ-yayin lōʾ-yippātēaḥ \ldots{} yibbāqēaʿ} --- ``like wine that is
not opened \ldots{} about to burst like new wineskins'': the verb
\emph{bāqaʿ} names exactly the structural failure of the vessel that the
Qur'anic \emph{tamayyaza} names. We therefore qualify our claim --- Q.
67:8 is \emph{an early and unusually transparent} lexicalisation of
container collapse, not a unique one. \textbf{Homeric Greek} uses
\emph{kholos} (bile, rising) and \emph{mēnis} (ritualised wrath);
Muellner (1996) and Cairns (2003) read both as fluid-and-heat schemas,
with no Iliadic image of vessel rupture. \textbf{Vedic and Classical
Sanskrit} lexicalise anger principally as \emph{krodha} and
\emph{manyu}, the latter personified as a martial deity associated with
Indra (ṚV 10.83--84); the dominant images are FIRE and WEAPON-RELEASE,
not vessel-disintegration. \textbf{Classical and Modern Chinese}
lexicalises anger through \emph{nù}, \emph{fèn}, and \emph{qì} (Yu
1995); Kövecses, Benczes and Szelid (2025) identify ANGER IS HOT
\emph{QÌ} IN A CONTAINER as the central schema. \textbf{Pre-Islamic
\emph{Jāhilī} Arabic} already deploys \emph{ḥamiya} ``to be heated'' and
\emph{ġalā} ``to boil'' for hostile arousal, alongside \emph{ġayẓ} in
the cognate fluid-heat register; the lexical groundwork for Q. 67:8 is
genuinely Arabian.

Five of the six traditions concentrate anger-imagery around
\emph{filling}, \emph{heating}, \emph{fluid-rising}, and \emph{exit /
overflow}; the \textbf{Hebrew Bible alone} offers a clear pre-Qur'anic
parallel to vessel-rupture proper (Job 32:19). We accordingly hold our
claim at \textbf{``an early and unusually transparent''} lexicalisation
of container collapse, with Job 32:19 acknowledged as a structurally
cognate Northwest-Semitic precedent (though Akkadian \emph{kabattu
napāḫu} ``liver bursting'', Greek Iliadic \emph{cholos} swelling
imagery, and Vedic \emph{manyu} combustion offer imperfect cognates of
step-(iv) container-rupture imagery that a properly stratified
cross-linguistic comparison may bring more fully into the picture). The
Qur'anic data thus \emph{strengthen} the cross-cultural universalist
hypothesis of CMT (Lakoff and Kövecses 1987; Kövecses 2000); they extend
the empirical base to seventh-century Arabic in a register that the 2025
\emph{Metaphors of ANGER} encyclopedia, which omits a dedicated
treatment of Classical Arabic, leaves un-canvassed.

\subsubsection{5.6. Implications for Qur'anic translation (conditional
on follow-up
evaluation)}\label{implications-for-quranic-translation-conditional-on-follow-up-evaluation}

Translation criticism has long observed that translations tend to level
the spectrum --- that \emph{ġaḍab} and \emph{ġayẓ} alike become
\emph{anger}; that \emph{baghy}, \emph{ṭughyān}, and \emph{ʿutuww} alike
become \emph{transgression} (Table 6). Recent computational work ---
Gaanoun and Alsuhaibani (2025) on sentiment preservation across seven
English translations, Khan et al.~(2025) on the broader
NLP-Qur'anic-studies field --- has begun to operationalise the levelling
concern at scale on coarse Ekman categories, though without a graded
reference model of the lexical spectrum against which the levelling
could be benchmarked. We frame the present discussion as
\textbf{conditional implications}: contingent on a follow-up systematic
evaluation (a stratified random sample of \textasciitilde30 verses
across the six stages, with multi-rater coding against the spectrum
reference model), the analysis would \emph{if confirmed} support the
following candidate equivalents:

\begin{quote}
\emph{uff}: vocal sigh of irritation · \emph{karh} (\emph{karāha}):
inner dislike · \emph{karh} (\emph{ikrāh}): coercion (a structural
extension, not an emotion) · \emph{ḍīq}: inner constriction · \emph{ḍāqa
dharʿan}: arm-span tightening / inhibited aggression · \emph{ḥuzn}:
persistent grief (transversal valence) · \emph{asaf}: regretful dismay
(with anger) · \emph{naqm}: vengeful disapproval · \emph{sakhaṭ}: moral
disapprobation · \emph{maqt}: intense moral aversion · \emph{ghaḍab}:
anger · \emph{ḥard}: anger combined with deliberate denial ·
\emph{ghayẓ}: contained / boiling rage · \emph{tamayyuz min al-ġayẓ}:
bursting from rage · \emph{baghy}: aggressive transgression ·
\emph{ṭughyān}: rebellious overflow · \emph{ʿutuww}: obstinate defiance.
\end{quote}

A second conditional implication is that pedagogical translations may
benefit from marking each lexeme's position on the continuum, but the
empirical case for this rests on the comparative comprehension study we
commend to future work.

\subsubsection{5.7. Implications for Islamic
psychology}\label{implications-for-islamic-psychology}

The continuum offers a culturally-grounded framework mapping onto
Gross's model without importing secular vocabulary, with three
conceptual implications: \textbf{(i) Prevention} --- early-stage
recognition through the indigenous S1--2 lexicon (\emph{uff},
\emph{karāha}, \emph{ḍīq}, \emph{ḥuzn}) supports CBT-style interception
of affective dysregulation; \textbf{(ii) Crisis intervention} ---
\emph{kaẓm} integrates with contemporary suppression-and-reappraisal
techniques; \textbf{(iii) Rehabilitation} --- post-failure Stage 6 is
framed as recoverable through \emph{tawba}, providing motivational
reinforcement for anger-disorder and post-violent-trauma rehabilitation.
These are conceptual; their translation into validated clinical
interventions awaits empirical studies we recommend as a priority.

\subsection{6. Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

\subsubsection{6.1. Substantive
contributions}\label{substantive-contributions}

This paper advances three contributions. \textbf{Methodologically}, we
have demonstrated that the integration of Izutsu's semantic-field
analysis, Kennedy--Sassoon scalar semantics, and Lakoff--Kövecses CMT
--- coupled with a reproducible computational pipeline over the QAC ---
produces a research apparatus stronger than the sum of its parts. To our
knowledge, this trifold integration with corpus-empirical validation has
not previously been deployed in Qur'anic studies, although Qāʾimīniyā
(2011, 2014) and Pakatchi and Afrashi (2020) have integrated Izutsu and
CMT.

\textbf{Descriptively}, we have produced a reproducible concordance of
312 attestations across the fourteen core spectrum roots, with
distributional and network-analytic outputs --- FDR-adjusted binomial
tests, marginal-preserving permutation null \emph{p}-values, and
bootstrap 95\% CIs on every centrality estimate --- providing a
foundation on which further philological and computational work can
build.

\textbf{Theoretically}, we have formulated and empirically defended the
\textbf{Qur'anic Action-Intensity Continuum Hypothesis}: the fourteen
core lexemes operate as a single graded continuum structured by
\emph{intensity of action} and partitioned into six phenomenologically
meaningful stages. The hypothesis is supported by qualitative evidence
from the four authoritative lexica and the broadened \emph{tafsīr}
consultation of §3.5, and by quantitative evidence from frequency,
distribution, morphology, and network structure. The κ = 0.79-validated
\emph{tafsīr}-coding audit grounds the bidirectional-causation reading
of Stage 6; the six-tradition survey of §5.5 calibrates Q. 67:8 as
\emph{early and unusually transparent} --- rather than uniquely
transparent --- lexicalisation of ``container collapse'', with Job 32:19
(\emph{bāqaʿ}) acknowledged as a structurally cognate Northwest-Semitic
precedent.

\subsubsection{6.2. Answers to the research
questions}\label{answers-to-the-research-questions}

\textbf{RQ1--RQ2.} The anger-spectrum field is a fourteen-node lexical
network organised by \emph{intensity of action}. The bridge node is
\emph{baghy} (closeness 0.55; \textasciitilde53\% of cross-field ties to
the umbrella moral-evaluation terms); stage-internal hubs are
\emph{ḥuzn} (S2) and \emph{ghaḍab} (S4). The ordering is licensed by the
trifold synthesis of §2 and shown by Table 1 to be internally encoded in
the four authoritative lexica.

\textbf{RQ3.} The empirical distribution is \emph{consistent with} ---
not by itself confirmatory of --- the continuum. The asymptotic χ²(5) =
227.15 is large, but permutation null \emph{p} = 0.24; the S1--5/S6
χ²(1) = 1.55 fails to reject, consistent with the
bidirectional-causation reading supported by the κ = 0.79 audit (A =
26\%, S = 49\%, A+S = 25\%; A-only re-analysis χ²(1) = 81.4, \emph{p}
\textless{} 0.001). Three roots survive Holm-Bonferroni emphatically
(\emph{karh}, \emph{baghy}, \emph{ghayẓ}); \emph{sakhaṭ} sits at adj.
\emph{p} = 0.0503, substantively supported by the convergence with the
\emph{Munāfiqūn}-discursive reading. The morphological gradient (verbal
S1--2 → nominal S6) supplies convergent corpus-internal evidence.

\textbf{RQ4.} The continuum is structurally homologous with Gross's
process model (S1--5), with Plutchik's intensity wheel and Spielberger's
STAXI architecture, and with Lazarus--Scherer--Roseman appraisal theory
(sūrat al-ʿAlaq, Q. 96:6--7, \emph{ṭughyān} on \emph{istighnāʾ}). It
extends beyond all four at Stage 6, qualified by the
bidirectional-causation caveat.

\textbf{RQ5.} \emph{Conditional} practical implications follow for
Qur'anic translation (§5.6) and for Islamic psychology (§5.7), framed as
hypotheses for empirical follow-up.

\subsubsection{6.3. Limitations and future
work}\label{limitations-and-future-work}

The analysis is bounded by five limitations, each pointing to a
constructive next step. (i) \textbf{Surface-lexical scope.}
Discourse-analytic close-reading of the spectrum's narrative
\emph{deployment} across suras is left for future work, in dialogue with
El-Awa (2006) and Sinai (2017, 2023). (ii) \textbf{\emph{Tafsīr}
engagement.} A \emph{tafsīr}-archive deep-dive --- systematic indexing
against the full classical/medieval/Sufi/modernist tradition
(al-Qushayrī, the \emph{Tafsīr} attributed to al-Kāshānī, Sulamī's
\emph{Ḥaqāʾiq}, Twelver, Muʿtazilī, and modernist materials), with
κ-validated multi-coder reading on contested verses --- would deepen
exegetical grounding. (iii) \textbf{Cognitive-linguistic apparatus.}
Engagement with classical Arabic \emph{bayān} theory (al-Jurjānī's
\emph{Dalāʾil al-Iʿjāz}, al-Sakkākī's \emph{Miftāḥ al-ʿUlūm}) would
supply an indigenous substrate. (iv) \textbf{\emph{Ḥadīth} integration.}
A parallel concordance over Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī, Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim, and al-Kāfī
would supply convergent evidence on \emph{kaẓm} and a cross-corpus
replication test for the underpowered \emph{asaf} 5/0 distribution. (v)
\textbf{Islamic-psychology operationalisation.} The §5.7 implications
await translation into pilot studies of \emph{kaẓm}-grounded
emotion-regulation training, validated against the STAXI and DERS. The
methodological commitment remains: computational evidence is a
\emph{complement to} --- not a replacement for --- the classical
philological and exegetical tradition.

\subsubsection{6.4. Closing remark}\label{closing-remark}

The Qur'anic lexicon encodes a sophisticated theory of the anger
spectrum --- not as explicit formulation but as the architectural design
of its semantic surface. Recovering that theory requires methodological
tools that bring together classical philological attentiveness,
contemporary linguistic-theoretical precision, and corpus-computational
verifiability. We hope the present study, alongside Izutsu, Qāʾimīniyā,
Pakatchi, Narimani et al., and Qarizadeh et al., advances this triple
integration.
