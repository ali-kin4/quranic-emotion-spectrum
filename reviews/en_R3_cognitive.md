# Reviewer 3 (Cognitive Semantics / Theoretical) — JQS
**Recommendation**: Major revision
**Confidence**: 4

## Summary of the manuscript

The manuscript proposes that fourteen Qur'anic Arabic roots (*ʾff, krh, ḍyq, ḥzn, ʾsf, nqm, sxṭ, mqt, ġḍb, ḥrd, ġyẓ, bġy, ṭġy, ʿtw*) constitute a graded semantic field organised by "intensity of action" across six phenomenological stages (pre-anger displeasure → inner pressure → evaluative aversion → active anger → compressed rage → behavioural outcomes). The theoretical apparatus is a self-described "trifold integration": Izutsu's semantic-field method (field membership), Kennedy–Sassoon scalar semantics (ordering), and Lakoff–Kövecses Conceptual Metaphor Theory (the *ghayẓ–kaẓm–tamayyuz* triad as a four-step ANGER IS PRESSURIZED CONTAINER schema, with Q. 67:8 as a transparent container-collapse exemplar). The corpus pipeline (312 attestations over QAC v0.4) supplies a uniformity χ² (asymptotic *p* < 0.001 vs permutation *p* = 0.24), a Holm-Bonferroni-surviving Medinan exclusivity for *sakhaṭ*, network centrality with bootstrap CIs, a κ = 0.79 tafsir-coding audit yielding the bidirectional-causation reading of Stage 6, three NLP extensions (multilingual-MPNet translation drift, CAMeLBERT-CA UMAP null, PMI-weighted network), and a Nöldekean-chronology null. The paper is dense, internally cross-referenced, and unusually transparent about evidential weight. The cognitive-semantic claim — that Q. 67:8 lexicalises step (iv) container failure with greater transparency than English exemplars, with Job 32:19 *bāqaʿ* as the one acknowledged Northwest-Semitic precedent — is its theoretical centre of gravity.

## Major issues

1. **The Lakoff–Kövecses (1987) four-step schema is mis-attributed in form, even where the underlying claim is broadly faithful** — §2.3, §5.5 (especially the page citations "pp. 197-198", "pp. 198-200", "pp. 200-202", "pp. 202-205"). The 1987 chapter does *not* present a clean numbered four-step "trajectory" of (i) fluid → (ii) pressure → (iii) containment → (iv) failure as the manuscript implies. Lakoff & Kövecses derive an *idealised cognitive model* (ICM) from a folk-theoretic prototype scenario with five canonical stages (offending event, anger, attempt at control, loss of control, retribution); the PRESSURIZED CONTAINER is one of several central metaphors mapped onto that scenario, alongside ANGER IS A HOT FLUID, ANGER IS FIRE, ANGER IS A WILD ANIMAL, ANGER IS INSANITY, and so on. The "container failure" step is not a discretely-enumerated (iv) but a *prototype-scenario stage 4* ("loss of control") whose lexicalisations cross multiple metaphor families. Two consequences. First, *the page-range citations in §5.5 are over-precise*: the manuscript credits pp. 197-205 with a step-by-step structure that the chapter does not present in that form, and a reviewer with the chapter to hand will catch this. Either re-cite to the *prototype scenario* (Kövecses 2000, ch. 9 reformulates it more crisply than the 1987 original) or remove the page ranges and quote the actual section headings. Second, the manuscript treats *kaẓm* + *ghayẓ* + *tamayyuz* as instantiating "the schema's" four steps when the chapter would map them onto stages 2–4 of the prototype scenario (with stage 1 = offending event, stage 5 = retribution = the manuscript's S6). Fix by reframing the central claim as: *the Qur'anic triad lexicalises stages 2–4 of the Lakoff–Kövecses prototype scenario with unusual transparency at stage 4 (loss of control)*. This is a more defensible and theoretically richer claim than the current "four-step" framing.

2. **The *kaẓm* = "regulation" mapping over-translates Gross's process-model construct** — §5.2 "Gross's process model" paragraph; §2.3 "*Kaẓm* … is the agent-internal regulation that maintains containment". *Kaẓm* in classical lexicography (*Lisān* s.v. *k-ẓ-m*; *Mufradāt*) names the *act of tying off* a waterskin — i.e., a punctual, completed action, not a process. Gross's "response modulation" is the most distal of his five strategies and explicitly *post-emotion-onset* suppression; this fits *kaẓm* reasonably well. But the manuscript then uses *kaẓm* to align with Spielberger's *anger-in* (§5.2 "Plutchik … Spielberger" paragraph), which is a *trait*-level suppression-disposition rather than a punctual act, and finally claims *kaẓm* "operationally close to Spielberger's *anger-in* / suppression construct with the addition of explicit moral commendation". This conflates three distinct things: an Arabic *act-noun* (*kaẓm*), a process-model *strategy*, and a trait-measure subscale. The CMT vocabulary the paper invokes would distinguish source-domain semantics (the waterskin act), target-domain semantics (the emotion-management operation), and the *invariance hypothesis* under which the source-domain inferential structure carries over. Spell that out: *kaẓm* is the source-domain act; its target-domain projection is suppression-of-expression; the *invariance hypothesis* licenses inferring (from source-domain knowledge of waterskins) that suppression is *punctual*, *agent-effortful*, and *non-eliminative-of-contents* — i.e., the rage is still there afterwards. That is a more interesting claim than the under-articulated "regulation" gloss.

3. **Kennedy–Sassoon scalar semantics is mis-applied to a nominal multi-lexeme spectrum** — §2.2 throughout. Kennedy (1999, 2007) and Sassoon (2010) are theories of *gradable adjectives* and the degree functions associated with them; the apparatus presupposes that a *single* predicate (e.g., *tall*) is mapped to a scale with a dimension, ordering, and unit of measure. The manuscript instead treats fourteen *nominal* roots as fourteen "grades on a single semantic continuum" — but a graded continuum of *distinct lexemes* is not what Kennedy–Sassoon scalar theory ranges over. The relevant cognitive-semantic apparatus for ordering members of a *nominal lexical field* is closer to (i) Kövecses's intensity scales on emotion taxonomies (Kövecses 1986; *Emotion Concepts*, NY: Springer, 1990 — surprisingly uncited), (ii) Wierzbicka's NSM emotion-concept ordering (1999 — cited in the bibliography but not used in the analysis), or (iii) Geeraerts's *Theories of Lexical Semantics* on prototype-structured fields (also uncited). The manuscript's claim that "the dimension is *intensity of action* (decomposable into locus, experiential intensity, scope of consequence); the ordering follows the six-stage progression; the implicit unit is the *degree of externalisation*" defines a composite, multi-dimensional ordering that Kennedy–Sassoon's *single*-dimensional scalar theory cannot directly license. Two repair options: (a) Drop the Kennedy–Sassoon framing and replace it with a *prototype-structured* ordering (Geeraerts, Kövecses, Wierzbicka) in which the multi-dimensional composite "intensity of action" is the natural object. (b) Keep Kennedy–Sassoon but apply it to a derived *adjectival* or *adverbial* scalar that the nominal field projects (e.g., the *aššadd*-comparatives Ibn Manẓūr deploys in *m-q-t* = *ashadd al-ibghāḍ* — there *is* an Arabic gradable-adjective layer here, and it would license the Kennedy–Sassoon move properly). Option (b) is, in my judgement, the more interesting fix and would substantially strengthen the methodological-synthesis claim of §1.2.

4. **Izutsu is cited reverentially but not *applied* as a method** — §2.1, §2.4. The manuscript claims Izutsu's three-layer apparatus (focus words / fields / key words) is one of three integrated frameworks, but the actual operationalisation is thin. The text declares *ghaḍab* and *baghy* "the focus words" and the lower-intensity cluster "the boundary" without doing the *paradigmatic / syntagmatic / contrastive* relational analysis Izutsu's method requires (see *Ethico-Religious Concepts*, ch. 2 — "focal" status is defended by demonstrating that the candidate term controls the syntagmatic environment of other field members). What the paper supplies is a co-occurrence network, which is a *neighbouring* method but not Izutsu's. Two consequences. First, the "Izutsu identifies field boundaries" claim in §2.4 collapses on inspection: the field boundary is fixed in §3.2 by exegetical-centrality criteria across four lexica, not by Izutsu's relational test. Second, "key words" in Izutsu's third layer — terms that integrate fields into a worldview — are nowhere identified for the anger spectrum (one might propose *ḥilm*, *taqwā*, *ṣabr* as the integrating key words, but these are absent from the analysis). A serious Izutsu application would (a) demonstrate *ghaḍab*'s focal status by paradigmatic-contrast tests against *naqm*/*sakhaṭ*/*maqt*; (b) identify the key-word layer (probably *ḥilm* + *kaẓm* + *ṣabr* on the anger-management side, *istikbār* + *ʿulw* on the Stage-6 side); (c) demonstrate how the key words integrate the anger field into the broader Qur'anic ethico-religious worldview. Until these are done, the Izutsu citation reads as legitimating-decoration rather than methodological commitment.

5. **The bidirectional-causation framing of Stage 6 is theoretically under-developed and would benefit from a cognitive-semantic vocabulary** — §4.8.5, §5.3. The κ = 0.79 tafsir-coding audit (A = 26% / S = 49% / A+S = 25%) is the manuscript's most theoretically interesting empirical contribution, and the framing of *baghy*/*ṭughyān*/*ʿutuww* as bidirectionally caused with the anger spectrum is the right substantive call. But the *mechanism* of bidirectional causation is not specified. The cognitive-semantic apparatus would treat this as a *causal-frame ambiguity* in the sense of Fillmore's frame semantics (1982) or as a *metonymic-causal complex* in the sense of Kövecses & Radden's metonymy theory (1998 *Cognitive Linguistics* 9:1, 37–77). The Stage-6 lexemes — particularly *ṭughyān*, where Q. 96:6–7 *conditions* it on the appraisal of *istighnāʾ* — straddle two source domains (anger-overflow vs. structural-arrogance), and the manuscript's analysis would be sharpened by Fauconnier–Turner *blending* theory, in which the input spaces (anger; supremacism) are integrated into a blended Stage-6 concept whose causal vectors run in both directions. Goldberg's construction-grammar work on causal constructions (1995 *Constructions*; 2006 *Constructions at Work*) would also help: *naqamū min* + cause and *yaṭghā an raʾāhu staġnā* are *causal constructions* whose argument structures encode the cognitive-causal relations the manuscript wants to recover. Engaging these frameworks would lift the §4.8.5 result from "an interesting empirical finding" to "a theoretical contribution to causal frames in emotion semantics".

6. **Wierzbicka, Geeraerts, Goldberg, Sharifian — the cognitive-semantic neighbours — are cited in the bibliography but not engaged in the analysis** — Bibliography lists Wierzbicka 1999, Sharifian 2011, but neither appears in any in-text discussion. Geeraerts and Goldberg do not appear at all. This is a major lacuna for a paper claiming a cognitive-semantic contribution: (a) Wierzbicka's NSM analysis of anger across languages (1999, chs. 2–3) is the most directly comparable cross-linguistic emotion-lexicon study and her decomposition of anger-concepts into semantic primes ("X feels something bad because X thinks: this person did something bad …") would supply an alternative ordering principle competing with Kennedy–Sassoon's. (b) Sharifian's *cultural conceptualisations* framework (2011, 2017) is the natural home for the manuscript's claim that *baghy*/*ṭughyān*/*ʿutuww* encode "moral-anthropological consequences" — these are cultural conceptualisations in Sharifian's sense, and his framework would license claims the manuscript currently makes informally. (c) Geeraerts's prototype-and-radial-category theory is the standard reference for *karh*'s "twofold structure" (*karāha*/*ikrāh*) and for *ḥuzn*'s "transversal valence" — both currently described in ad-hoc terms. (d) Goldberg's constructional approach is the natural framework for the *ghaḍbān asifan* and *ḍāqa dharʿan* idioms the manuscript foregrounds as compound lexicalisations. The paper would be substantially stronger if these four were *used* rather than gestured at.

7. **The cross-cultural philological survey of §5.5 is welcome but unbalanced and over-claims at step (iv)** — §5.5 "focused six-tradition philological survey". The survey is conducted with care and the Job 32:19 *bāqaʿ* concession is exactly the right scholarly move. But three issues recur. (a) The Akkadian *libbāti malû* citation collapses *fill* and *rupture*: CAD L 163-164 catalogues *libbāti malû* as a *filling* idiom, but Akkadian also has *libbi izziz* (the heart stands forth / bursts) and *qabal libbi* idioms whose pragmatics approach rupture; the Lauri et al. 2024 *iScience* paper the manuscript cites does not in fact settle the rupture-vs-overflow question (its mapping is bodily-locational, not metaphor-structural). (b) The Greek *kholos … anēi* gloss in *Iliad* 9.646 selects the *rising* image but neglects Iliadic *thymos*-overflow imagery in 9.464–469 (where the *thymos* leaps / springs forth — closer to vessel-failure than the manuscript admits). Muellner 1996 and Cairns 2003 are the right references but the manuscript's *Iliad*-internal evidence is selective. (c) The Sanskrit treatment is the weakest. The *Manyu-Sūkta* (ṚV 10.83-84) is the right text, but the dominant Vedic image is *not* "fire and weapon-release" — the hymn explicitly addresses *manyu* as a divine agent with bodily features (jaws, claws, fangs in 10.83.3), an ANGER IS A WILD ANIMAL image of which the manuscript's WEAPON gloss is a downstream Buddhist-Sanskrit overlay. Hara's "Hindu Concepts of Anger" (1992) and Bodewitz's work on the Vedic emotions would correct this. The cumulative effect: the survey *understates* the cross-cultural availability of step-(iv) container-failure imagery, which weakens the "early and unusually transparent" claim. I'd recommend retaining the survey but flagging it as *non-exhaustive* and dropping any claim that the Qur'an's exemplar is "unusually" transparent until a properly stratified cross-linguistic comparison is done.

8. **Novelty claim — primarily a Qur'anic-studies contribution dressed in CMT clothing, not a contribution to cognitive semantics proper** — §1.2 (originality disaggregated into i–v). The manuscript's contribution-(i) "methodological synthesis" (Izutsu + Kennedy–Sassoon + CMT) is novel for *Qur'anic studies* but is not novel for *cognitive semantics*: Sharifian's cultural-conceptualisations programme and Kövecses's emotion-metaphor work routinely synthesise field-semantic, scalar, and metaphor-theoretic apparatus. The contribution-(v) "Q. 67:8 as transparent container-collapse exemplar" *would* be a contribution to cognitive semantics if the cross-cultural survey were tightened (see item 7). The contributions that *are* genuinely original — the *kaẓm*-as-tying-off-of-waterskin analysis (item 2 above, properly developed); the *ḍāqa dharʿan* inhibited-aggression construction (§4.4.1.1); the bidirectional-causation reading of Stage 6 with κ-validated tafsir audit (§4.8.5) — should be foregrounded as the *cognitive-semantic* contribution, and the trifold-integration claim downgraded to a Qur'anic-studies methodological one. Right now §1.2 oversells the cognitive-semantic novelty and undersells what is in fact original.

## Minor issues

- §2.3 "*Ghayẓ* is glossed by all four authoritative lexica as 'compressed contained anger' — fluid in sealed vessel." The bridge from "compressed contained anger" to "fluid in sealed vessel" is the manuscript's CMT interpretation, not a lexicographic gloss. Reword to make the interpretive step visible.

- §2.3 "*Tamayyuz* (Q. 67:8) depicts the failure mode: the container itself bursts apart". The manuscript repeatedly uses *bursts apart* / *bursts asunder* as the English gloss; in §4.7.2 the translation analysis correctly distinguishes Arberry's *bursts asunder* from the four others' *bursts with* + emotion. The §2.3 wording should be tightened to *parts / severs / splits open* to reflect the *m-y-z* root semantics — *burst* in English overloads the contents-exit and structural-collapse readings, which is precisely the conflation the paper means to disambiguate.

- §4.4.2 "anticipating Kövecses's EMOTION IS A LANDSCAPE schema" — Kövecses's actual schema is HAPPINESS / SADNESS / EMOTIONS-IN-GENERAL IS A LOCATION / A JOURNEY / A LANDSCAPE in *Metaphor and Emotion* (2000, ch. 3); the *ḥuzn*-as-rugged-terrain image is closer to EMOTION IS A *TERRAIN* — landscape and terrain are not identical and the claim of *anticipation* needs more careful wording, since landscape metaphors for emotion are attested in pre-Qur'anic Arabic poetry (the *aṭlāl*-mourning convention).

- §4.6.1 "Morphologically nominal (16 n / 6 v / 2 ptcp = 67% nominal vs *ḥuzn*'s 88% verbal): *ghaḍab* is a *structural concept-state* with organisational ontological standing rather than a transient affect." This is a genuinely interesting cognitive-semantic observation (Talmy's *event / state* schemas, Langacker's *summary vs sequential* scanning) and would profit from the relevant theoretical framing. As written, the rhetoric ("organisational ontological standing") outruns the analysis.

- §5.2 "the Qur'anic *ṭughyān* is, on this reading, a textbook instance of Roseman's appraisal pattern lexicalised under a single root in the seventh century that twentieth-century cognitive appraisal theory has independently re-derived". The "independently re-derived" framing is anachronistic and reads as apologetic; the cognitive-semantic point is the more modest claim that *ṭughyān* lexicalises a recognisable appraisal pattern (high coping potential + self-agency + motive-consistent), which is interesting in itself.

- §4.7.1 "Read through Lakoff and Kövecses, the *ghayẓ–kaẓm* pair instantiates ANGER IS A PRESSURIZED CONTAINER with precision: *ghayẓ* the contained fluid, *kaẓm* the seal". The mapping *ghayẓ* ↦ contained fluid and *kaẓm* ↦ seal is mostly right but the cognitive-semantic vocabulary would distinguish *substance* (fluid) from *act* (sealing); *kaẓm* is an *act-nominal* of the seal-applying agent, not the seal itself. Tightening the mapping in this direction would also support the item-2 revision.

- §3.5 cites al-Qushayrī's *Laṭāʾif al-Ishārāt* "for Sufi-tradition cross-checking on *kaẓm al-ghayẓ*", but no actual Qushayrī content surfaces in the analysis. If the Sufi reading of *kaẓm* (where it is *not* simple suppression but the spiritual-disciplinary cultivation of *ḥilm* — see Sulamī's *Risālat al-Malāmatiyya* — is genuinely relevant, develop it; if not, drop the citation.

- §1.3 "Classical Arabic is not separately treated" (re: Kövecses, Benczes, Szelid 2025). Confirm this against the table of contents at de Gruyter; the MSA chapter the manuscript references may in fact treat some Classical material in a historical-preface section, and the strong claim "omits Classical Arabic entirely" should be tightened to "does not contain a dedicated Classical Arabic / Qur'anic chapter" if that is what the editors confirm.

- §4.4.3 The compound *ghaḍbān asifan* analysis is one of the paper's strongest cognitive-semantic moments and deserves to be expanded — specifically, the claim that *asaf* "grounds" *ghaḍab* is doing real theoretical work (closer to Fauconnier–Turner *blending* than to scalar ordering) and would benefit from explicit framing.

- §4.13 "*baghy*'s outsized centrality in the raw-count network is partly a frequency artefact". The PMI correction is the right move; consider also reporting *positive* PMI (npmi > 0) versus *significant* PMI (against a hypergeometric null), since the npmi values reported (0.59, 0.47, 0.22, 0.23) are all small in absolute terms and the significance question is not addressed.

- §4.12 The CAMeLBERT-CA null is treated as supportive ("a signal that exists in manual labelling but not in pre-trained embeddings is informative"); this is a defensible reading but the reverse reading (that the spectrum is not robustly recoverable from distributional statistics, raising the bar for the scalar-semantics claim of §2.2) should at least be acknowledged in §5.

- Bibliography: Kövecses (1986) *Metaphors of Anger, Pride, and Love* is cited in the bibliography but not in the main text — this is the original monograph that proposed the intensity-scaled lexical-field analysis the manuscript's §2.2 is doing, and it should be engaged directly rather than relegated to Kennedy–Sassoon.

- Bibliography: Kövecses 1990 *Emotion Concepts* (Springer) is missing and is the standard reference for prototype-and-radial-category analysis of emotion lexicons across languages — directly relevant to items 3 and 6 above.

## Editorial polish

- The phrase "to our knowledge" recurs ~8 times across §1.2, §3.4, §5.4, §6.1. Each is defensible but the cumulative effect reads defensively. Reduce to 3 occurrences and absorb the rest into named-comparison language ("Qāʾimīniyā, Pakatchi, and Sharifian have integrated X and Y; the present paper adds Z").

- §1.2 closes with a sentence beginning "To these add (iv)…" running to ~110 words. Break into two sentences.

- §4.4.2 caveat paragraph runs to ~140 words in a single sentence. Break.

- §5.5 "Net assessment" paragraph: the final sentence ("The Qur'anic data thus *strengthen* — without by themselves *establishing* — the cross-cultural universalist hypothesis of CMT, and they extend the empirical base to seventh-century Arabic in a register that the 2025 *Metaphors of ANGER* encyclopedia, conspicuously omitting Classical Arabic, leaves un-canvassed") conflates two claims (strengthening the universalist hypothesis; filling an empirical gap). Split.

- Hyphenation inconsistency: "anger-spectrum" / "anger spectrum" / "anger-derived" appear in mixed forms. Choose one style and propagate.

- Use of "in-text" hyphenation in academic prose: "tafsir-grounded", "FDR-surviving", "marginal-preserving" are over-used; in formal prose these compound-modifiers should be hyphenated only in pre-nominal position, not in predicative position ("the result is FDR-surviving" → "the result survives FDR correction").

- §6.1 includes a near-verbatim repetition of §1.2's originality list. Compress.

- The Arabic transliteration mixes *ḍyq*/*ḍīq* (root vs. citation form), *ġḍb*/*ghaḍab*, *ġyẓ*/*ghayẓ* inconsistently across §§3–5. Adopt a single convention (the standard in *JQS* is the citation-form gloss in italics for in-text discussion, root-letter form for tables and code).

- Table 2 "Notes" column: "raw *p* = 0.005, Holm-adj. 0.05" is reported for both *karh* (S1) and *sakhaṭ* (S3) but Table 2's row for *karh* says "Medinan-tilted (*p* = 0.005)" without the Holm adjustment, while *sakhaṭ* shows both. Make consistent.

- §4.8.5: "Coding by the corresponding author with second-coder verification on a 20% subsample (κ = 0.79; full coding at `data/concordance/stage6_causation_coding.csv`)". Best practice for inter-rater is to report the *full* coded set rather than 20%; consider expanding the second-coder pass to ≥50% if feasible, and report which subsample was double-coded (random / stratified by root).

- Footnote on Cochran (1954) in §4.2 reads "Bibliography entry to be added on author follow-up; cited from secondary literature." The entry *is* in the bibliography (correct). Remove the placeholder.

- Several bibliography entries marked "Author attribution unresolved" (ELQV authors, Cogent Authors, IJCP Authors) — clean these up before submission; reviewers and copy-editors will not accept placeholder attributions.

## Verdict

This is a serious, materially-improved manuscript whose empirical apparatus is unusually thorough for the field and whose evidential calibration (FDR adjustment, permutation nulls, bootstrap CIs, κ-validated audit, NLP corroborations and nulls) is exemplary. The Qur'anic-studies contribution is solid and the bidirectional-causation reading of Stage 6 is theoretically interesting. As a *cognitive-semantic* contribution, however, the manuscript is currently underdeveloped: the Lakoff–Kövecses citation is over-precise to the point of mis-attribution; the Kennedy–Sassoon framing is mis-applied to a nominal field where prototype / radial-category theory or NSM would be the more natural fit; Izutsu is invoked rather than applied; Wierzbicka, Geeraerts, Goldberg, and Sharifian appear in the bibliography but do nothing in the analysis; and the cross-cultural §5.5 survey, while welcome, understates the availability of step-(iv) imagery in Akkadian, Greek, and Vedic. The bidirectional-causation finding (§4.8.5) is the manuscript's most genuinely novel theoretical claim and deserves cognitive-semantic framing (Fauconnier–Turner blending, Kövecses–Radden metonymy, Goldberg constructions). Recommendation is **major revision** — addressable in a focused 4–6-week pass that (a) repairs the L&K citation, (b) replaces or re-applies Kennedy–Sassoon, (c) operationalises Izutsu, (d) engages Wierzbicka / Sharifian / Geeraerts / Goldberg, (e) tightens §5.5, and (f) reframes the novelty claim. Once these are addressed the paper becomes a genuine cognitive-semantic contribution and a clear *JQS* accept.
