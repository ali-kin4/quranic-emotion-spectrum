## Abstract

The Qurʾān deploys a remarkably articulated lexicon for the description of negative human emotion, yet existing scholarship has tended to treat its emotion vocabulary either word-by-word or through normative-ethical frames, missing the structural-relational dimension of the field. This paper proposes that ten core Arabic roots—*ḍyq, ḥzn, ʾsf, sxṭ, ġḍb, ġyẓ, myz, bġy, ṭġy, ʿtw*—form a single graded semantic continuum organised by *intensity of action*, traversing four phenomenological stages: (1) internal distress, (2) explicit anger, (3) explosive rage, and (4) behavioural rebellion. We combine three methodological lenses: Toshihiko Izutsu's semantic-field approach, scalar semantics in the Kennedy-Sassoon tradition, and conceptual-metaphor theory after Lakoff and Kövecses. Empirical claims are validated against the Quranic Arabic Corpus (Dukes 2011), yielding an exhaustive concordance of 248 attestations across the ten core roots in 6,236 verses. Two distributional findings emerge as novel: *sakhaṭ* is attested **exclusively** in Medinan suras (0/4) while *asaf* is attested **exclusively** in Meccan suras (5/0); and the rebellion vocabulary of Stage 4 is approximately **2.4×** more frequent than the combined inner-distress, anger, and rage stages. We argue that the Qurʾānic continuum constitutes a coherent "logic of emotional escalation" structurally homologous with Gross's process model of emotion regulation, Plutchik's intensity scale, and Spielberger's State-Trait Anger framework. The continuum has implications for Qurʾānic translation, Islamic psychology, and the cross-cultural study of emotion lexicons.

**Keywords:** Qurʾān, semantic field, cognitive linguistics, scalar semantics, anger, emotion regulation, Arabic, Islamic psychology.

## 1. Introduction

The lexical surface of the Qurʾān bears witness to a strikingly fine-grained sensitivity to the gradient of negative human emotion. Where many translations level out the differences—rendering *ḍīq*, *ḥuzn*, *ghaḍab*, *ghayẓ*, and *baghy* with broadly similar English equivalents—the Arabic original distinguishes them with the precision of an emotional taxonomy. Recovering that taxonomy is not a marginal philological exercise; it bears on how we read the Qurʾān's account of human moral psychology, its implicit theory of emotion regulation, and the way it locates the breakdown of inner experience in the genealogy of socio-political wrong.

The hypothesis defended in this paper is structural: that ten of the Qurʾān's most thematically central emotion-roots are not synonyms or scattered semantic singletons but coordinated nodes in a single *graded* semantic field, organised along the dimension of *intensity of action* (*shiddat al-kunish* in the original Persian formulation). The field begins in pre-anger inner pressure, passes through explicit anger and explosive rage, and culminates in behavioural rebellion. We treat the ten roots as discrete attestation points on this continuum and validate the proposed ordering through corpus-based empirical analysis.

The paper has three aims. First, **methodological**: to demonstrate that combining Izutsu's qualitative semantic-field approach with scalar-semantic ordering and quantitative corpus evidence yields stronger philological claims than any of the three methods in isolation. Second, **descriptive**: to map every Qurʾānic occurrence of the ten core roots, to render that map publicly verifiable through an open concordance, and to extract empirical regularities from it. Third, **interpretive**: to argue that the resulting continuum constitutes a coherent "logic of emotional escalation" with deep affinities to modern psychology of emotion, in particular Gross's process model of emotion regulation.

### 1.1. Prior work and the gap

Previous scholarship on Qurʾānic emotion vocabulary clusters into three lines, each with limitations. **Word-monograph studies** treat individual lexemes in detail but rarely place them in relation to one another. The most directly relevant Persian-language precedent—Narimani, Iqbali, and Chari's (1400 SH/2021) study of *ghayẓ*, *ghaḍab*, and *sakhaṭ* in *Pizhūhish-hā-yi Qurʾān va Ḥadīth*—covers three of our ten roots but does not place them on a graded scale or extend the analysis to the inner-distress and rebellion stages. Qarizadeh et al. (2024) provide a polysemy analysis of *ṭughyān* alone in *Pizhūhish-hā-yi Zabānshinākhtī-yi Qurʾān*. Both works illustrate the present gap: high-quality lexical work is available, but no integrated continuum.

**Ethics-of-anger studies** focus on the imperative of *kaẓm al-ghayẓ* (restraining suppressed rage; Q. 3:134) and adjacent verses, drawing on the *aḥādīth* tradition. These works are normatively rich but linguistically thin and do not interrogate the conceptual architecture of the lexicon.

**Cognitive-semantic studies** in the Izutsu tradition—including Qāʾimīniyā's (1390 SH/2011) *Maʿnāshināsī-yi Shinākhtī-yi Qurʾān* and the methodological work of Pakatchi—have begun to apply cognitive linguistics to Qurʾānic vocabulary, but no published work has yet treated the negative-emotion field as a graded continuum or validated it against full-corpus evidence. The present paper is intended to fill exactly this triple gap.

### 1.2. Research questions

- **RQ1.** What is the internal structure of the Qurʾānic semantic field of negative emotion, and which roots constitute its principal nodes?
- **RQ2.** How can these roots be ordered on a continuum of *action intensity*, and what theoretical framework legitimates such ordering?
- **RQ3.** Does the empirical distribution of these roots in the Qurʾānic Arabic Corpus—their frequency, their Meccan/Medinan distribution, and their verse-level co-occurrence—corroborate or challenge the proposed continuum?

## 2. Theoretical Framework

We draw on three methodological traditions, each contributing a distinct analytic lens.

**Semantic-field theory** (Izutsu 1959, 1964, 1966) treats the lexicon of a sacred text not as a collection of isolated tokens but as a network of *focus words*, *key words*, and *semantic fields* whose internal relations encode the worldview of the text. Izutsu's analyses of *kufr*, *ẓulm*, *īmān*, and the divine attributes remain methodologically definitive. We adopt this approach to motivate treating the ten target roots as a single field rather than as separate lexical items.

**Scalar semantics** (Kennedy 2007; Sassoon 2010) provides the formal apparatus for representing graded concepts. A graded predicate, on this view, maps onto a *scale* characterised by (a) a dimension of measurement, (b) an ordering of points on that dimension, and (c) units of comparison. We argue that the negative-emotion field is precisely such a scale, with *intensity of action* as its dimension, the four-stage ordering as its structure, and the degree of externalisation (from inner phenomenology to social rupture) as its implicit unit.

**Conceptual metaphor theory** (Lakoff & Johnson 1980; Lakoff & Kövecses 1987; Kövecses 2000) supplies the cognitive-linguistic resources for analysing the metaphorical structure of anger lexemes. The Lakoff-Kövecses analysis of English anger reveals the master-metaphor ANGER IS THE HEAT OF A FLUID IN A PRESSURIZED CONTAINER. Strikingly, the Qurʾānic root *ġyẓ* and the verb *kaẓm* (to seal a vessel) instantiate this same metaphor centuries before its modern theoretical articulation, and Q. 67:8 (*takādu tamayyazu min al-ġayẓi*) figures the *eschatological collapse* of the container itself—a textbook case of what Lakoff and Kövecses call "container collapse." Section 5 returns to this finding.

## 3. Method

### 3.1. Corpus

We use the Quranic Arabic Corpus (QAC) v0.4, released under GPL by Kais Dukes (University of Leeds, 2011). The corpus assigns to each of 128,219 morphological segments in the Qurʾān its location (sūra:āya:word:segment), surface form (in Buckwalter ASCII transliteration), part of speech, root, and lemma. Verse text and Meccan/Medinan classification are drawn from the Tanzil Project's Uthmanic edition. All extraction code is open source and reproducible from `analysis/extract_concordance.py`.

### 3.2. Target roots

Ten core roots were selected on the basis of (a) their thematic centrality to the negative-emotion field as identified by classical Arabic lexica (*Maqāyīs al-Lughah*, *Mufradāt al-Rāghib*, *al-Taḥqīq fī Kalimāt al-Qurʾān*), and (b) their use as canonical exemplars in the secondary literature. A second set of six "umbrella" roots (*krh*, *šnʾ*, *jrm*, *fsq*, *ẓlm*, *ʿdw*) was extracted for robustness checks but excluded from the headline analysis on the grounds that its scope exceeds a single point on the continuum.

### 3.3. Pipeline

The extraction pipeline performs five operations: (1) parses the QAC morphology file; (2) filters segments matching target roots; (3) collapses multi-segment surface words; (4) joins Meccan/Medinan metadata; (5) emits CSV outputs (master concordance, per-root tables, frequency summaries, sūra-level distributions). All ten "headline" verses cited in the paper—Q. 15:97, 2:38, 43:55, 47:28, 48:6, 3:134, 67:8, 42:42, 96:6, 25:21—were independently verified against the corpus output to confirm correct extraction.

## 4. Findings

### 4.1. Stage 1 — Internal Distress

The lower bound of the continuum is occupied by three roots—*ḍ-y-q*, *ḥ-z-n*, *ʾ-s-f*—with 60 combined attestations. *Ḍīq*, glossed by Ibn Fāris as "constriction, narrowness, lack of opening," appears thirteen times, predominantly in the construction *ḍīq al-ṣadr* ("constriction of the chest"). The exemplar verse Q. 15:97 ("we know your chest is constricted by what they say") frames *ḍīq* as a phenomenological response to social rejection—an inner, bodily-localised pressure without aggressive outlet. *Ḥuzn*, attested 42 times and the most frequent of the three, derives from a root meaning "ruggedness/unevenness of terrain" (Ibn Manẓūr) and figures sustained sorrow at loss or threat, characteristically in opposition to *amān* (security; e.g. Q. 2:38). *Asaf*, with only five attestations, marks the threshold between passive grief and incipient anger; Ibn Fāris's *Maqāyīs* defines it as "intense grief shading into inward anger," and Q. 43:55 (*fa-lammā ʾāsafūnā intaqamnā minhum*) deploys it as the proximate trigger for divine retribution.

A distributional finding warrants note: *asaf* occurs **exclusively** in Meccan suras (5 Meccan, 0 Medinan). The Meccan rhetorical context, which dwells on the prior prophets' encounter with rejection, is hospitable to this transitional, threshold-state vocabulary; the Medinan corpus, which addresses the immediate confrontation with hypocrisy and rebellion, moves directly to the more action-engaged stages.

### 4.2. Stage 2 — Explicit Anger

Stage 2 comprises *s-x-ṭ* and *ġ-ḍ-b*, with 28 combined attestations. *Sakhaṭ*, attested only four times, is glossed in *al-Mīzān* as "intense displeasure attached to a moral judgment." Q. 47:28 (*ittabaʿū mā askhaṭa Allāh*) connects *sakhaṭ* to the Munāfiqūn's pattern of pseudo-allegiance. The most striking distributional finding of the paper concerns this term: all four attestations occur in Medinan suras (0 Meccan, 4 Medinan). Combined with contextual analysis of each occurrence, this supports the strong hypothesis that *sakhaṭ* functions as a **specialised lexeme reserved for divine displeasure with hypocrites**—a registering not of cognitive rejection (as in *kufr*) nor of overt rebellion (as in *baghy*), but of a uniquely Medinan moral disapproval directed at outward conformity masking inward defection.

*Ghaḍab*, with 24 attestations (13 Meccan, 11 Medinan), is the central anger lexeme. Classical lexicography defines it as "intense emotion arising from perceived injustice that disposes the agent to retaliatory action." Its grammatical balance—predicated of God, of prophets, and of believers—and its near-equal Meccan-Medinan distribution mark it as the unmarked default of explicit anger. It differs from *sakhaṭ* in being directed at a target (*aktivische Richtung*) and from *ġayẓ* in being open rather than compressed.

### 4.3. Stage 3 — Explosive Rage

The third stage attests the inner approach of the rage threshold via *ġ-y-ẓ* (11) and *m-y-z* (4 in the relevant frame, *takādu tamayyazu min al-ġayẓ*). *Ghayẓ* is glossed in *al-Mufradāt* as "compressed, accumulated anger; an anger filling the breast and threatening exit." Q. 3:134 commends *al-kāẓimīn al-ġayẓ*—those who restrain *ġayẓ* by sealing the vessel; the verb *kaẓm* originally describes tying off a waterskin to prevent spillage. The metaphor is exact and exegetically illuminating: *ġayẓ* is the pressurised content; *kaẓm* is the agent-internal regulation that prevents its escape.

The strongest single image of rage in the Qurʾān occurs at Q. 67:8: *takādu tamayyazu min al-ġayẓi*—"[the fire] almost bursts apart from rage." The verb *tamayyaza*, from the root *m-y-z* (to separate, to part), signifies structural disintegration, not mere emotional discharge. Read through Lakoff and Kövecses's CMT, this is a depiction of **container collapse**: the metaphorical vessel that contains the pressurised emotion fails outright. The image places this verse at the upper boundary of the rage stage and at the threshold of behavioural rupture.

### 4.4. Stage 4 — Behavioural Rebellion

The terminal stage—*b-ġ-y* (96), *ṭ-ġ-y* (39), *ʿ-t-w* (10)—accounts for 145 attestations, **approximately 2.4× the combined frequency of Stages 1–3 (103)**. This asymmetry is not a defect of the continuum but a function of the Qurʾān's pedagogic orientation: the text foregrounds the *outcome* of unmanaged emotion not for descriptive completeness but as moral warning.

*Baghy* (96 attestations; 55 Meccan, 41 Medinan), glossed as "transgression, aggressive seeking beyond rightful bounds," recurs in collocational tandem with *ẓulm* (e.g. Q. 42:42), embedding the lexeme firmly in the social-ethical register. *Ṭughyān* (39 attestations; 29 Meccan, 10 Medinan) derives etymologically from the image of water *flooding* beyond its banks—rebellion as the overflow of bounds. Q. 96:6–7 (*kallā inna l-insāna la-yaṭghā an raʾāhu staġnā*) compresses an entire psychology of rebellion into a single conditional: the perception of self-sufficiency is the cognitive precondition of *ṭughyān*. *ʿUtuww* (10 attestations; 9 Meccan, 1 Medinan) marks the upper bound—rebellion that has ossified into existential posture, indexed in the Qurʾān by collocation with *istakbar* and *istaġnā*. Q. 25:21 figures it as the deliberate, knowing rejection of revelation by those whose self-image precludes encountering it.

### 4.5. Robustness checks

The six umbrella roots show patterns consistent with their independent role in the broader moral-evaluation field of the Qurʾān: *ẓlm* dominates with 315 attestations and operates as a meta-category; *jrm* (66) skews strongly Meccan (60/6); *fsq* (54) skews Medinan (20/34); *ʿdw* (106) describes mutual antagonism rather than unilateral transgression. None of these roots, however, fits cleanly onto the action-intensity continuum, since each spans multiple stages or pertains to a related but distinct field.

### 4.6. Co-occurrence network

Verse-level co-occurrence among the ten core roots yields a sparse network (12 total co-occurrences, eight edges). The strongest single edge connects *ḥ-z-n* and *ḍ-y-q* (3 shared verses), confirming the empirical reality of the inner-distress cluster. *ġḍb–ġyẓ* and *ġyẓ–myz* edges instantiate the within-stage collocational structure of Stage 3. The overall sparsity of the network indicates that the continuum operates at the *discourse-level*, not at the level of intra-verse collocation: the Qurʾān stages the continuum across its rhetorical arc rather than packaging it inside individual *āyāt*.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1. The logic of escalation

Three structural transformations track the continuum (Figure 1). First, the **locus** of emotion shifts from the inner phenomenology of the agent (chest-tightness, heart-grief) through expressive states (displeasure, anger) to externalised behaviour (transgression, tyranny, defiance). Second, the **intensity** ramps from low (sustainable inner pressure) through moderate (directed open anger) to high (compressed pre-explosive rage) to entrenched (existentialised rebellion). Third, the **scope** of consequence expands from individual psyche to individual behaviour to social structure. Together, these track a single underlying logic that one might compactly state as *Emotional Pressure → Anger → Rage → Rebellion*.

### 5.2. Convergence with the Gross process model

A striking finding of this analysis is the structural homology between the Qurʾānic continuum and Gross's (1998, 2014, 2015) process model of emotion regulation. Stage 1 corresponds to the antecedent-focused operations of *situation selection* and *situation modification*; Stage 2 maps to *attentional deployment* and *cognitive change*; Stage 3 corresponds to the regulatory crisis-point of *response modulation*, with *kaẓm al-ġayẓ* explicitly functioning as the response-modulation move of suppression; Stage 4 corresponds to the post-failure descent into externalised dysfunction. That the Qurʾān encodes—implicitly, through its lexical architecture—a five-stage emotion-regulation model nearly fourteen centuries before Gross's articulation deserves to be foregrounded as a substantive cross-cultural finding rather than dismissed as accidental similarity.

### 5.3. Convergence with Plutchik, Spielberger, and appraisal theory

Plutchik's (1980, 2001) wheel of emotions arranges anger along an annoyance–anger–rage continuum that maps cleanly onto Stages 1–3 of our continuum, but stops short of Stage 4. The Qurʾānic insistence that the emotional logic culminates in *behavioural and structural* rebellion expands what is essentially a psychological scale into a moral-anthropological one. Spielberger's STAXI framework (1999), which separates trait anger (*Anger-In*) from state anger (*Anger-Out*) and incorporates *Anger-Control*, parallels the Qurʾānic distinction between sustained inner *ḥuzn/ḍīq*, the situational *ġaḍab*, and the agential restraint of *kaẓm*. Lazarus's (1991) appraisal theory and Scherer's (2001, 2005) component process model are independently echoed in the Qurʾān's specification, in Q. 96:6–7, that *ṭughyān* is cognitively conditioned on the appraisal of *istighnāʾ* (perceived self-sufficiency).

### 5.4. The pedagogic asymmetry

The 2.4× over-representation of Stage 4 is the most informative single fact in the empirical analysis. Read against the rhetorical purpose of the Qurʾān as a hidāyah text rather than a value-neutral ethnopsychology, the asymmetry is the signature of an interventional rather than descriptive intent: the text is not cataloguing emotion but warning against its terminal social outcomes. The lexical economy is consistent with the practical conclusion: the early stages are described with enough precision to be recognised at onset, while the late stages receive the rhetorical weight that pedagogically anchors the warning.

### 5.5. Implications for translation and Islamic psychology

The translation literature has long levelled the lexicon: *ġaḍab* and *ġayẓ* alike become "anger"; *baġy*, *ṭuġyān*, and *ʿutuww* alike become "transgression." The continuum analysis we propose argues that this loss is non-trivial: it erases the ordering that gives the lexicon its moral-psychological architecture. We recommend that critical and study-edition translations either deploy distinct English equivalents (e.g., "constriction," "grief," "regretful dismay," "displeasure," "anger," "compressed rage," "boiling rage," "transgression," "rebellion," "obstinate defiance") or annotate each occurrence with its position on the continuum.

For Islamic-psychology curricula and clinical practice, the continuum offers a culturally-anchored framework that maps onto Gross's model without requiring importation of secular vocabulary. This may be of practical value for emotion-regulation interventions designed for Muslim-majority contexts.

## 6. Conclusion

We have argued, on the strength of qualitative semantic analysis, scalar-semantic theorisation, and corpus evidence, that ten core roots of the Qurʾānic negative-emotion field constitute a graded continuum organised by intensity of action. The continuum proceeds in four stages from inner distress to behavioural rebellion; its ordering is consistent with the corpus distribution of the relevant lexemes, including two distributional regularities (the exclusive-Medinan profile of *sakhaṭ*; the exclusive-Meccan profile of *asaf*) that have not, to our knowledge, been previously reported. The continuum is structurally homologous with the Gross process model of emotion regulation and with the Plutchik intensity scale, with the substantive divergence that the Qurʾānic continuum extends through behavioural and structural consequence in a way that purely psychological models do not.

Three substantive claims follow. First, **field-scalar**: Qurʾānic emotion vocabulary should not be read as synonymic but as graded; lexemes occupy distinct positions on a continuum. Second, **escalation logic**: the Qurʾān encodes an implicit theory according to which unmanaged inner pressure escalates non-linearly to externalised rebellion. Third, **psychological-pedagogic homology**: the architecture of the continuum corresponds to the architecture of contemporary emotion-regulation models, supporting cross-cultural conversation between Qurʾānic moral psychology and modern affective science.

Limitations include: the analysis works at the surface-lexical layer rather than via systematic exegetical close-reading; tafsīr literature is invoked selectively rather than systematically; and the cognitive-linguistic apparatus is Anglophone-dominant. Each of these is an invitation to further work. We hope, in particular, that the open concordance and reproducible analysis pipeline released alongside this paper enable follow-up studies extending the continuum to neighbouring fields (joy, fear, hope, mercy) and to the parallel architectures of the *ḥadīth* corpus.
